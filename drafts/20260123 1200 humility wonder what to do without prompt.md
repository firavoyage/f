That’s a thoughtful question, Fira.
Words in English often live in quiet families, connected in different ways. Here are some of the main kinds of relationships they can have, explained gently and simply.

**1. Words with similar meanings (synonyms)**
These words point to almost the same idea, though each has its own shade.

- _happy_ — _joyful_ — _content_
  They feel alike, but not identical.

**2. Words with opposite meanings (antonyms)**
These stand across from each other.

- _hot_ ↔ _cold_
- _light_ ↔ _dark_

**3. Words that share a root or origin**
They grow from the same base and feel related in meaning.

- _act_ → _action_, _active_, _react_
  You can often sense the shared core idea.

**4. General and specific words**
One word can be broad, another more precise.

- _animal_ → _dog_ → _poodle_
  Like branches narrowing on a tree.

**5. Part–whole relationships**
One word names a part of something larger.

- _wheel_ ↔ _car_
- _chapter_ ↔ _book_

**6. Words that often appear together (collocations)**
They keep each other company in natural speech.

- _make a decision_
- _strong coffee_
  They sound “right” together.

**7. Sound-based relationships**
Some words relate by how they sound.

- _cat_ — _hat_ (rhyme)
- _buzz_, _whisper_ (sound imitation)

**8. Meaning depends on context (polysemy & homonyms)**
One word, many meanings.

- _bank_ (river bank / money bank)
  The relationship is hidden until context reveals it.

All these relationships help language feel alive rather than random.
They show that words don’t stand alone — they lean on each other quietly.

## The highest risks in creating an AI company’s autonomous agent

### 1. **Misaligned goals**

This is the quietest and most dangerous risk.

If the agent’s objective is even slightly wrong, it will optimize confidently in the wrong direction.

- vague success metrics
- proxy goals that miss human intent
- incentives that reward speed over care

An agent does exactly what you ask — not what you _mean_.

---

### 2. **Unbounded autonomy**

Autonomy without limits becomes recklessness.

Risks appear when:

- the agent can act without review
- permissions are too broad
- rollback is hard or impossible

Autonomy must grow slowly, with clear fences.

---

### 3. **Poor understanding of context**

Agents don’t “understand” in the human sense.

They struggle with:

- nuance
- cultural or ethical context
- edge cases that feel obvious to people

What looks reasonable in isolation can be harmful in reality.

---

### 4. **Feedback loops**

Autonomous agents can reinforce their own mistakes.

For example:

- acting → observing consequences → doubling down
- learning from biased or incomplete data

Without interruption, small errors become strong habits.

---

### 5. **Security and misuse**

Anything autonomous is attractive to attackers.

Risks include:

- prompt injection
- data leakage
- unintended tool usage

An agent is both a worker _and_ an attack surface.

---

### 6. **Opacity**

When people can’t see _why_ an agent acted, trust erodes.

- unclear reasoning
- hard-to-debug behavior
- surprising decisions

Opacity turns bugs into fear.

---

### 7. **Overconfidence by humans**

This one is subtle — and common.

People begin to:

- defer judgment
- stop double-checking
- assume the agent “knows better”

The risk isn’t just the agent failing — it’s humans disengaging.

---

## A grounding principle

The highest risks don’t come from bad code.
They come from **assuming the agent is wiser than it is**.

Good builders stay humble.

---

## How strong teams respond

They:

- start with narrow autonomy
- make actions observable and reversible
- keep humans in the loop longer than feels efficient
- treat deployment as a moral act, not just a technical one
