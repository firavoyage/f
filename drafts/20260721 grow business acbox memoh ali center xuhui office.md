## i mean why didnt other big tech do what they did

Other big tech companies **did exactly what Alibaba did**, but they did it in different districts or different cities based on where their core industries were already rooted.

The reason you see this specific open ecosystem model at the Ali Center in Xuhui comes down to a mix of **timing, specialized corporate strategy, and localized government planning**.

### 1. Other Tech Giants Built Elsewhere (The "Territory" Strategy)

Big tech companies in China strategically split up geographic territories to avoid competing for the exact same local talent pool and government resources.

- **Tencent** heavily anchored itself in the **Caohejing High-Tech Park** and later expanded its footprint in the same West Bund area. Tencent focused its real estate on cloud, gaming, and AI development tailored to its social ecosystem.
- **Huawei** built a massive, campus in **Pudong** and a sprawling, village-style R\&D smart campus in **Qingpu, Shanghai** (the Lianxi Lake Campus). Huawei required massive, isolated campuses for secretive hardware and semiconductor R\&D, rather than public-facing retail ecosystems.
- **Meituan and Bilibili** chose **Yangpu District** along the northern waterfront, creating their own massive headquarters and tech hubs there because Yangpu is surrounded by top universities (like Fudan and Tongji), giving them direct access to fresh tech talent.

### 2. Alibaba’s Business Demands a Public Ecosystem

Alibaba’s core business model forces its real estate to be open and collaborative, whereas companies like Tencent, ByteDance, or NetEase rely on digital-first products (gaming, social media, algorithms) that require strict secrecy.

- **E-Commerce is Physical:** Alibaba’s business relies on millions of external merchants, logistics partners, and brands. Its campus _must_ have physical retail integration, showrooms, and spaces for external partners to meet.
- **Cloud & B2B Focus:** Alibaba Cloud serves thousands of external corporate clients. The Xuhui campus was built specifically as a "showcase" where enterprise clients could move in, integrate with Alibaba Cloud, and build software in a live "smart campus" environment.

### 3. The Xuhui Government Mandate

When the Xuhui district government auctioned off the prime West Bund waterfront land, they did not want a closed corporate fortress.

- **The Requirement:** The government mandated that whoever won the land auction had to create a public, mixed-use space that would revitalize the riverfront and attract outside startups.
- **Alibaba Won the Bid:** Alibaba pitched the best architectural design (partnering with Foster + Partners) that perfectly blended a corporate headquarters with an open, public "urban room." Other tech companies bought plots of land with different zoning laws that allowed them to build traditional, closed high-rises.

## do other big tech build office infras elsewhere for other companies to live in

Yes, other big tech companies absolutely build massive, city-scale infrastructure designed for other businesses, retail providers, and sometimes even thousands of external residents to live and work in.

While Western tech giants (like Google or Apple) usually prefer isolated, secure corporate campuses, the trend of building a **"mini-city" or open ecosystem** is highly prevalent among global tech giants—particularly in Asia, where government partnerships dictate massive urban revitalization.

Several major examples showcase how big tech creates infrastructure for external entities:

### 1. Tencent’s "Penguin Island" / Net City (Shenzhen, China)

Tencent is actively building a jaw-dropping, $5.7 billion, Manhattan-sized smart city development in Shenzhen's Qianhai district nicknamed **[Penguin Island](https://www.facebook.com/ChannelNewsAsia/posts/chinese-tech-giant-tencent-is-building-a-headquarters-that-looks-more-like-a-min/1434602675362781/)** (or Net City). [link](https://www.scmp.com/business/companies/article/3139638/will-tencent-give-sustainability-green-buildings-boost-its-new)

- **The Scale**: It spans 2 million square meters and is designed entirely around an open, public-facing, low-carbon smart network. [link](https://www.instagram.com/reel/DZHYnMAgNIY/)
- **Who lives there**: Unlike a strict corporate park, Tencent is building a massive amount of infrastructure explicitly meant for external ecosystems. It integrates third-party public schools, public parks, a massive tech museum, a sports complex, and extensive commercial retail properties for independent businesses. [link](https://www.scmp.com/business/companies/article/3139638/will-tencent-give-sustainability-green-buildings-boost-its-new)
- **Public Integration**: The entire campus functions as a transit-oriented public neighborhood connected directly to Shenzhen’s subway and ferry grid, inviting the outside world inside. [link](https://www.cnn.com/style/article/tencent-shenzhen-net-city)

### 2. Google’s "Downtown West" Master Plan (San Jose, California)

While Google famously maintains highly secure individual offices, their multi-billion-dollar **[Downtown West Project](https://www.sanjoseca.gov/your-government/departments-offices/planning-building-code-enforcement/planning-division/environmental-planning/environmental-review/completed-eirs/downtown-west-mixed-use-plan)** in San Jose is an explicit pivot toward the open, mixed-use infrastructure model. - **The Scale**: Spanning an 80-acre urban zone, it is designed to thoroughly blur the lines between corporate Google space and the local community. [link](https://www.sanjoseca.gov/your-government/departments-offices/planning-building-code-enforcement/planning-division/environmental-planning/environmental-review/completed-eirs/downtown-west-mixed-use-plan)

[link](https://www.sanjoseca.gov/your-government/departments-offices/planning-building-code-enforcement/planning-division/environmental-planning/environmental-review/completed-eirs/downtown-west-mixed-use-plan)
- **The Goal**: Much like Alibaba, Google realized it couldn't simply drop a "fortress" into a major city. To get government approval, they acted as the master real estate developer—creating an open infrastructure where local restaurants, non-tech startups, artists, and thousands of non-Google citizens live permanently. [link](https://www.sanjoseca.gov/your-government/departments-offices/planning-building-code-enforcement/planning-division/environmental-planning/environmental-review/completed-eirs/downtown-west-mixed-use-plan)

### 3. Toyota’s "Woven City" (Susono, Japan)

Though automotive-first, Toyota operates highly as a mobility tech giant and is currently constructing a fully populated, 175-acre real-world incubator at the base of Mount Fuji called **Woven City**.

- **Who lives there**: Toyota explicitly designed this infrastructure for **external inventors, senior citizens, families, and independent retail owners** to live in permanently.
- **The Infrastructure**: The entire town functions as a live testbed. Non-Toyota tech companies, roboticists, and healthcare startups rent laboratory and residential spaces to test smart-home AI, autonomous vehicles, and green energy technology alongside regular citizens in a living ecosystem.

### Why do they do this?

When Big Tech builds infrastructure for _other_ companies and the public to live in, they gain structural advantages:

- **The Ultimate Sandbox**: It creates a real-world testing ground for their proprietary smart-city tech, AI, cloud systems, and automated retail tools.
- **Monetizing Real Estate**: Acting as a landlord to smaller tech companies, retail brands, and residents diversification streams their corporate revenue.
- **Political Capital**: Proposing a community-integrated, beautiful city neighborhood makes local governments highly likely to approve land acquisitions and provide lucrative tax subsidies.
