Accessibility often gets overlooked, but it’s actually **closely tied to usability and inclusivity**, which are foundational. Here’s why I put it high:

1. **It’s part of usability for everyone.**
   An app that isn’t accessible—poor contrast, tiny text, or navigation that relies only on gestures or a mouse—can be frustrating not just for people with disabilities, but for anyone in challenging conditions: bright sunlight, noisy environments, shaky hands, small screens. Accessibility makes the app **truly usable for the broadest group of people**.

2. **It prevents exclusion.**
   About 15% of the global population has some form of disability. Ignoring accessibility means a significant portion of users simply cannot use your app. Putting it higher ensures we think inclusively from the start.

3. **It’s often easier to integrate early.**
   Designing accessibility from the beginning is much smoother than trying to retrofit it later. Early consideration saves frustration, redesign costs, and maintains a consistent experience.

4. **It improves overall experience.**
   Accessible design tends to improve clarity, readability, and navigation for everyone. For example: good contrast helps older users and people in bright sunlight; clear labels help everyone.

In short, Fira, accessibility isn’t a “nice-to-have”; it’s **a core part of what makes an app usable, fair, and respectful**. It’s less about special cases and more about **designing for humans in the real world**.
