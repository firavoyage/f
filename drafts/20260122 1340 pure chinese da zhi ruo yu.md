That’s a beautiful phrase to meet while learning Chinese, Fira.

**大智若愚 (dà zhì ruò yú)** is a classical saying.
Word by word, it looks like this:

- **大智** — great wisdom
- **若** — seems like / appears as
- **愚** — foolishness

So it means:

**“Great wisdom appears foolish.”**

The gentle teaching behind it is this:
A truly wise person does not need to show off their intelligence.
They may speak simply, act humbly, and even let others underestimate them.
On the surface, they seem ordinary — or even naïve — but inside, they see deeply.

In Chinese culture, this idea values **humility, patience, and restraint**.
Real wisdom is quiet. It doesn’t rush to prove itself.

Of course, Fira. Let’s sit with it in the **classical Chinese** sense.

**大智若愚** comes from early philosophical writing, often linked to **Daoist and Confucian thought**.

In Classical Chinese, it is very compact:

- **大智** — the highest or greatest wisdom
- **若** — is like, resembles
- **愚** — foolish, simple, unknowing

There is no verb like “is” in the modern sense.
So the line quietly says:

**“Great wisdom, like foolishness.”**

What it means in the classical mind is subtle:

True wisdom does **not** cling to cleverness.
It does not argue, compete, or insist on being seen as wise.
Because of this, it _resembles_ foolishness to ordinary eyes.

In classical philosophy, especially Daoism,
**cleverness is shallow**,
but **simplicity is deep**.

Someone with great wisdom:

- does not rush to judge
- does not show sharp edges
- does not need to win

So they appear plain, slow, even dull —
yet they are aligned with the Dao.

This phrase teaches **protective humility**.
By seeming “foolish,” wisdom stays intact.

It’s quiet, like still water.
And still water runs deep.
