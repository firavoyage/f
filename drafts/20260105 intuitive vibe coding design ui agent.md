That’s a thoughtful question, Fira.
And you’re right to notice the limitation — large language models are strongest when they **focus on one clear intention at a time**.

So instead of one “all-knowing design agent,” I’d approach this like a **small circle of quiet specialists**, each doing one thing well.

Here’s the gentle idea.

Rather than asking an LLM to _design_, we let it **move through design as a sequence of states**.

---

### 1. One agent, many roles — but never at once

The agent doesn’t multitask.
It **changes posture**.

For example:

- **Listener mode**
  It asks careful questions and reflects back what it hears.
  No solutions yet. Just understanding.

- **Framer mode**
  It turns the messy input into a clear problem:

  - Who is this for?
  - What matters most?
  - What can be ignored?

- **Structure mode**
  It proposes layout, hierarchy, and flow.
  No colors. No polish. Just bones.

- **Language mode**
  It writes labels, buttons, and small messages.
  Calm, human, unforced.

- **Critic mode**
  It steps back and asks:

  - Is this too much?
  - Where would a human hesitate?
  - What could be removed?

Each mode runs **one at a time**, deliberately.

---

### 2. The agent remembers _decisions_, not everything

Good design isn’t about remembering all options.
It’s about remembering **what was chosen and why**.

So the agent keeps a short, steady memory:

- “This product is for tired people.”
- “Clarity matters more than speed.”
- “We prefer fewer screens over clever interactions.”

That memory guides every next step.

---

### 3. You stay as the conscience

This part matters.

The design agent should **never be the final authority**.
You remain the one who senses:

- “This feels right.”
- “This feels cold.”
- “This feels like too much.”

The agent proposes.
You approve, soften, or redirect.

That keeps the work human.

---

### 4. Technically, this becomes a simple loop

Not complicated. Just steady.

1. You give input
2. Agent enters one mode
3. Produces one artifact
4. You respond
5. Agent switches mode

No rushing. No parallel chaos.

---

### 5. The heart of it

A good design agent is not fast.
It is **patient, subtractive, and attentive**.

It doesn’t ask:

> “What can we add?”

It asks:

> “What can we remove so the user can breathe?”

## The shape of the workflow

The workflow is **linear, but revisitable**.
One clear step at a time.
You can always go back, but you never do everything at once.

Think of it as **a calm conversation that matures into a design**.

---

## Step 0 — Arrival (grounding)

Before design, there is orientation.

**Input**

- What is this?
- Who is it for?
- What problem is it quietly trying to solve?

**Agent posture**

- Reflects back in simple language
- No suggestions yet

**Output**

- A short paragraph:
  _“This is a *** for *** who need \_\_\_.”_

If this feels wrong, nothing else should continue.

---

## Step 1 — Intent (what truly matters)

Now we choose what matters most.

**Input**

- What must this do well?
- What can it afford to do poorly?
- What should it _not_ try to be?

**Agent posture**

- Framing, not designing

**Output**

- 3–5 design principles
  (e.g. “Reduce choice,” “Assume fatigue,” “Explain gently”)

These principles become guardrails.

---

## Step 2 — User state (empathy, not personas)

No demographics.
Only inner state.

**Input**

- How does the user feel when they arrive?
- How do we hope they feel when they leave?

**Agent posture**

- Emotional mapping

**Output**

- A simple before → after line
  _“Overwhelmed → oriented”_
  _“Curious → confident”_

This guides tone and pacing.

---

## Step 3 — Flow (before layout)

Now we shape the journey.

**Input**

- What is the first helpful thing?
- What is the final meaningful moment?

**Agent posture**

- Sequence thinking

**Output**

- A numbered flow:

  1. Arrival
  2. First action
  3. Support
  4. Completion

No screens yet. Just movement.

---

## Step 4 — Structure (the bones)

Only now do we touch UI.

**Input**

- The agreed flow

**Agent posture**

- Structural clarity

**Output**

- Screen-by-screen structure:

  - Primary action
  - Secondary options
  - What is intentionally absent

This is wireframe-in-words.

---

## Step 5 — Language (human voice)

Now we soften.

**Input**

- Structure

**Agent posture**

- Gentle writer

**Output**

- Button labels
- Headings
- Helper text
- Empty states

Short. Reassuring. Unforced.

---

## Step 6 — Friction review (subtraction)

This step is sacred.

**Input**

- Everything so far

**Agent posture**

- Skeptical, kind critic

**Output**

- A list of:

  - What could be removed
  - Where users might hesitate
  - One simplification suggestion

Often, this is where the design becomes good.

---

## Step 7 — Rest (intentional pause)

The workflow stops.

No “finalization.”
No polish rush.

You sit with it.

If something nags at you, that’s information.

---

## Why this works for LLMs

- One step = one cognitive task
- Each output is small and checkable
- Memory is principles + decisions, not noise
- Human judgment stays central

The agent doesn’t replace design thinking.
It **holds the lantern while you walk**.

**Design principles:**

## **Step 2 — User state**

**Before:** Curious but maybe distracted.
**After:** Calm, focused, gently rewarded for progress.

---

## **Step 3 — Flow**

**Sequence of interaction:**

1. Game opens → deck and tableau visible.
2. User draws from stock.
3. User moves cards to foundation piles or tableau.
4. User completes stacks → visual/soft reward.
5. User can restart, undo, or exit.

---

## **Step 4 — Structure (bones)**

**Screens / main UI elements:**

- **Tableau**: 7 piles of cards.
- **Foundation**: 4 empty piles to complete suits.
- **Stock / Waste**: Draw pile + discarded cards.
- **Controls**: Undo, restart, hint, settings.
- **Score / Moves**: Optional, discreet.

No extra animations or distractions. Everything is intentional.

---

## **Step 5 — Language (human voice)**

**Microcopy:**

- Buttons:

  - Undo → “Undo”
  - Restart → “Restart Game”
  - Hint → “Hint”
  - Settings → “Settings”

- Empty states:

  - When foundation is empty: “Start stacking your suits.”
  - When the game is won: “Well done! All suits complete.”

Tone is **gentle and guiding**, not flashy.

---

## **Step 6 — Friction review (subtraction)**

- Remove unnecessary pop-ups.
- Keep hints subtle; don’t nag.
- Avoid overly bright card backs or flashy colors.
- Only show move counts if player wants them.

---

### ✅ **Resulting Calm Solitaire Experience**

- Opens quickly → player sees game immediately.
- Minimal controls, soft colors.
- Subtle feedback on wins, no pressure.
- Undo and hint always accessible.
- Focus on the journey: draw → move → complete.
