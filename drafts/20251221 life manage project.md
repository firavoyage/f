### The only 5 questions you ever really need

These are not “best practices.”
They are **anchors**. You come back to them again and again.

#### 1. *What am I trying to learn right now?*

Early projects are about learning, not winning.

* Learning if the idea makes sense
* Learning if *you* enjoy working on it
* Learning what’s harder than expected

If you can’t answer this question, you’re drifting.

> Practical habit:
> Before any work session, write one sentence:
> **“Today I’m trying to learn…”**

That’s management.

---

#### 2. *What is the smallest step that moves this forward?*

Big plans create fear.
Small steps create motion.

Not:

* “Build the system”
  But:
* “Make one screen”
* “Store one piece of data”
* “Write one rough script”

If the step feels heavy, it’s too big.

> Practical rule:
> If a task takes more than 1–2 hours, cut it in half.

That’s project management without a tool.

---

#### 3. *What assumption am I making?*

Every project rests on invisible guesses.

Examples:

* “Users will understand this”
* “This library will be easy”
* “I’ll stay motivated”

When assumptions stay hidden, they cause pain.

> Practical habit:
> When stuck, ask:
> **“What am I assuming is true right now?”**

Then test *that*, not the whole project.

---

#### 4. *What is costing me energy?*

Progress is not just output.
It’s **sustainability**.

Watch for:

* Constant context switching
* Endless refactoring
* Learning too many things at once

Your energy is a real constraint, not a weakness.

> Practical check (weekly):
>
> * What drained me?
> * What gave me energy?
>   Do more of the second.

That’s real management.

---

#### 5. *Is this still worth continuing?*

This is the question most people avoid.

Stopping is not failure.
Dragging something lifeless is.

> Practical permission:
> You are allowed to stop a project **once it has taught you something**.

That’s maturity.

---

### A simple structure you can reuse forever

No frameworks. No jargon.

Every project moves through **three repeating phases**:

#### 1. Explore

You don’t know much. You try small things.

* messy
* curious
* lightweight

#### 2. Build

Something seems promising. You add structure.

* clearer goals
* fewer experiments
* more consistency

#### 3. Reflect

You pause.

* What worked?
* What surprised me?
* What should change?

Then you loop back.

If you ever feel lost, ask:
**“Am I exploring, building, or reflecting?”**

That’s enough structure.

Avoid asking for:

* “Best architecture”
* “Perfect plan”
  Too early, those harm more than help.
