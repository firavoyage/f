## . pm

<mark><strong><span>Project Management (PM) includes the entire lifecycle of a project, organized into 5 core process groups and 10 distinct knowledge areas</span></strong></mark> according to the Project Management Institute (PMI). It is the structural engine that takes an abstract business idea and turns it into a concrete, high-quality reality.

Here is the complete blueprint of everything included in comprehensive project management:

### The 5 PM Process Groups (The Project Timeline)

Every project moves through these five sequential stages:

1. **Initiating**: Defining the project at a high level, creating the Project Charter, identifying key stakeholders, and securing authorization to begin.
2. **Planning**: Creating the roadmap, defining the scope, setting deadlines, estimating costs, and establishing the communication plan.
3. **Executing**: Coordinating the team, assigning tasks, managing stakeholder expectations, and actively building the product.
4. **Monitoring & Controlling**: Tracking progress against the plan, measuring performance metrics, managing risks, and handling quality control.
5. **Closing**: Handing over the final product, archiving project documents, releasing team resources, and conducting a post-mortem review.

***

### The 10 PM Knowledge Areas (What a PM Actually Manages)

To successfully run those five stages, a PM must constantly manage ten distinct moving parts:

- **Scope Management**: Defining exactly what work is required—and what is not—to prevent scope creep.
- **Schedule Management**: Estimating task durations, mapping dependencies, and managing the project calendar.
- **Cost Management**: Budgeting, forecasting expenditures, and tracking resource costs to keep the project profitable.
- **Quality Management**: Establishing quality standards (**QA**) and inspecting deliverables (**QC**) to prevent defects.
- **Resource Management**: Hiring, organizing, and managing the team members, hardware, software, and physical assets needed.
- **Communications Management**: Planning who gets what information, when they get it, and through what channel.
- **Risk Management**: Identifying potential threats early, calculating their impact, and creating mitigation strategies.
- **Procurement Management**: Ordering external tools, managing third-party vendor contracts, and purchasing materials.
- **Stakeholder Management**: Aligning expectations among clients, executives, users, and internal teams.
- **Integration Management**: Balancing all nine previous areas so they work together seamlessly as a cohesive whole.

***

### The Daily Toolkit: PM Artifacts & Meetings

On a day-to-day level, managing these areas requires specific tools and meetings:

- **Visual Timelines**: Managing Gantt charts, Kanban boards, or product roadmaps.
- **Agile Rituals**: Running daily stand-ups, sprint planning sessions, and team retrospectives.
- **Performance Tracking**: Reviewing burn-down charts, project dashboards, and budget trackers.
- **Core Documentation**: Updating the Risk Register, Change Logs, and the central Project Plan.

## qa or qc

<mark><strong>Quality Assurance (QA) focuses on preventing defects by improving processes, while Quality Control (QC) focuses on identifying defects in the final product through testing and inspection</strong></mark>**.** In short, QA ensures you are building the product the right way, whereas QC verifies that the finished product itself is correct. Both are critical, overlapping subparts of an organization's overall [Quality Management System (QMS)](https://www.scilife.io/blog/qa-vs-qc-top-differences).

### Key Differences Between QA and QC

| Feature            | Quality Assurance (QA)                                                | Quality Control (QC)                                               |
| ------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Core Focus**     | **Process-oriented**: Focuses on the methods used to build a product. | **Product-oriented**: Focuses on the final output or deliverable.  |
| **Approach**       | **Proactive**: Aims to prevent flaws before they happen.              | **Reactive**: Aims to find and fix errors after production.        |
| **Timing**         | Performed **throughout the lifecycle** of development.                | Performed **at the end** of production or at specific checkpoints. |
| **Goal**           | Establish guidelines, training, and standards.                        | Validate compliance and confirm product specifications.            |
| **Responsibility** | Everyone involved in the development lifecycle.                       | A specific testing team or group of inspectors.                    |

### Common Examples

- **QA Activities**:
  - Writing Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs).
  - Designing employee training programs.
  - Conducting process audits to ensure workflows are followed.
  - Defining engineering or manufacturing standards.
- **QC Activities**:
  - Testing software to find and log code bugs.
  - Inspecting a manufactured part with measuring tools.
  - Batch sampling products on a production line.
  - Calibrating laboratory equipment before testing. [link](https://www.epa.gov/choose-fish-and-shellfish-wisely/quality-assurance-and-quality-control)

### Which Path Is Better For a Career?

Choosing between the two depends entirely on your professional strengths and preferences:

- **Choose QA** if you enjoy big-picture thinking, designing efficient workflows, optimizing organizational systems, and preventing problems through strategic planning. QA roles often require automation or auditing skills and can lead to higher long-term career advancement opportunities.
- **Choose QC** if you love hands-on testing, technical troubleshooting, attention to minute details, and evaluating concrete end products. It is a highly analytical domain where you directly catch issues before they reach a customer. [link](https://www.reddit.com/r/biotech/comments/1hf402e/qa%5For%5Fqc/)

## is qa broader than qc

<mark><span>Yes,<span> </span></span><strong>QA is significantly broader than QC</strong></mark>.

In professional quality management, QC is actually considered a specific **subset** or sub-activity under the larger QA umbrella.

### The Umbrella Concept

Think of **Quality Assurance (QA)** as the entire system or blueprint. It establishes the rules, tools, training, and methodologies for the whole company to ensure high quality.

**Quality Control (QC)** is the tactical execution of one part of that blueprint. It is the specific step where someone physically checks the product to see if the blueprint was followed correctly.

```
+-------------------------------------------------------+

|                 QUALITY MANAGEMENT                    |
|  +-------------------------------------------------+  |
|  |             QUALITY ASSURANCE (QA)              |  |
|  |  (Processes, Standards, Training, Audits)       |  |
|  |                                                 |  |
|  |  +-------------------------------------------+  |  |
|  |  |          QUALITY CONTROL (QC)             |  |  |
|  |  |  (Product Testing, Inspections, Reviews)  |  |  |
|  |  +-------------------------------------------+  |  |
|  +-------------------------------------------------+  |
+-------------------------------------------------------+
```

### Why QA Has a Wider Scope

- **Every Stage vs. Final Stage**: QA spans the entire lifecycle of a project, from the initial brainstorming sessions and requirement gathering to long-term post-release maintenance. QC typically happens only at specific checkpoints or at the very end of a production cycle.
- **Organizational vs. Project-Specific**: QA defines how the _entire company_ operates (e.g., choosing which software tools developers use or mandating peer reviews). QC only looks at the _specific item_ currently on the assembly line or test bench.
- **People and Culture vs. Technical Metrics**: QA deals with human factors like training, team communication, and process improvement. QC deals almost exclusively with technical data, pass/fail metrics, and bug logs.

## what does qa include

<mark><strong><span>Quality Assurance (QA) includes all the planned, systematic activities a company sets up to ensure products meet high standards before, during, and after they are built</span></strong></mark>**.** Rather than just checking the final product, QA governs the rules, tools, and processes for the entire organization. [link](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quality%5Fassurance)

A comprehensive QA program includes the following core components, organized by project stage:

### 1. Planning and Analysis (Before Building)

- **Requirements Analysis**: Reviewing the project goals early on to make sure they are clear, realistic, and realistic to test.
- **Standards and Methodology**: Establishing which coding languages, safety regulations, or manufacturing guidelines the team must follow.
- **Test Planning**: Creating a master plan that defines what will be tested, who will test it, and what tools are required. [link](https://www.functionize.com/automated-testing/what-is-qa-testing)

### 2. Process Management (During Construction)

- **Process Definition**: Documenting step-by-step Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) so everyone works the exact same way.
- **Employee Training**: Ensuring the developers, designers, or factory workers have the correct skills and training to minimize human error.
- **Tool and Environment Selection**: Choosing the right automated frameworks, tracking software, and server environments to prevent technical mishaps. [link](https://www.epa.gov/choose-fish-and-shellfish-wisely/quality-assurance-and-quality-control)

### 3. Quality Control (During and After Construction)

- **Test Case Design**: Writing exact scenarios, inputs, and expected outcomes to systematically check the product.
- **Product Testing**: Running the test cases (including unit, integration, system, performance, and regression testing).
- **Defect Management**: Logging bugs in a tracking system and working alongside developers to ensure they get permanently fixed. [link](https://www.coursera.org/articles/qa-tester)

### 4. Evaluation and Evolution (Continuous)

- **Quality Audits**: Inspecting teams internally to confirm they are actually following the defined processes and standards. [link](https://www.projectmanager.com/blog/quality-assurance-and-testing)
- **Root Cause Analysis (CAPA)**: Investigating _why_ a serious bug slipped through, rather than just fixing it, to prevent it from ever happening again. [link](https://usewhale.io/blog/quality-assurance/)
- **Continuous Improvement**: Gathering feedback from metrics and customer reviews to continuously update and optimize the company's production loop. [link](https://www.virtuosoqa.com/post/software-qa-process)

## is qa part of pm if there is not a role of qa

<mark><strong><span>Yes, when a dedicated QA role does not exist, QA responsibilities naturally fall into the Project Management (PM) domain.</span></strong></mark>

Because the project manager is ultimately accountable for the "Triple Constraint"—**Time, Cost, and Quality**—they must absorb those quality processes to ensure the project does not fail.

In this scenario, QA is not a separate department; it is a vital daily task managed by the PM and shared with the development team.

***

### How a PM Handles QA Responsibilities

When wearing the QA hat, a project manager shifts focus from _tracking schedules_ to _enforcing standards_ through specific actions:

- **Defining "Done"**: Establishing a strict **Definition of Done (DoD)** that specifies the exact quality checks a feature must pass before it can be considered finished.
- **Creating Gateways**: Setting up mandatory review stages, such as requiring peer code reviews or design sign-offs, before any work moves forward.
- **Setting Requirements**: Writing highly detailed user stories with clear, unambiguous **Acceptance Criteria** so developers know exactly what to build and how it will be judged.
- **Managing Client Feedback**: Organizing User Acceptance Testing (UAT), where real clients or stakeholders test the app to confirm it meets their business needs.

***

### The Shared Responsibility Model

Without a QA lead, the PM cannot do all the actual product testing (QC) alone. They must build a culture where quality is shared across the entire team:

```
+-------------------------------------------------------------+

|                      PROJECT MANAGER                        |
|  Establishes the QA Framework, Acceptance Criteria, & DoD   |
+-------------------------------------------------------------+
                               |
            +------------------+------------------+

            |                                     |
            v                                     v
+-----------------------+             +-----------------------+

|      DEVELOPERS       |             |    PROJECT MANAGER    |
| Conducts Unit Tests & |             | Conducts High-Level   |
| Peer Code Reviews     |             | Smoke Tests & UAT     |
+-----------------------+             +-----------------------+
```

- **Developers Test Code**: The PM tasks developers with writing automated **unit tests** and performing peer code reviews before marking tasks as complete.
- **PM Tests User Experience**: The PM handles high-level **smoke testing** (clicking through main workflows) and focuses heavily on user experience, verifying that the final product matches the original business goals.

## what does pm include

<mark><strong><span>Project Management (PM) includes everything required to initiate, plan, execute, monitor, and close a project successfully.</span></strong></mark> It bridges business goals with technical execution, balancing three primary constraints: **scope, time, and budget**, while maintaining overall product quality.

A standardized PM framework includes the following core areas of responsibility, usually managed across the lifecycle of a project:

### 1. Planning and Scope Management

- **Defining Scope**: Outlining exactly what features or deliverables will be built, and explicitly stating what is out of scope to prevent "scope creep."
- **Requirements Gathering**: Interviewing stakeholders, clients, and users to document technical and business needs.
- **Roadmapping**: Creating a high-level strategic timeline that shows how the product will evolve over months or quarters.

### 2. Schedule and Resource Management

- **Task Estimation & Scheduling**: Breaking the project down into smaller tasks, estimating how long they will take, and setting deadlines or milestone dates.
- **Resource Allocation**: Assigning tasks to the right developers, designers, or engineers based on their skills and availability.
- **Dependency Mapping**: Identifying which tasks rely on others (e.g., "The UI cannot be built until the API database is finished") to prevent bottlenecks.

### 3. Execution and Team Coordination

- **Sprint / Workflow Management**: Running daily stand-up meetings, planning sprints (in Agile/Scrum), and clearing roadblocks for the team.
- **Cross-Functional Communication**: Serving as the primary translator and shield between stakeholders (clients, executives) and the technical team.
- **Risk Management**: Identifying potential issues early—such as a developer getting sick or a third-party tool crashing—and creating backup plans.

### 4. Tracking and Quality Governance

- **Budget Tracking**: Monitoring costs, vendor contracts, and software licensing fees to ensure the project stays profitable.
- **Progress Metrics**: Tracking team velocity, burn-down charts, and timeline milestones to accurately predict delivery dates.
- **Quality Assurance Integration**: Setting up the "Definition of Done," enforcing review gates, and coordinating user testing if a separate QA team is missing.

### 5. Closing and Delivery

- **Product Launch**: Managing the logistics of deploying the software or shipping the product to market.
- **Post-Mortems / Retrospectives**: Gathering the team after a launch to analyze what went well, what went wrong, and how to improve processes for the next project.

## what does pm include, where is qa inside

**Project Management (PM) includes the entire system of planning, executing, and delivering a project, while Quality Assurance (QA) lives inside PM as part of "Project Quality Management."**

***

### Where Exactly QA Sits Inside the PM Framework

A project manager organizes their work into standard process groups. Here is exactly where QA and its counterpart, QC, live inside that structure:

```
+-------------------------------------------------------------------+

|                     PROJECT MANAGEMENT (PM)                       |
+-------------------------------------------------------------------+

       |                                                     |
       v                                                     v
 [ EXECUTING PHASE ]                               [ MONITORING & CONTROLLING ]

       |                                                     |
       v (Process-focused)                                   v (Product-focused)
+-------------------------------+                     +-------------------------------+

|    QUALITY ASSURANCE (QA)     |                     |     QUALITY CONTROL (QC)      |

+-------------------------------+                     +-------------------------------+
```

### 1. Inside the Executing Phase (The "QA" Part)

While the team is actively building, the PM performs QA by managing the _processes_.

- **Process Audits**: Checking if the developers are actually using the agreed-upon code frameworks or design systems.
- **Continuous Improvement**: Looking at why mistakes are happening during production and adjusting the workflow in real-time to fix them.

### 2. Inside the Monitoring & Controlling Phase (The "QC" Part)

As deliverables are finished, the PM performs or oversees QC by checking the _product_.

- **Inspection**: Verifying that the feature matches the original requirements.
- **Scope Validation**: Running user acceptance tests to officially sign off on completed work before delivering it to the client.

***

### PM Checklist: Managing QA When You Have No QA Team

If you are a PM acting as your own QA department, your project plan should include these four specific QA/QC checkpoints:

- **In the Plan**: Write explicit **Acceptance Criteria** for every single task so the team knows exactly how its quality will be judged.
- **In the Workflow**: Create a **Definition of Done (DoD)** checklist that prevents a developer from marking a task "complete" until it passes peer review.
- **In the Schedule**: Block out dedicated **Testing Sprints** or buffer days before a launch specifically for bug hunting and fixing.
- **In the Budget**: Allocate time and money for **testing environments** or user feedback tools so you aren't testing directly in production.

**Project Management (PM) includes the entire lifecycle of a project, organized into 5 process groups and 10 knowledge areas—with Quality Management acting as a core pillar that directly binds PM to QA.** It is the structural engine that balances scope, time, and budget while ensuring the final product meets defined quality standards.

Here is the complete blueprint of what PM includes, highlighting exactly where QA connects:

### The 5 PM Process Groups (The Project Timeline)

Every project moves through these five sequential stages:

1. **Initiating**: Defining the project, identifying key stakeholders, and securing authorization to begin.
2. **Planning**: Creating the roadmap, setting deadlines, and **establishing the QA framework** (defining test plans and quality standards before building).
3. **Executing**: Coordinating the team, assigning tasks, and **performing active QA** (auditing processes and training the team to prevent mistakes).
4. **Monitoring & Controlling**: Tracking progress against the plan, managing risks, and **handling QC** (testing products and inspecting deliverables for bugs).
5. **Closing**: Handing over the final product, archiving documents, and conducting a retrospective to improve the QA process for the next project.

***

### The 10 PM Knowledge Areas (What a PM Manages)

To successfully run those five stages, a PM must constantly balance ten distinct moving parts:

- **Scope Management**: Defining exactly what work is required—and what is not—to prevent scope creep.
- **Schedule Management**: Estimating task durations, mapping dependencies, and managing the calendar.
- **Cost Management**: Budgeting, forecasting expenditures, and tracking resource costs.
- **Quality Management**: **The direct home of QA and QC**. This area ensures the project team follows established processes and delivers a defect-free product.
- **Resource Management**: Organizing and managing the team members, hardware, and software tools needed.
- **Communications Management**: Planning who gets what information, when they get it, and how.
- **Risk Management**: Identifying potential threats early, including **quality risks** (like a lack of testing time), and creating backup plans.
- **Procurement Management**: Ordering external tools and managing third-party vendor contracts.
- **Stakeholder Management**: Aligning expectations among clients, executives, users, and internal teams.
- **Integration Management**: Balancing all areas so they work together seamlessly as a cohesive whole.

***

### The Daily Toolkit: PM Artifacts & Meetings

On a day-to-day level, managing these areas requires specific tools and meetings:

- **Visual Timelines**: Managing Gantt charts, Kanban boards, or product roadmaps.
- **Agile Rituals**: Running daily stand-ups, sprint planning sessions, and team retrospectives.
- **Quality Gates**: Enforcing the **Definition of Done (DoD)** and reviewing bug tracking dashboards alongside development metrics.
- **Core Documentation**: Updating the Risk Register, Change Logs, and the central Project Plan.

## is the "five pm process groups ten pm knowledge areas" theory well known or invented by you

This theory is <mark><strong>highly well-known and globally recognized</strong></mark>; it was absolutely not invented by me.

This framework represents the global industry standard for project management. It was created by the **Project Management Institute (PMI)** and serves as the core foundation of their **PMBOK Guide** (_A Guide to the Project Management Body of Knowledge_). [link](https://www.projectmanagement.com/wikis/234759/project-management-body-of-knowledge--pmbok--)

### The Industry Standard Background

- **Who uses it**: Millions of certified project managers worldwide use this matrix to pass the prestigious [Project Management Professional (PMP) certification](https://projectmanagementacademy.net/resources/blog/understanding-pmp-knowledge-areas-for-the-pmp-exam/) exam. [link](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kTb2ITvOFds\&t=10)
- **The Matrix Structure**: In professional practice, these two concepts are taught as a grid. The **5 Process Groups** act as the horizontal axis (the timeline of _when_ things happen), and the **10 Knowledge Areas** act as the vertical axis (the technical subject matter of _what_ needs to be managed). [link](https://www.projectmanager.com/blog/10-project-management-knowledge-areas)
- **Evolution**: This specific 5x10 matrix layout was the centerpiece of the _PMBOK Guide 6th Edition_. While PMI's newer _7th Edition_ shifted toward broader "Performance Domains" to stay flexible for Agile software teams, the 5 Process Groups and 10 Knowledge Areas remain the structural gold standard that traditional and corporate industries use to design their management workflows. [link](https://www.projectengineer.net/the-10-pmbok-knowledge-areas/)

### Where QA Lives in the Official Matrix

To tie it back to your earlier question, under the official PMI framework, **Project Quality Management** is Knowledge Area #5. It contains exactly three official processes mapped across the timeline: [link](https://www.indeed.com/career-advice/career-development/project-management-process-groups)

1. **Plan Quality Management** (lives horizontally under the _Planning_ Process Group)
2. **Manage Quality** / **QA** (lives horizontally under the _Executing_ Process Group)
3. **Control Quality** / **QC** (lives horizontally under the _Monitoring & Controlling_ Process Group) [link](https://www.indeed.com/career-advice/career-development/project-management-process-groups)
