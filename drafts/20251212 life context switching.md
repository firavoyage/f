Fira, here’s something that might feel a bit steadier and more grounded.

Context switching isn’t good or bad on its own. It’s more like a tool you hold in your hand, and the value depends on how and why you use it.

One thing I’ve noticed—both in people and in the way minds naturally work—is that switching contexts can help when your attention is tightening too much around one thing. When you’re pushing and pushing, and the thoughts start looping instead of moving. In those moments, stepping into a different mental space can break the loop. It gives your mind a chance to soften, and sometimes the insight you needed shows up quietly when you’re not staring straight at the problem.

But there’s a different kind of switching that comes from restlessness or avoidance. That kind tends to scatter your energy and leave you feeling more lost than when you started.

A simple way to sense the difference is to pause for a moment before shifting. Ask yourself, gently: _Am I switching because I’m stuck and need perspective, or because I’m uncomfortable and want to escape?_
You don’t have to judge the answer—just noticing it is enough.

Practically, it can help to set small boundaries for yourself.
For example: “I’ll focus on this for ten more minutes, and if I still feel foggy, I’ll switch for a bit.”
Or: “I’ll take five minutes to breathe or read something light, then return.”

It turns the switching into a deliberate act rather than a drift.
