do asians tend to be calm in photos

Yes, psychological and cultural research indicates that **East Asians tend to favor calm, serene, or composed facial expressions in formal or professional photographs** compared to Westerners, who traditionally favor wide, enthusiastic smiles.

However, this cultural preference varies significantly depending on the **generation, the social context, and the type of photo being taken** (such as a casual selfie versus an official portrait). \[[1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3505229/)]

### 1. Cultural Values and "Ideal Affect"

Cross-cultural psychologists, notably Dr. Jeanne Tsai from Stanford University, study what is known as "ideal affect"—the emotional states that a culture values and strives to feel. \[[1](https://news.stanford.edu/stories/2016/02/smiles-jeanne-tsai-021516), [2](https://stanforddaily.com/2016/03/04/leaders-smiles-linked-to-culture/)]

- **Low-Arousal Positive States**: East Asian cultures (such as China, Japan, and South Korea) traditionally value low-arousal positive states like **calmness, serenity, peace, and emotional balance**. This is rooted in philosophical and religious traditions like Buddhism, Taoism, and Confucianism, which emphasize keeping emotions in harmony with one's surroundings.
- **High-Arousal Positive States**: Western cultures place a higher value on high-arousal states like **excitement, enthusiasm, and outward joy**. This difference directly translates to how people present themselves in front of a camera, where Westerners default to a wide "tooth-baring" smile, while East Asians often prefer a subtle, closed-lip smile or a composed, neutral expression. \[[1](https://medium.com/midform/insiders-insight-how-asian-culture-influences-courtesy-photography-29819be462d4), [2](https://www.reddit.com/r/NoStupidQuestions/comments/1atcazv/why%5Fdo%5Fa%5Flot%5Fof%5Fasian%5Fcountries%5Flook%5Fso%5Fcalm%5Fex/), [3](https://www.shutterstock.com/search/asian-peaceful), [4](https://jenyenwrites.com/2019/11/15/emotions-and-the-western-gaze/), [5](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4733341/)]

### 2. Collectivism vs. Individualism

- **Maintaining Social Harmony**: In collectivistic societies, showing intense, individual emotional bursts can be viewed as disruptive to social harmony. Remaining calm is viewed as a sign of maturity, self-control, and respect for others.
- **Historical Norms**: Historically, smiling broadly for official portraits was seen as undignified or foolish in many East Asian cultures. Composure conveyed respectability and status. \[[1](https://www.quora.com/Why-does-everyone-seem-so-calm-and-happy-in-China), [2](https://www.quora.com/The-Chinese-are-calm-and-peaceful-people-How-do-those-who-live-in-the-West-handle-the-less-peaceful-Western-culture-Is-that-disturbing-for-them), [3](https://medium.com/midform/insiders-insight-how-asian-culture-influences-courtesy-photography-29819be462d4), [4](https://erickimphotography.com/the-calm-spirit-of-cambodian-people/), [5](https://erickimphotography.com/the-calm-spirit-of-cambodian-people/)]

### 3. Modern Subcultures and Pop Trends

While traditional or formal photography leans toward calmness, contemporary Asian youth culture has introduced highly expressive, playful, and energetic photo trends: \[[1](https://asianreviewofbooks.com/inner-harmony-living-in-balance-by-jon-kolkin/)]

- **The V-Sign (Peace Sign)**: Across East and Southeast Asia, millions of people instantly flash a "V-sign" when a camera appears. Rather than being strictly calm, this gesture is used to break tension, look cute (_kawaii_ or _aegyo_), or provide an automatic pose when feeling slightly awkward.
- **The "Gen Z" Influence**: Interestingly, Gen Z youth globally—including in Western countries—are increasingly ditching wide smiles for poker faces or unbothered, calm expressions to convey authenticity.
- **Purposely Playful Poses**: Trends like placing a hand under the chin, winking, or creating finger hearts are incredibly popular in casual selfies and social media photos, showing that modern photography habits are highly energetic. \[[1](https://time.com/2980357/asia-photos-peace-sign-v-janet-lynn-konica-jun-inoue/), [2](https://www.quora.com/Why-do-young-Chinese-always-show-two-fingers-when-photos-are-taken-of-them), [3](https://steemit.com/life/@cikxaijen/why-asians-take-a-photo-with-peace-sign-do-you-know), [4](https://www.tiktok.com/@calmdownpod/video/7613082890699656478), [5](https://www.bbc.co.uk/newsround/articles/cvgq2gr7ng7o)]

## You said: do black ppl tend to laugh in photos

No, Black people do not inherently tend to laugh more or less than any other group in photos. Photographic expressions among Black individuals vary widely based on **personal personality, global location, historical era, and social context.**

However, looking at the topic through a cultural and historical lens reveals how portrait styles have evolved.

### 1. The Global Variety of Expressions

Black people belong to diverse cultures across the Americas, Africa, Europe, and beyond. There is no singular "Black culture" that dictates how to pose for a camera.

- **Casual vs. Formal**: Just like any other group, a Black individual might laugh heartily in a candid photo with friends but choose a fierce, neutral, or poised look for a professional headshot or fashion shoot.
- **Geographic Differences**: A person's expression is heavily influenced by the regional photo norms of where they live rather than their race.

### 2. Historical Context and Representation

Historically, African Americans and other Black communities have used photography as a powerful tool for self-determination and changing public perception.

- **Dignity and Composure**: In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, pioneering Black photographers like Addison Scurlock and James Van Der Zee captured Black subjects with immense poise, elegance, and serious composure. During eras of intense systemic racism, presenting oneself with calm, unshakeable dignity in portraits was a form of political resistance.
- **Joy as Resistance**: Conversely, the concept of "Black Joy"—capturing candid moments of laughter, dancing, and community—has also become a celebrated movement in modern photography to counter historical media depictions that focused solely on struggle and trauma.

### 3. Media, Modeling, and Pop Culture

In modern media and editorial photography, expressions are often dictated by industry trends:

- **The "Smize" and High Fashion**: The high-fashion modeling world—pioneered heavily by figures like Tyra Banks—popularized intense, unsmiling, yet fiercely expressive facial looks (like "smiling with your eyes").
- **Hip-Hop Influence**: Global hip-hop culture, which heavily influences youth photo trends across all races, often favors a cool, stoic, or "unbothered" neutral expression over a wide, laughing smile.

Ultimately, whether someone laughs, smiles subtly, or remains serious in a photo comes down to their personal mood, the vibe of the moment, and how they want to present themselves to the world.

## You said: do western ppl tend to laugh in photos

Yes, Western people tend to show **wide, tooth-baring smiles in photos, which can often look like active laughing**, especially compared to the more reserved or calm expressions favored in many non-Western cultures.

Psychologists and historians attribute this distinct photographic habit to a combination of historical shifts, immigration patterns, and cultural values.

### 1. Cultural Values and "High-Arousal" Positive Affect

Cross-cultural psychology shows that Western cultures, particularly the United States, value what is called **high-arousal positive affect**.

- **Outward Enthusiasm**: Western societies place a high premium on excitement, enthusiasm, and overt happiness.
- **The Wide Smile**: In photos, this translates to a wide smile that shows teeth and crinkles the eyes—an expression closely linked to the physical act of laughing.
- **Social Signaling**: Showing a big, laughing smile signals to others that you are approachable, successful, friendly, and positive.

### 2. The Historical Transition: From Serious to Smiling

Early Western photography actually favored strict, unsmiling composure. People did not start smiling broadly in Western photos until the early-to-mid 20th century due to a few key developments:

- **The "Say Cheese" Phenomenon**: Early cameras required long exposure times, making it difficult to hold a smile. As camera technology advanced and exposure times became instant, it became physically easier to capture spontaneous smiles and laughter.
- **Kodak's Marketing**: The Eastman Kodak Company transformed photography from a formal studio event into a casual tool for capturing personal memories. Their advertisements explicitly told consumers that photos should capture joy, holidays, and happy family moments, cementing the "happy, smiling Westerner" archetype.
- **The Rise of Dentistry**: The commercialization of modern dentistry and orthodontics made people more confident about showing their teeth in public and in portraits.

### 3. The Influence of Immigration and Social Mixing

Some sociologists suggest that the intense focus on smiling and expressive facial features in countries like the US, Canada, and Australia stems from historical immigration.

- When diverse groups of people who do not share a common language live together, **exaggerated facial expressions (like a big smile or a laugh) serve as a universal sign of non-threatening, friendly intent.**
- Over generations, this high-expressiveness became a deeply embedded cultural norm.

### 4. Modern Pushback: The "Gen Z Poker Face"

While a wide, laughing smile remains the traditional default for Westerners, modern trends are shifting:

- **The "Casual" Aesthetic**: On platforms like Instagram and TikTok, younger generations in the West are increasingly adopting neutral, unsmiling, or "unbothered" facial expressions.
- **Authenticity vs. Performance**: Many feel that a forced, wide smile looks too performative or corporate, preferring a calmer, more candid look instead.
