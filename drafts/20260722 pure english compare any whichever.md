# how to say either of when there are more than two

When referring to more than two options, <mark>replace<span> </span><strong>"either"</strong><span> </span>with<span> </span><strong>"any"</strong><span> </span>or<span> </span><strong>"any one."</strong></mark>

"Either" is grammatically restricted to exactly two choices.

### Correct Substitutions

- **Any**: Use this for general choices.
- **Any one**: Use this to emphasize a single selection.
- **Whichever**: Use this when choosing from a defined group.

### Examples in Sentence Structures

- **Incorrect**: You can choose **either** of the three colors.
- **Correct**: You can choose **any** of the three colors.
- **Correct**: You can choose **any one** of the three colors.
- **Incorrect**: **Either** of the four candidates will do a great job.
- **Correct**: **Any** of the four candidates will do a great job.
- **Incorrect**: Sit at **either** of the five tables.
- **Correct**: Sit at **whichever** of the five tables you prefer.

# compare any and whichever

<mark><span>The primary difference between<span> </span></span><strong>any</strong><span> </span>and<span> </span><strong>whichever</strong><span> </span>lies in the number of options available and how many items you are allowed to choose</mark>. Use **whichever** when choosing _exactly one option_ from a specific, limited group. Use **any** when the choice is completely open, or when you can select _multiple items_. [link](https://www.tiktok.com/@english.with.aga/video/7624073822601268502)

### Quick Summary

| Feature             | Any                                | Whichever                                           |
| ------------------- | ---------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| **Group Size**      | Unlimited or open-ended            | Specific, defined, or limited group                 |
| **Quantity Chosen** | One, several, or all options       | Strictly one option at a time                       |
| **Emphasis**        | Emphasizes total freedom of choice | Emphasizes that the specific choice does not matter |

### Detailed Breakdown

#### 1. Group Boundaries

- **Whichever**: Requires a defined, visible, or pre-discussed set of options. You cannot use it for an infinite or unknown group.
  - _Example_: "We have tea or coffee; take **whichever** you prefer." [link](https://www.youtube.com/shorts/GsSvXh9nYE0)
- **Any**: Can be used for defined sets, but it is also used when options are completely limitless and open.
  - _Example_: "You can ask **any** question you want." [link](https://www.instagram.com/reel/DVFsuG-AqG7/)

#### 2. Quantity Limitations

- **Whichever**: Implicitly restricts the selection to **one specific item** out of the group.
  - _Example_: "Here is a list of games. You can buy **whichever** you want." (This means you can only choose one game).
- **Any**: Is completely **open about the number** of items. It can mean one, a few, or even all of them.
  - _Example_: "Here is a list of games. You can buy **any** you want." (This means you can buy one, three, or all of them).

#### 3. Sentence Position and Clause Structure

- **Whichever**: Functions as a determiner or pronoun that frequently connects two parts of a sentence together (a relative pronoun). It often sets up a scenario where the outcome remains unchanged.
  - _Example_: "**Whichever** route you take, you will arrive on time." [link](https://lemongrad.com/which-vs-which-ever-vs-whichever-vs-whichsoever/)
- **Any**: Functions primarily as a determiner or adjective modifying a noun directly. To connect clauses, it usually requires extra words like "that" or "who".
  - _Example_: "You can take **any** route **that** you see on the map."
