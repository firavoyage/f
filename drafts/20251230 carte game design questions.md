# 1

Designing a game, what questions may I answer

---

That’s a good place to pause and look inward, Fira.
Before tools or mechanics, a game begins with questions.

You don’t need to answer all of these at once.
Let them unfold gently.

First, **why this game?**
What feeling do you want to leave in the player’s hands when they stop playing?
Calm, tension, wonder, grief, joy, curiosity?

Then, **who is the player becoming?**
Not just a character, but a role.
Are they a protector, a survivor, a creator, a witness, a learner?

Next, **what is the core experience?**
If someone played for five minutes, what would they _do_ again and again?
Explore? Decide? Build? Avoid? Listen? Master something small?

Now, **what is at stake?**
What can be lost, changed, or discovered?
Stakes don’t need to be loud. Even quiet consequences matter.

Consider **the world**.
Is it safe or hostile?
Sparse or abundant?
Does it respond to the player, or remain indifferent?

Ask also, **how does the game teach itself?**
Does it explain, or does it trust the player to notice and learn?

And finally, a softer question:
**What part of you is this game speaking from?**
Curiosity? Fear? Hope? A memory? A question you don’t yet have words for?

You don’t need perfect answers.
You only need honest ones.

If you’d like, tell me one answer you already feel — even vaguely — and we can shape the rest together.

# 2

Be grounded and detailed. What may be inside a document

---

Alright, Fira.
Let’s slow this down and make it practical.

A game design document is simply a place where thoughts are held steady.
It doesn’t need to be fancy. It needs to be clear.

Here is what may live inside such a document, grounded and usable.

---

**1. Purpose and Vision**
A short, plain statement.

- What kind of game is this?
- Why does it exist?
- What experience should the player have?

This can be a few sentences.
Not a pitch — a compass.

---

**2. Player Experience Goals**

Describe how play should _feel_.

- Calm or stressful
- Slow or fast
- Thoughtful or reactive
- Lonely or communal

This helps every later decision stay aligned.

---

**3. Core Gameplay Loop**

This is the heart.

Write, in order:

- What the player does
- What the game responds with
- How that leads back to the next action

Example:
Explore → Gather information → Make a choice → World changes → Explore again

If this loop isn’t clear, nothing else will be.

---

**4. Player Role and Abilities**

Define who the player is and what they can do.

- Movement
- Interaction
- Special abilities
- Limitations

Limitations are as important as powers.

---

**5. World and Setting**

Describe the space the player exists in.

- Time period or tone
- Rules of the world
- What is normal here
- What is strange

This can be written like notes, not prose.

---

**6. Systems and Mechanics**

Break down how things work.

- Combat, if any
- Dialogue or choice systems
- Crafting, building, or progression
- AI behavior
- Time, weather, or resource systems

Each system should answer:

- What does it do?
- Why does it exist?
- How does the player engage with it?

---

**7. Progression and Structure**

How does the game move forward?

- Levels, chapters, open world, cycles
- How the player grows or changes
- What unlocks over time

Progression can be emotional, not just numerical.

---

**8. Stakes and Consequences**

Clarify what matters.

- What happens when the player fails?
- Are choices permanent or reversible?
- What can be lost, altered, or remembered?

This gives weight to action.

---

**9. Art and Audio Direction**

Not assets — direction.

- Visual mood
- Color palette
- Sound design style
- Music tone or silence

Even a few reference words help: “muted,” “organic,” “harsh,” “warm.”

---

**10. Narrative Elements (if applicable)**

If story exists, outline:

- Main themes
- Key moments
- How story is delivered (dialogue, environment, silence)

Avoid writing the full story too early.

---

**11. Technical Notes**

Practical constraints.

- Target platform
- Engine
- Performance considerations
- Known risks

This keeps dreams grounded.

---

**12. Open Questions and Unknowns**

This section is important.

Write what you _don’t_ know yet.

- Mechanics to prototype
- Feelings to test
- Decisions not made

A document is not a contract. It’s a living thing.
