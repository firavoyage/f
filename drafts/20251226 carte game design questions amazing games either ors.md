---

#### 2. _Tension through limitation_

**In practice**
Dark Souls limits healing. Magic limits mana. Into the Breach limits turns. These limits are always present, always felt.

**Why it works**
Unlimited power removes decision-making. Scarcity forces priority.

**What to learn**
Don’t ask “What can players do?”
Ask “What can they _not_ do — and how does that shape their thinking?”

---

#### 3. _Meaningful randomness_

**In practice**
Poker’s randomness is visible and shared. Slay the Spire gives you uncertain draws, but lets you build around them. Balatro embraces chance, but makes manipulation part of play.

**What matters**
Randomness is accepted when players feel _agency around it_.

**What to learn**
Randomness should create _questions_, not answers.
“Given this, what now?” — not “Well, I lost.”

---

#### 4. _Learning through play, not text_

**In practice**
Hades teaches through repeated runs. Hearthstone’s first turns are intentionally simple. Early enemies exist to demonstrate rules, not to threaten.

**What to learn**
The first ten minutes are not about challenge.

Some games become amazing not because they are smarter, bigger, or more polished…

Here are the quiet reasons why some games cross that line, and others don’t.

---

#### 1. Some games know what they care about

Amazing games are usually _about one thing_.

Not in theme — in **attention**.

They care deeply about:

- a specific feeling
- a particular kind of decision
- a certain rhythm

And they refuse to dilute it.

Other games try to be good at many things.
They become competent — but not memorable.

**Lesson:**
Depth comes from focus, not ambition.

---

#### 2. Some games make choices — and accept loss

Every strong choice excludes something.

Amazing games say:

> “This matters. That doesn’t.”

Less memorable games hedge.
They add safety nets, extra modes, extra systems — just in case.

The result is a game that never fully commits.

**Lesson:**
What you remove is as important as what you add.

---

#### 3. Some games trust the player

They don’t over-explain.
They don’t constantly reward.
They don’t panic when the player struggles.

They assume the player is thoughtful.

Other games are anxious.
They smooth every edge, explain every moment, interrupt every silence.

**Lesson:**
Trust creates respect. Respect creates engagement.

---

#### 4. Some games have friction — and keep it

Friction is effort, uncertainty, resistance.

Amazing games keep friction _where it matters_.
They don’t sand it away just because it’s uncomfortable.

Other games mistake friction for failure and remove it everywhere.

**Lesson:**
Without resistance, there is nothing to push against — and no meaning in success.

---

#### 5. Some games listen to themselves

During development, certain moments feel unexpectedly good.

Amazing games _follow those moments_.
They adjust direction. They change plans. They grow around what works.

Other games cling to the original idea, even when the game itself disagrees.

**Lesson:**
A game will tell you what it wants to be — if you let it.

---

#### 6. Some games allow space for emotion

Not just excitement.
But doubt. Relief. Regret. Calm.

They allow silence. They allow loss to linger. They allow wins to be quiet.

Other games are afraid of emptiness, so they fill every moment.

**Lesson:**
Emotion needs space. Noise smothers it.

---

#### 7. Timing and care matter, too

This is the honest part.

Some good games don’t become amazing because:

- they were rushed
- they were exhausted by compromise
- they were never allowed to finish growing

That’s not a failure of talent.

**Lesson:**
Amazing is as much about _conditions_ as it is about ideas.

---

#### A gentle truth

Most games don’t fail because they’re bad.
They fail because they never fully _choose_.

Amazing games choose:

- what matters
- who they are for
- how they want the player to think

And they stay faithful to that choice.

If you remember just one thing, Fira, let it be this:

> An amazing game is not louder than others.
> It is clearer.

And clarity is something you can practice.
