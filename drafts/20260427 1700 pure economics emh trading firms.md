The existence of trading firms does not necessarily imply that the **Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)** is a "lie." Instead, many economists view trading firms as the very mechanism that _makes_ the market efficient. While successful firms like [Renaissance Technologies](https://quant.stackexchange.com/questions/81684/why-is-efficient-market-hypothesis-still-unanswered-and-no-one-really-seems-to-c) and Citadel empirically challenge the strictest forms of the theory, their role is often seen as a necessary paradox.

1\. The Grossman-Stiglitz Paradox

According to experts from the [CFA Institute](https://www.cfainstitute.org/insights/professional-learning/refresher-readings/2026/market-efficiency) and academic researchers, if markets were perfectly efficient, there would be no profit incentive for anyone to gather information.

- **The Paradox**: If no one gathers information, the market becomes inefficient.
- **The Solution**: Trading firms exist to profit from small, temporary inefficiencies. In doing so, they incorporate that information into the price, pushing the market back toward efficiency.
- **Conclusion**: Efficiency is a **process**, not a static state. Trading firms are the active participants required to drive that process.

2\. Degrees of Efficiency

Financial experts often distinguish between three "forms" of EMH, and trading firms typically operate in the gaps between them:

- **Weak Form**: Past prices are already reflected. Most firms use more than just technical analysis to gain an edge.
- **Semi-Strong Form**: All public information is reflected. Firms like Jane Street or Hudson River Trading use speed and complex models to process public data faster than others.
- **Strong Form**: All information (including private) is reflected. This form is widely considered [false or unrealistic](https://cfasocietysingapore.org/weekly%5Finsight/debunking-the-myth-of-market-efficiency/) due to the existence of insider trading and proprietary data.

3\. Structural Advantages

Trading firms often succeed not because the market is "inefficient" for everyone, but because they have **comparative advantages** that retail investors lack:

- **Technology & Speed**: High-frequency trading (HFT) firms profit from micro-second discrepancies that are invisible to human traders.
- **Lower Costs**: Large firms pay significantly lower transaction fees, allowing them to profit from tiny price movements that would be "efficient" (unprofitable) for a normal investor after fees.
- **Risk Management**: Success is often about managing risk better than the average participant rather than predicting the future perfectly.

4\. Is the EMH Still Useful?

Even if firms like Citadel prove the market isn't _perfectly_ efficient, proponents like Eugene Fama argue the EMH is still a useful model.

- Studies from Morningstar consistently show that most active fund managers fail to beat passive index funds over the long term, suggesting the market is "efficient enough" for most people.

can i win with past data

Winning in trading with past data is possible, but it is not as simple as finding a pattern that worked once and repeating it. In financial markets, using past data to predict the future is known as **backtesting**, and it is the foundation of almost all quantitative and systematic trading.

1\. The Core Philosophy: "History Rhymes"

The premise of using past data is that market participants are human (or programmed by humans), and their collective psychological reactions to events often create recurring patterns.

- **Trend Following**: Identifying long-term upward or downward trajectories based on historical price movement.
- **Mean Reversion**: Betting that prices will eventually return to their historical average after an extreme move.
- **Support & Resistance**: Using historical price points where the market previously "bounced" or "stalled" to predict future turning points.

2\. How to Use Data Without "Cheating" Yourself

The biggest danger is **overfitting**—creating a strategy so perfectly tuned to the past that it fails the moment the market changes. To truly "win," you must follow a rigorous process:

- **In-Sample vs. Out-of-Sample Testing**: Train your strategy on one set of historical data (e.g., 2015–2020) and then test it on "unseen" data (e.g., 2021–2024) to see if it still works.
- **Walk-Forward Analysis**: Continually re-testing and moving your data windows forward to adapt to changing "market regimes".
- **Account for "Black Swans"**: A strategy that made money for 10 years can be wiped out in one day by an event it hasn't seen before, like the COVID-19 crash or the 2008 financial crisis.

3\. The Limits of Past Data

While historical data provides a "map," it is not a crystal ball.

- **Look-Ahead Bias**: A common mistake where a backtest accidentally uses information from the "future" (e.g., executing a trade at the daily low price, which was only known after the day ended).
- **Market Evolution**: Strategies that worked in the 1990s often fail today because modern trading firms have already identified those patterns and traded them into "efficiency".
- **Execution Reality**: Backtests often ignore **slippage** (the difference between the price you want and the price you get) and **commissions**, which can turn a winning historical strategy into a losing real-world one.

4\. Modern Tools for Data Trading

To compete, you can use specialized libraries and platforms that handle massive historical datasets:

- **Python Libraries**: Use `Pandas` for data manipulation, `Backtrader` or `Zipline` for simulations, and `Scikit-learn` or `XGBoost` for machine learning models.
- **API Providers**: Services like [Finage](https://finage.co.uk/blog/how-to-backtest-your-trading-strategies-using-historical-stock-data-apis--68226205937e360d4b16c4e8) or [Financial Modeling Prep](https://site.financialmodelingprep.com/education/financial-analysis/Backtesting-Investment-Strategies-with-Historical-Data-A-Guide-to-Smarter-Investing) provide clean, structured historical data that accounts for stock splits and dividends.

**✅ Verdict**: You can win with past data if you use it to find a **statistical edge** rather than a "guaranteed" prediction. The goal is to be right 55-60% of the time over hundreds of trades, not 100% of the time on one trade.

how effective is backtesting, are they already exploited enough by big firms

5:33 pm

Backtesting remains a fundamental tool, but it is better understood as a "sanity check" rather than a prediction of profit. While big firms have indeed exploited the most obvious historical patterns, they have not—and cannot—exhaust all opportunities.

1\. Effectiveness: The Reality Gap

Backtesting is effective for proving a strategy has a **statistical edge**, but it often fails in live trading because it simulates a "perfect" world. In reality, a strategy's performance typically drops by **20% to 50%** when moving from a backtest to a live environment.

- **What it's good for:** Identifying if a pattern (like mean reversion or trend following) has historically worked and defining your "worst-case scenario" (maximum drawdown).
- **Why it "lies":** Most retail backtests ignore **execution friction**. A strategy showing 15% annual returns can collapse to near zero once you factor in spreads, slippage, and transaction fees.
- **The Overfitting Trap:** If you tweak your rules until the past looks perfect, you are "fitting the noise" rather than a real signal. These strategies almost always fail when exposed to new data.

2\. Are Big Firms Exploiting Everything?

Big firms have largely "solved" high-frequency and large-cap historical patterns, leading to **alpha decay**—the erosion of a strategy's edge as more participants discover it. However, gaps remain for smaller players:

- **Crowded Signals:** Institutional alpha decays quickly (sometimes by 50% in just 3 days for simple strategies) because firms with multi-million dollar infrastructures execute them faster.
- **The Speed Gap:** Big firms fight over "MicroAlphas" that last milliseconds. If your strategy relies on 1-hour or daily charts, you aren't really competing with their HFT algorithms.
- **Institutional Constraints:** Large funds cannot trade "micro-inefficiencies" (e.g., small-cap stocks or obscure crypto pairs) because their massive size would move the price too much. A strategy that makes $50,000 a year is "invisible" to a firm like Citadel but can be a win for you.

3\. How to Backtest Like a Pro

To avoid being "exploited" by the market's natural efficiency, your backtesting must be more rigorous:

- **Use Out-of-Sample Data:** Train your model on one period (e.g., 2018–2022) and test it on a period it has never seen (2023–2024).
- **Walk-Forward Analysis:** Instead of one static test, continually "roll" your training and testing windows forward to see if the strategy adapts to different market regimes (e.g., bull vs. bear markets).
- **Include Realistic Costs:** Manually subtract at least 0.1% to 0.5% per trade for slippage and commissions, even if your software says "zero fees".
- **Check for Survivorship Bias:** Ensure your historical data includes companies that went bankrupt; if you only test on stocks that still exist today, your results will be artificially high.

**The Bottom Line:** Backtesting isn't about finding a "money printer"; it's about **exploration**—finding where your strategy is fragile before you risk real money.

how effective is backtesting if you limit auto trading e.g. you trade once a day or a month

When you limit your trading frequency to once a day or once a month, backtesting actually becomes **more reliable** than it is for high-frequency strategies.

Lowering the frequency shifts the challenge from **technical execution** (speed) to **strategic insight** (logic).

1\. Why it’s MORE Effective

- **Reduced "Noise":** High-frequency data is filled with random "micro-noise" caused by algorithm battles. Daily or monthly data reflects broader economic trends and corporate fundamentals, which are more stable and easier to backtest accurately.
- **Execution Buffer:** If you trade once a month, a small delay in execution (slippage) of 0.1% barely affects your annual return. If you trade 100 times a day, that same 0.1% will bankrupt you.
- **Lower Costs:** Your "drag" from commissions and spreads is minimal. This makes the gap between your backtest results and your live results much smaller.

2\. Why it’s TRICKIER

- **Smaller Sample Size:** If you trade once a month, a 10-year backtest only gives you **120 data points**. Statistically, this is very "thin." One or two lucky trades (like being in the market during a random 20% jump) can make a bad strategy look like a genius one.
- **Increased Risk of "Black Swans":** When you hold a position for a month, you are exposed to "gap risk"—major news events that happen overnight or over the weekend when you can't exit.
- **Opportunity Cost:** If your monthly "signal" is wrong, you are stuck in a losing trade for 30 days while the rest of the market moves.

3\. Comparison of Backtesting Effectiveness

| Feature               | Intraday (High Freq)         | Daily/Monthly (Low Freq)                |
| --------------------- | ---------------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| **Data Quality**      | Critical (tick data needed)  | Simple (OHLC data is fine)              |
| **Slippage Impact**   | High (can ruin the strategy) | Low (negligible)                        |
| **Statistical Power** | High (thousands of trades)   | **Low (few trades)**                    |
| **Big Firm Rivalry**  | Extreme (they will beat you) | **Lower (they can't "speed" past you)** |

4\. The "Big Firm" Angle

At these lower frequencies, you are no longer competing with **HFT (High-Frequency Trading)** bots. You are competing with **Asset Managers** and **Hedge Funds**.

- They _do_ use backtesting for monthly rebalancing, but they are often slowed down by "Institutional Inertia."
- Because you are small, you can enter and exit a monthly position in a mid-cap stock in seconds. A big firm might take three days to build that same position without moving the price. **This is your "win" condition.**

How to make a Daily/Monthly Backtest "Real":

1. **Test over 20+ years:** Since you have so few trades, you need to see how the strategy performed in the 2000 dot-com bubble, the 2008 crash, and the 2020 spike.
2. **Focus on Drawdown:** Since you're holding longer, you need to know: "Can I stomach a 25% drop in the middle of the month without panicking?"
3. **Monte Carlo Simulation:** Run your backtest results through a "shuffler" to see if the strategy relies on the _order_ of events or if it’s actually robust.
