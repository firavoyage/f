The 1989 Tiananmen Square crackdown split modern Chinese history into two distinct eras, shifting the country from an open period of political experimentation to a hyper-controlled, economically driven superpower.

Here is how China changed before and after the event:

The Political Climate

- **Before**: The 1980s were a period of political openness. Top leaders like Hu Yaobang and Zhao Ziyang openly debated democratic reforms, freedom of the press, and legal separations between the ruling Party and the government.
- **After**: Political reform was permanently frozen. The Communist Party consolidated total control, purged reform-minded leaders, and established a zero-tolerance policy for organized political dissent.

Ideology and Education

- **Before**: Intellectuals and university students heavily embraced Western philosophy, democratic ideals, and global cultural exchanges. Marxism was openly questioned on college campuses.
- **After**: The government launched a massive Patriotic Education Campaign. Textbooks were rewritten to emphasize nationalism, loyalty to the state, and warnings against "Western ideological infiltration."

The Social Contract

- **Before**: Citizens frequently held public demonstrations to complain about inflation, official corruption, and a lack of personal freedom.
- **After**: A new, unspoken social contract emerged. The state guaranteed rapid economic growth, material wealth, and consumer freedom, provided citizens accepted total political compliance.

Media and Information Control

- **Before**: State newspapers were experimenting with independent journalism. During the 1989 protests, state media journalists even marched in solidarity, briefly broadcasting the truth.
- **After**: Censorship became absolute. The state centralized all news outlets, leading to the eventual creation of the "Great Firewall"—the world's most sophisticated digital censorship and surveillance apparatus.

International Relations

- **Before**: China enjoyed warm, strengthening ties with Western democracies. The US and Europe viewed China as an ally against the Soviet Union and a promising partner for global integration.
- **After**: Western nations imposed strict arms embargoes and economic sanctions. While economic ties eventually recovered, the crackdown permanently damaged Western trust and cemented long-term ideological rivalry.
