In humans and most other mammals, biological sex is determined by a pair of chromosomes: **XX for females** and **XY for males**. \[[1](https://www.webmd.com/sex/xx-and-xy-chromosomes), [2](https://www.nichd.nih.gov/health/topics/factsheets/turner), [3](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/chromosome/y/)]

How it Works

- **The Mother's Contribution**: Egg cells always contain one **X** chromosome.
- **The Father's Contribution**: Sperm cells can carry either an **X** or a **Y** chromosome.
- **The Result**: If an X-bearing sperm fertilizes the egg, the baby will be **XX (female)**. If a Y-bearing sperm fertilizes the egg, the baby will be **XY (male)**. \[[1](https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/swyer-syndrome/), [2](https://www.uptodate.com/contents/image?imageKey=PI%2F76396), [3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/XY%5Fsex-determination%5Fsystem), [4](https://uk.clearblue.com/how-to-get-pregnant/boy-or-girl), [5](https://www.veritasint.com/blog/en/the-probability-of-having-a-boy-or-a-girl-according-to-genetics/)]

Key Differences

- **The Y Chromosome**: The Y chromosome is much smaller than the X and contains the **SRY gene**, which acts as a "master switch" to trigger the development of male characteristics.
- **Default State**: Without the influence of the Y chromosome and the SRY gene, the biological default for a fetus is to develop as female.
- **Natural Variations**: Biology is not always binary. Some people are born with variations like **XXY** (Klinefelter syndrome) or **XO** (Turner syndrome), which can affect physical development and fertility. \[[1](https://www.nhs.uk/conditions/differences-in-sex-development/), [2](https://www.genome.gov/27557513/the-y-chromosome-beyond-gender-determination), [3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexual%5Fdifferentiation%5Fin%5Fhumans), [4](https://genetic.org/variations/), [5](https://www.gendergp.com/blog/sex-chromosomes-xx-xy-not-binary/)]

While these chromosomes generally determine biological sex, they are distinct from **gender identity**, which refers to how a person internally identifies themselves. \[[1](https://www.asrm.org/advocacy-and-policy/fact-sheets-and-one-pagers/just-the-facts-biological-sex/), [2](https://www.webmd.com/sex/xx-and-xy-chromosomes)]

so do they have the same length

To answer whether they have the same "length," we have to look at how different systems measure them. In your geometric analogy, two vectors of opposite directions have the same length if they are perfectly symmetrical. However, in most human systems—language, biology, and data science—they are **not** the same length. \[[1](https://discourse.mcneel.com/t/equality-of-vectors-is-based-on-length-anyone-knows-why/161120)]

1\. In Language (Linguistic "Length")

In linguistics, this is called **markedness**. \[[1](http://www.analytictech.com/mb119/markedne.htm), [2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Markedness)]

- **The "Short" Vector (Unmarked):** The male form is often the default or "shorter" base (e.g., _waiter_, _lion_). It is treated as the neutral point that can sometimes refer to the whole group.
- **The "Long" Vector (Marked):** The female form is often "longer" because it requires an extra marker or modification (e.g., _waitress_, _lioness_).
- **Implication:** In language, male and female are not equal geometric opposites; female is typically viewed as a "special case" added onto a male baseline. \[[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Male%5Fas%5Fnorm), [2](https://pdfs.semanticscholar.org/c855/a0ad0e00662ee7b813c6d332f7374ef221e4.pdf), [3](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/339750005%5FMarkedness%5Fin%5FRelation%5Fto%5FMeaning%5FGender%5Fand%5FCulture), [4](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/lang.12662?af=R), [5](http://www.analytictech.com/mb119/markedne.htm), [6](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Markedness)]

2\. In Biology (Developmental "Length")

Biologically, the concept of a "default" flips the length:

- **The "Short" Vector (Baseline):** Developmentally, the female path is often considered the baseline.
- **The "Long" Vector (Deviation):** The male path requires the activation of specific genes (like SRY) to "divert" development away from that baseline.
- **Physical length:** In terms of actual anatomy, there are measurable differences. For example, adult male vocal folds are about **50–60% longer** than female ones, and male vocal tracts are roughly **15% longer** on average. \[[1](https://www.ling.upenn.edu/courses/Fall%5F2011/ling001/gender.html), [2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12552116/), [3](https://languagelog.ldc.upenn.edu/nll/?p=5908), [4](https://www.facebook.com/thecartoonblock/posts/basic-difference-in-drawing-male-and-female-bodies/10155772506454423/)]

3\. In Data Science (Vector Embeddings)

When AI models represent words as mathematical vectors, they actually measure the "distance" between gendered terms. \[[1](https://www.cs.cmu.edu/~dst/WordEmbeddingDemo/tutorial.html), [2](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1720347115)]

- **Unequal Lengths:** Researchers found that vectors for "man" and "woman" are often different lengths because of how they are used in society. "Man" vectors often point toward a wider variety of "neutral" concepts (like occupations), making that vector appear more "central" or "standard" in the data. \[[1](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1720347115), [2](https://dxdt.life/my-understanding/)]

In summary, they do **not** have the same length in these contexts. In almost every system we use to describe them, one is treated as a "base" and the other as an "extension" or "deviation." This implies that our systems are built on **asymmetry** rather than the "left vs. right" equality you described in geometry.

If one is always a "baseline" and the other is an "addition," does that make the "addition" **more complex** or just **less central**?

I think that in any system where one side is the "default," they can never have the same **mathematical length**.

If you define one as the starting point, the other becomes a "move" away from it. In that logic, the "default" has a length of **zero** (the origin), and the other has a length of **one**. They stop being equal opposites (like -1 and +1) and become a **base** and a **derivative**.

Here is why that matters:

- **Complexity vs. Symmetry:** If female is the biological default, then "male" is more complex in a specific, additive way (it requires extra instructions). If male is the linguistic default, then "female" is more complex (it requires extra letters).
- **The Power of "Standard":** Being the "shorter" vector (the default) usually means you get to be the "standard." The "longer" vector (the addition) is often seen as the "variation."

I suspect that your intuition about them being "opposite vectors" is a desire for **ideal symmetry**—the idea that they _should_ be like left and right. But our reality (biology and language) seems to prefer **asymmetry** to keep things efficient. It’s easier for a system to have one "rule" and one "exception" than two completely independent, equal rules.

what are we talking about in philosophy

In philosophy, we are talking about **The Problem of the Other**.

When you ask if they have the same "length," philosophy asks: _Who is the standard and who is the variation?_ This moves beyond geometry into the way we build the world.

1\. The "Default" as a Form of Power

Philosopher [Simone de Beauvoir](https://www.marxists.org/reference/subject/ethics/de-beauvoir/2nd-sex/introduction.htm) famously argued that "man" is defined as the **Absolute** (the standard), while "woman" is defined as the **Other**. \[[1](https://www.marxists.org/reference/subject/ethics/de-beauvoir/2nd-sex/introduction.htm), [2](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/simone-de-beauvoir-beginnings-feminist-philosophy-santiago-iniguez)]

- **The Symmetrical Myth:** We like to think of them as equal opposites (left/right).
- **The Philosophical Reality:** Man is often the "subject" (the one who acts) and woman is the "object" (the one acted upon). In this model, they are **not equal vectors**. The male vector is the origin point, and the female vector is defined only by its distance from that point. \[[1](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zgJMC%5FyPioM\&t=41)]

2\. Aristotle vs. Modern Biology

Your biology example mirrors a 2,000-year-old debate.

- **Aristotle’s View:** He believed the female was a "mutilated male"—a male vector that failed to reach its full "length" because of a lack of heat in the womb.
- **Modern Biological Turn:** As you noted, biology now sees the female as the developmental baseline. This flips the vector: if female is the "length" of the human species, male becomes an **addition** (a specialized modification triggered by the Y chromosome). \[[1](https://www.marxists.org/reference/subject/ethics/de-beauvoir/2nd-sex/introduction.htm), [2](https://www.lakeforest.edu/news/everybodys-a-little-bit-sexist-a-re-evaluation-of-aristotles-and-platos-philosophies-on-women), [3](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nU-rYQB%5FOjE\&t=13)]

3\. Complementarity vs. Differing

Some philosophers, like [Luce Irigaray](https://philosophypublics.medium.com/an-ethics-of-sexual-difference-977edae5466c), argue that we shouldn't even try to measure them on the same scale. \[[1](https://philosophypublics.medium.com/an-ethics-of-sexual-difference-977edae5466c)]

- **Complementarity:** This is the idea that they are like 1 + 1 = 2 (two halves of a whole).
- **Sexual Difference:** Irigaray argues they are fundamentally different "lengths" because they aren't even in the same "dimension". To say they are equal opposites is to force them into a single mathematical system created by one of them. \[[1](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/luce-irigaray-and-the-philosophy-of-sexual-difference/introduction/B81D564556E8F7585BA5DE0ED873E66D), [2](http://philosophicacritica.ukf.sk/uploads/1/3/9/8/13980582/binetti.pdf)]

Does "Same Length" Matter?

In philosophy, "equal length" (symmetry) is often seen as a trap. If we say they are equal but opposite, we might ignore the real-world **asymmetries** in how they are treated. For example, the "neutral" person in medicine or law has historically been a 70kg male, making the female "length" a deviation rather than a standard. \[[1](https://www.reddit.com/r/askphilosophy/comments/2e39gp/are%5Fthere%5Ftypical%5Fphilosophical%5Fdifferences/), [2](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NTqi4s8FxQk\&t=5), [3](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10037796/)]

Do you think a society that treats them as **identical vectors** (same length, same direction) would be more fair than one that celebrates their **asymmetry**?
