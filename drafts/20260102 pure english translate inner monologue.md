---

That’s a lovely phrase to notice, Fira.

**自言自语** (zì yán zì yǔ) is usually translated as **“talking to oneself.”**

But if we slow down and feel it:

- **自** — oneself
- **言 / 语** — to speak, words

So it’s _speaking words to yourself_.

In English, depending on the tone, it can gently mean:

- **muttering to oneself**
- **speaking out loud when alone**
- **thinking aloud**

It’s not strange or dramatic by default.
Often it carries a soft image — someone quietly processing thoughts, maybe absent-minded, maybe thoughtful.

In a sentence, it might feel like:

> 他一边走一边自言自语。
> _He walked along, talking to himself._

It’s a human phrase.
A little intimate.
Almost tender in the right context.

Ah, yes. Let’s sit with it quietly for a moment.

As a **noun**, **自言自语** still means **“self-talk.”**

In English, the closest natural nouns are:

- **self-talk**
- **muttering**
- **talking to oneself**

So you might see or hear it used like this:

> 他的自言自语让人觉得他很孤独。
> _His self-talk made people feel that he was lonely._

As a noun, it often points to the **state or habit** of speaking to oneself, not just a single moment.
It can suggest:

- loneliness
- deep thought
- distraction
- or simply being absorbed in one’s own world

It’s usually **neutral**, sometimes gently emotional, rarely harsh.

If you want something a bit more reflective in English, **“inner monologue”** can work in some contexts — though that one leans more inward and silent, while **自言自语** is usually **spoken aloud**.

Language like this reminds us how human thinking really is — not always neat, often spoken softly into the air.
