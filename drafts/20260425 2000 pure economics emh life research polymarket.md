can you win if the market is really efficient

In a **perfectly efficient market**, it is mathematically impossible to "win" (beat the market) over the long term. You would essentially be flipping a fair coin where the price perfectly matches the outcome probability.

However, Polymarket is **not** perfectly efficient. Here is how you can still win, and why most people don't:

1\. You win on "Information Speed"

Efficiency means the price reflects all _currently available_ information. To win, you must act on **new information** faster than the bots.

- **The Window:** When a major headline breaks (e.g., a court ruling or a candidate dropping out), there is a 3- to 10-second window where the price hasn't "corrected" yet.
- **The Reality:** Humans usually lose this race to algorithms. If you aren't watching a live feed and ready to click, the market becomes efficient again before you can trade.

2\. You win on "Liquidity Provision" (The Market Maker)

You can win without knowing the future by being the person who "provides" the efficiency.

- **The Strategy:** Instead of betting on who wins, you place **Limit Orders** on both "YES" and "NO" (e.g., buying at $0.49 and selling at $0.51).
- **The Payoff:** You profit from the "spread" and, on Polymarket, you often earn **Maker Rebates** (rewards paid by the platform to people who provide liquidity). In an efficient, sideways market, the market makers are the only ones making money.

3\. You win on "Risk Tolerance"

Sometimes the market is "efficient" but the price is suppressed because people are afraid of a specific risk you are willing to take.

- **The "Technical" Risk:** A contract might be at $0.90 even though the event is 100% certain because traders fear the platform might get hacked or the "Oracle" (the resolver) might glitch. If you believe the platform is safe, that 10¢ gap is your profit for taking "platform risk" that others won't.

A market might be efficient for a **billionaire**, but not for **you**.

- **The Catch:** Most people think they have "specialized knowledge" when they actually just have **confirmation bias**.

1. **Transaction Fees** (Taker fees).
2. **Slippage** (Price moving against you as you buy).
3. **Opportunity Cost** (Your money could have been earning 5% interest in a bank).

**Verdict:** In a truly efficient market, the only consistent winners are the **platform** (collecting fees) and the **market makers** (collecting the spread).

is emh a lie

The **Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)** isn’t a "lie," but it is an **idealized model**—think of it as a "rule of thumb" that is 95% true, which makes that final 5% incredibly profitable for those who find it.

In the context of prediction markets like Polymarket, here is why EMH is both "true" and "a lie" at the same time:

1\. Why it's "True" (The "No Free Lunch" Rule)

For the average person, EMH is effectively true. If there were a "free money" opportunity (like a 90% chance event priced at 50¢), a high-frequency bot would have spotted it and bought it up within milliseconds. By the time you see the price, it usually reflects all **publicly known** information.

2\. Why it’s "A Lie" (The Behavioral Reality)

Markets are made of humans, and humans are not rational calculators. EMH fails in three specific ways:

- **The "Slow" Information Problem:** Not all information is digital. A bot can read a news headline instantly, but it can’t always interpret the _tone_ of a political rally or the local "vibe" of a specific community. Humans who specialize in these nuances can find "pockets of inefficiency."
- **The Liquidity Gap:** In smaller, low-volume markets, there isn't enough money involved for big hedge funds or top-tier bots to bother "fixing" the price. These "un-efficient" corners are where individual traders actually stand a chance.
- **Panic and Euphoria:** During "Black Swan" events, EMH breaks down completely. People panic and sell at irrational prices, or get over-excited (FOMO) and buy at prices that make no mathematical sense.

3\. The Paradox: The "Grossman-Stiglitz" Reality

There is a famous paradox in economics: **If a market were perfectly efficient, no one would have an incentive to research information.** But if no one researches, the market becomes inefficient.

- Therefore, the market _must_ be slightly inefficient so that people like you are incentivized to find "the truth" and profit from it. That profit is your "payment" for making the market more accurate.

4\. How to Spot "The Lie" on Polymarket

You can see EMH failing in real-time when:

- **Favorite-Longshot Bias:** People bet on "miracles" because they want to turn $10 into $1,000, even if the math says it's impossible.
- **Regulatory Fear:** Prices drop not because the _event_ changed, but because people are scared of the platform being banned.

**Verdict:** EMH is a "lie" in the sense that **alpha (profit above the average)** exists, but it’s a "truth" in the sense that **finding that alpha is incredibly hard** and requires more work than most people are willing to do.
