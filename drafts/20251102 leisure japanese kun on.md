Of course! This is a fundamental concept in learning Japanese. Here’s a detailed comparison of **Kun'yomi** and **On'yomi** readings.

### The Core Idea: A Simple Analogy

Think of a single kanji character as a stem cell.

- **Kun'yomi (訓読み)** is its **Native Japanese** reading. It's like the stem cell becoming a unique, specialized cell for a specific Japanese word.
- **On'yomi (音読み)** is its **Chinese-Derived** reading. It's like the stem cell being used in a compound with other cells, often losing its individual identity to create a new, more complex word.

---

### Comparison Table

| Feature              | Kun'yomi (訓読み)                                                       | On'yomi (音読み)                                                             |
| :------------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Origin**           | Native Japanese words.                                                  | Imported from ancient Chinese pronunciation.                                 |
| **Meaning**          | The kanji _is_ the word; it has a full meaning on its own.              | The kanji represents a concept; often needs another kanji to form a word.    |
| **Usage**            | **Stand-alone words** or with **Okurigana** (hiragana suffixes).        | Almost always in **compound words** (2+ kanji together).                     |
| **Sound/Feel**       | Often longer, softer, more syllabic. (e.g., `か` - ka, `はし` - hashi). | Often shorter, sharper, more consonant-heavy. (e.g., `カ` - ka, `シ` - shi). |
| **Number per Kanji** | Often one or two common readings.                                       | Can have multiple, from different Chinese dynasties/regions.                 |
| **Learning Hint**    | Think of the **meaning** of the word.                                   | Think of the **sound** in a compound.                                        |

---

### Detailed Breakdown with Examples

#### Kun'yomi (The Native Japanese Reading)

- **Purpose:** To represent a native Japanese word that already existed before the kanji was imported from China. The kanji was assigned to that word based on its meaning.
- **How to Spot It:**
  1.  When the kanji is **alone** and means a full word.
      - Example: **水** (みず, _mizu_) - "water"
      - Example: **山** (やま, _yama_) - "mountain"
  2.  When it's followed by **Hiragana (Okurigana)**, usually for verb conjugations or adjectives.
      - Example: **食**べる (たべる, _taberu_) - "to eat"
      - Example: **高**い (たかい, _takai_) - "high, expensive"
      - Example: **嬉**しい (うれしい, _ureshii_) - "happy"

#### On'yomi (The Chinese-Derived Reading)

- **Purpose:** To approximate the original Chinese sound of the character. These readings are used to create compound words, much like Latin and Greek roots are used in English (e.g., _aqua_+_duct_, _tele_+_scope_).
- **How to Spot It:**
  1.  Almost always in **Kanji Compounds** (熟語, _jukugo_), where two or more kanji are stuck together.
      - Example: **水** (スイ, _sui_) + **道** (ドウ, _dō_) = **水道** (すいどう, _suidō_) - "water supply"
      - Example: **火** (カ, _ka_) + **山** (ザン, _san_) = **火山** (かざん, _kazan_) - "volcano" (fire-mountain)
      - Example: **電** (デン, _den_) + **車** (シャ, _sha_) = **電車** (でんしゃ, _densha_) - "train" (electric-car)

---

### A Practical Example: The Kanji 人 (Person)

This kanji perfectly illustrates both readings and why they are necessary.

- **Kun'yomi:** **ひと** (_hito_)

  - Used when the kanji is alone: **人** (ひと) - "person"
  - Used with okurigana: **日本人** (にほん**じん**, \*Nihon**jin\***) - "Japanese person" (Here, 人 uses its on'yomi _jin_ because it's part of a compound with 日本, but the _jin_ is a kun'yomi-derived reading for this specific compound. This is an exception that proves the rule!)

- **On'yomi:** **ジン** (_jin_) and **ニン** (_nin_)
  - **ジン** (_jin_): **三人** (さん**にん**, \*san**nin\***) - "three people"
  - **ニン** (_nin_): **人間** (**にん**げん, **\*nin**gen\*) - "human being"
  - Used in compounds: **美人** (び**じん**, \*bi**jin\***) - "beautiful person" (beauty-person)

> **Note:** The fact that 人 has _two_ common on'yomi (_jin_ and _nin_) is a classic beginner's challenge, but you learn which one to use based on the specific word.

### How to Approach Learning Them

1.  **Learn Vocabulary, Not Just Readings:** Don't memorize "Kanji X has kun'yomi A, B, C and on'yomi D, E, F." This is inefficient. Instead, learn **words**.

    - Learn the word **大きい** (おおきい, _ōkii_) - "big". Now you know the kun'yomi おお for 大.
    - Learn the word **大学** (だいがく, _daigaku_) - "university". Now you know the on'yomi ダイ for 大.

2.  **Look for Patterns:** As you learn, you'll see patterns. The on'yomi ショウ (_shō_) appears in many kanji like 生、勝、商、章. Recognizing these sounds helps you guess the reading of new compounds.

3.  **Accept the Ambiguity:** Sometimes, a word will break the "rules," or the "correct" reading is based on context. The famous word **今日** can be read as **きょう** (_kyō_, kun-based) or **こんにち** (_konnichi_, on-based). This is why learning words in context is crucial.

**In summary:** **Kun'yomi** is for the native, stand-alone words. **On'yomi** is for the Chinese-derived, compound words. Mastering the balance between them is the key to unlocking literacy in Japanese.

---

That's an excellent and very observant question! It gets to a common teaching technique.

**The short answer is: No, that's not a rule, but it's a very common and helpful learning aid.**

In actual Japanese writing, readings are almost **never** explicitly written next to the kanji in daily life. However, in textbooks, dictionaries, and study guides, authors often use this visual convention to help learners quickly distinguish between the two types of readings.

Here’s the breakdown:

### The Common Learning Convention

- **Kun'yomi is often shown in Hiragana.**

  - Example: **食**べる (た**べる**)
  - Example: **水** (みず)

- **On'yomi is often shown in Katakana.**
  - Example: **食事** (ショク**ジ**)
  - Example: **水道** (スイ**ドウ**)

**Why do they do this?**
It's a fantastic visual cue. When you see the katakana, it immediately signals "This is the Chinese-derived, compound-word reading." When you see the hiragana, it signals "This is the native Japanese, often stand-alone word reading."

---

### The Reality in Real Japanese Writing

In standard Japanese text (books, newspapers, websites), you will **not** see this distinction used to display readings. The readings are implied by the word itself.

However, there is one crucial situation where readings _are_ written out: **Furigana**.

**Furigana** are the small kana characters written above or next to a kanji to show its pronunciation. They are used in children's books, manga, and sometimes for rare or difficult kanji in adult texts.

**And the rule for furigana is: They are _almost always written in Hiragana_, regardless of whether it's a Kun'yomi or On'yomi reading.**

Let's look at examples from real Japanese:

| Kanji Word | Reading Type | How it's Written in Furigana |
| :--------- | :----------- | :--------------------------- |
| **食べる** | Kun'yomi     | **た**べる (Hiragana)        |
| **大きい** | Kun'yomi     | **おお**きい (Hiragana)      |
| **水道**   | On'yomi      | **すいどう** (Hiragana)      |
| **学生**   | On'yomi      | **がくせい** (Hiragana)      |

As you can see, in practice, **hiragana is the universal script for showing pronunciation**, even for on'yomi readings which are historically linked to katakana.

### Summary

- **Learning Aid:** Yes, using **hiragana for Kun** and **katakana for On** is a widespread and useful trick in textbooks and study materials.
- **Real-World Writing:** No. In actual Japanese text, you don't see the readings written out unless necessary.
- **Furigana Rule:** When readings _are_ provided (as furigana), they are **almost exclusively written in Hiragana** for both Kun'yomi and On'yomi.

So, your observation is spot-on for the learning context! It's a great way to train your brain, but just remember that when you graduate to reading real Japanese, you'll see hiragana doing all the work for pronunciation hints.
