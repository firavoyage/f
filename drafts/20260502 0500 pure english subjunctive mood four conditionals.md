english subjunctive mood

The **English subjunctive mood** is <mark style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);">a grammatical form used to express things that are not certain facts, such as wishes, suggestions, demands, and hypothetical scenarios</mark>. Unlike other moods like the indicative (which states facts) or the imperative (which gives commands), the subjunctive describes imagined or unreal situations. \[[1](https://www.grammarly.com/blog/grammar/subjunctive/), [2](https://preply.com/en/blog/english-subjunctive/), [3](https://www.scribbr.co.uk/verb/the-subjunctive-mood/), [4](https://www.masterclass.com/articles/subjunctive-mood), [5](https://quillbot.com/blog/verbs/subjunctive-mood/)]

How to Form the Subjunctive

Modern English rarely has distinct verb inflections for the subjunctive, making it difficult to notice. It primarily appears in two forms: \[[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English%5Fsubjunctive), [2](https://www.merriam-webster.com/grammar/getting-in-the-subjunctive-mood)]

- **Present Subjunctive (Mandative):** Uses the **bare form** of the verb (the infinitive without "to") for all subjects, including the third-person singular.
  - _Indicative:_ She **stays** home.
  - _Subjunctive:_ I suggest that she **stay** home.
  - _With "to be":_ It is essential that he **be** here.
- **Past Subjunctive (Irrealis):** Primarily uses **"were"** instead of "was" for all subjects to indicate a situation is contrary to fact.
  - _Example:_ If I **were** you, I wouldn't do that.
  - _Example:_ I wish she **were** here. \[[1](https://langeek.co/en/grammar/course/40/subjunctive), [2](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/microsoft-365-life-hacks/writing/understanding-the-subjunctive-mood), [3](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=adev01XL%5F30), [4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English%5Fsubjunctive), [5](https://www.grammarly.com/blog/grammar/subjunctive/), [6](https://preply.com/en/blog/english-subjunctive/), [7](https://www.merriam-webster.com/grammar/getting-in-the-subjunctive-mood), [8](https://www.scribbr.co.uk/verb/the-subjunctive-mood/)]

Common Triggers

The subjunctive is usually "triggered" by specific verbs, adjectives, or conjunctions in a main clause. \[[1](https://www.busuu.com/en/english/subjunctive-mood), [2](https://helpingyoulearnspanish.com/tips-for-learning-the-spanish-subjunctive-mood/)]

Fixed Expressions

Several "fossilized" phrases in English still use the subjunctive: \[[1](https://www.merriam-webster.com/grammar/getting-in-the-subjunctive-mood), [2](https://www.cambridge.org/elt/blog/2022/04/28/if-english-have-a-subjunctive-what-be-it/)]

- **"God save the Queen/King"**
- **"Suffice it to say"**
- **"Be that as it may"**
- **"Heaven forbid"**
- **"Come what may"** \[[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English%5Fsubjunctive), [2](https://www.englishclub.com/grammar/subjunctive.php), [3](https://www.busuu.com/en/english/subjunctive-mood), [4](https://www.scribbr.co.uk/verb/the-subjunctive-mood/), [5](https://quillbot.com/blog/verbs/subjunctive-mood/)]

Subjunctive vs. Alternatives

In many modern contexts, especially in British English, the subjunctive is often replaced by **"should + infinitive"** or even the standard indicative form to sound less formal. \[[1](https://www.scribbr.com/verbs/subjunctive-mood/), [2](https://www.bbc.co.uk/worldservice/learningenglish/grammar/learnit/learnitv105.shtml), [3](https://www.scribbr.co.uk/verb/the-subjunctive-mood/)]

- _Subjunctive:_ It's important that he **arrive** on time.
- _Alternative:_ It's important that he **should arrive** on time.
- _Informal Indicative:_ It's important that he **arrives** on time. \[[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English%5Fsubjunctive), [2](https://www.bbc.co.uk/worldservice/learningenglish/grammar/learnit/learnitv105.shtml), [3](https://www.englishclub.com/grammar/subjunctive.php), [4](https://www.bbc.co.uk/worldservice/learningenglish/youmeus/learnit/learnitv105.shtml), [5](https://englishlessonsbrighton.co.uk/subjunctive-mood-form-use/)]

english conditionals

English conditionals are <mark style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);">complex sentences used to describe a condition (starting with "if" or "unless") and its result</mark>. They allow speakers to express real-life facts, likely future events, or completely hypothetical scenarios. \[[1](https://www.grammarly.com/blog/sentences/conditional-sentences/), [2](https://preply.com/en/blog/5-types-of-conditional-sentences-in-english/), [3](https://magoosh.com/ielts/the-four-types-of-conditionals-and-how-to-use-them/), [4](https://dictionary.cambridge.org/grammar/british-grammar/conditionals)]

The Four Main Types

English conditionals fall into four main categories based on probability and time: \[[1](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=veTo6eWJQrM\&pp=0gcJCfwAo7VqN5tD), [2](https://www.grammarly.com/blog/sentences/conditional-sentences/)]

- **Zero (Facts/Truths):** If + Present Simple, Present Simple (e.g., "If you heat ice, it melts").
- **First (Possible Future):** If + Present Simple, Will + Base Verb (e.g., "If it rains, I will stay home").
- **Second (Hypothetical/Unlikely):** If + Past Simple, Would + Base Verb (e.g., "If I won, I would travel").
- **Third (Impossible Past/Regret):** If + Past Perfect, Would have + Past Participle (e.g., "If they had prepared, they would have won"). \[[1](https://www.ef.edu/english-resources/english-grammar/conditional/), [2](https://www.ef.com/wwen/english-resources/english-grammar/conditional/), [3](https://oxfordhousebcn.com/en/4-conditionals-in-english-and-when-to-use-them/), [4](https://www.geeksforgeeks.org/ssc-banking/rules-of-conditional-sentences-with-examples/), [5](https://www.wallstreetenglish.com/exercises/how-to-use-conditionals-in-english-zero-first-second-third-and-mixed/), [6](https://learnenglish.britishcouncil.org/free-resources/grammar/b1-b2/conditionals-third-mixed), [7](https://www.grammarly.com/blog/sentences/conditional-sentences/)]

Variations and Usage

- **Mixed Conditionals:** Combine different times, like a past condition affecting the present (e.g., "If I had worked harder, I would be in a better job").
- **Alternatives:** "Unless" can replace "if...not," while "as long as" or "provided that" set specific conditions.
- **Inversion:** Formal alternatives exist, such as using "Had I known" instead of "If I had known". \[[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English%5Fconditional%5Fsentences), [2](https://learnenglishteens.britishcouncil.org/grammar/b1-b2-grammar/conditionals), [3](https://www.grammarly.com/blog/sentences/conditional-sentences/), [4](https://learnenglish.britishcouncil.org/free-resources/grammar/b1-b2/conditionals-third-mixed), [5](https://www.wallstreetenglish.com/exercises/how-to-use-conditionals-in-english-zero-first-second-third-and-mixed/), [6](https://preply.com/en/blog/5-types-of-conditional-sentences-in-english/)]

For further practice, you can find interactive exercises and detailed guides on sites like [British Council LearnEnglish](https://learnenglish.britishcouncil.org/free-resources/grammar/b1-b2/conditionals-zero-first-second) or [Grammarly](https://www.grammarly.com/blog/sentences/conditional-sentences/). \[[1](https://lan-portal.uob.edu.ly/mirror/ITUNE/Q4X0605038/sentence%5Fstructure-learnenglish-british%5Fcouncil.pdf), [2](https://edukatesingapore.com/how-to-learn-complex-sentence-structure-for-psle-english-fencing-method/), [3](https://teast.co/blog/grammar-sites)]

english conditionals

English conditional sentences <mark style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);">consist of two parts: a <strong>condition</strong> (the "if" clause) and a <strong>result</strong> (the main clause)</mark>. They are used to discuss possibilities, imaginary scenarios, and cause-and-effect relationships. \[[1](https://www.grammarly.com/blog/sentences/conditional-sentences/), [2](https://quillbot.com/blog/sentence-and-word-structure/conditional-sentences/), [3](https://preply.com/en/blog/5-types-of-conditional-sentences-in-english/)]

The Five Main Types

Grammarians typically divide conditionals into five types based on their timeframe and likelihood. \[[1](https://www.ef.edu/english-resources/english-grammar/conditional/), [2](https://preply.com/en/blog/5-types-of-conditional-sentences-in-english/)]

| Type \[[1](https://www.grammarly.com/blog/sentences/conditional-sentences/), [2](https://kseacademy.com/en/blog/grammar/intermediate/conditional-sentences/), [3](https://kseacademy.com/en/blog/grammar/intermediate/conditional-sentences/), [4](https://www.ef.edu/english-resources/english-grammar/conditional/)] | Function                           | Structure                                       | Example                                                  |
| ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Zero**                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               | General truths & scientific facts  | If + Present Simple, Present Simple             | "If you heat ice, it melts."                             |
| **First**                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              | Possible future events             | If + Present Simple, Will + Verb                | "If it rains, I will stay home."                         |
| **Second**                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                             | Unlikely/Hypothetical present      | If + Past Simple, Would + Verb                  | "If I won the lottery, I would travel."                  |
| **Third**                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              | Unreal past/Regrets                | If + Past Perfect, Would have + Past Participle | "If I had studied, I would have passed."                 |
| **Mixed**                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              | Past condition with present result | If + Past Perfect, Would + Verb                 | "If I had worked harder, I would have a better job now." |

Key Usage Rules

- **Clause Order**: You can swap the order of the clauses without changing the meaning. However, if the "if" clause comes first, you must use a **comma** before the result clause.
- **Unless**: The word "unless" can often replace "if... not" (e.g., "Unless you hurry, you'll miss it" means "If you don't hurry...").
- **"If I were"**: In formal English, "were" is used for all persons in the second conditional (e.g., "If I were you...") to indicate an unreal situation.
- **Formal Inversions**: In formal contexts, you can omit "if" by inverting the verb and subject (e.g., "Had I known" instead of "If I had known"). \[[1](https://www.grammarly.com/blog/sentences/conditional-sentences/), [2](https://kseacademy.com/en/blog/grammar/intermediate/conditional-sentences/), [3](https://www.busuu.com/en/english/conditional-sentences), [4](https://learnenglishteens.britishcouncil.org/grammar/b1-b2-grammar/conditionals), [5](https://www.madonna.edu/resources/writing-center/online-tutoring/Conditionals.pdf), [6](https://magoosh.com/ielts/the-four-types-of-conditionals-and-how-to-use-them/), [7](https://preply.com/en/blog/5-types-of-conditional-sentences-in-english/)]
