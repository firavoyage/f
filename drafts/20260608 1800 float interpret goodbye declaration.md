# グッバイ宣言 song meaning

The song **"Goodbye Sengen" (グッバイ宣言 / Goodbye Declaration)** by Chinozo is fundamentally <mark style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);">an <strong>anthem about embracing your true self, rejecting societal conformity, and finding freedom by shutting out the noise of the world</strong></mark>**.** Released in 2020 during the global pandemic, its themes of self-isolation heavily resonated with listeners worldwide. \[[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goodbye%5FSengen), [2](https://www.letras.com/chinozo/good-bye-declaration/english.html), [3](https://www.animatetimes.com/news/details.php?id=1658884320)]

Here is a breakdown of the specific meanings and themes within the song:

### 🏠 "Shut-in Absolute Justice" (引き籠り絶対ジャスティス)

A core phrase in the song is _"Hikikomori Zettai Justice"_. While "hikikomori" usually refers to a social recluse or shut-in with negative connotations, the song rebrands it as an act of defiance. Staying home is not framed as a sign of defeat, but as a deliberate choice to step away from a toxic, chaotic society. \[[1](https://www.letras.com/chinozo/good-bye-declaration/english.html)]

### 🌆 Saying Goodbye to Conformity

The "Goodbye" in the title is a declaration of independence from societal pressures: \[[1](https://www.letras.com/chinozo/good-bye-declaration/english.html)]

- **Rejecting "Common Sense":** The lyrics state that the city’s expectations, "common sense," and unsolicited advice hold no real meaning.
- **The "Puppet" Realization:** The protagonist describes realizing that their emotions were acting like a "marionette" (puppet). Stepping away allows them to cut those strings.
- **Dropping the Facade:** The lyrics switch fluidly between the masculine pronoun _"ore"_ and feminine pronoun _"watashi"_. This highlights a universal struggle with identity, suggesting the speaker is stripping away social labels to find their raw self. \[[1](https://www.letras.com/chinozo/good-bye-declaration/english.html), [2](https://www.animatetimes.com/news/details.php?id=1658884320)]

### 🌸 "Blooming Out of Season"

Instead of wasting energy trying to fit into a crowded, stressful city that causes endless anxiety, the protagonist chooses their bedroom as a sanctuary. The lyrics express a desire to "set my ego free" and "bloom out of season" in a safe place where shame and embarrassment do not exist. It asserts that people can still create, grow, and thrive beautifully on their own terms.

# 狂い咲く meaning

The Japanese word **狂い咲く (kuruizaku)** literally means <mark style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);"><strong>"to bloom out of season"</strong></mark>. \[[1](https://kanji.jepang.org/en/kosakata/%E7%8B%82%E3%81%84%E5%92%B2%E3%81%8F)]

Here is a breakdown of its components, literal meaning, and figurative uses.

### 🌸 Breakdown of the Word

- **狂い (kurui)**: Derived from the verb _kuruu_ (狂う), meaning to go mad, to get out of order, or to behave abnormally.
- **咲く (saku)**: A verb meaning to bloom or blossom. \[[1](https://jisho.org/search/%E7%8B%82%E3%81%84%E5%92%B2%E3%81%8F)]

When combined, they describe a plant's biological clock "going crazy," causing it to flower at the wrong time of the year (such as a cherry blossom tree blooming during a warm autumn week instead of spring). \[[1](https://www.niwa919.com/jumokui-blog/2021/12/3118/)]

### 📖 Meanings and Usage

- **Literal Meaning:** Off-season blooming.
  - _Example:_ 狂い咲きの桜 (Cherry blossoms blooming out of season).
- **Figurative Meaning:** An unexpected, late-stage comeback or a sudden resurgence of energy/popularity when something is otherwise in decline.
  - _Example:_ A veteran athlete or an older rock band suddenly topping the charts again after years of obscurity might be described as "blooming out of season." \[[1](https://www.weblio.jp/content/%E7%8B%82%E3%81%84%E5%92%B2%E3%81%8F)]

### 📝 Related Form

- **狂い咲き (kuruizaki):** This is the noun form of the word, meaning "an off-season bloom" or "a late comeback". \[[1](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%8B%82%E3%81%84%E5%92%B2%E3%81%8D), [2](https://mazii.net/en-US/search/word/jaen/%E7%8B%82%E3%81%84%E5%92%B2%E3%81%8D), [3](https://www.linguee.com/japanese-english/translation/%E7%8B%82%E3%81%84%E5%92%B2%E3%81%8F.html)]

## You said: metaphorical meaning

It describes someone or something achieving a massive burst of success, energy, or beauty at a time when everyone assumed their prime had already passed.

### 💡 Key Metaphorical Concepts

- **The Late-Bloomer Comeback:** A veteran athlete, aging artist, or older rock band suddenly topping the charts or winning championships again after years of obscurity.
- **A Second Youth:** An older person suddenly experiencing a burst of passion, romance, or vitality later in life.

- **狂い咲く老兵 (Kuruizaku rōhei):** "An old soldier blooming out of season" — A veteran who returns to the battlefield or arena and outperforms the younger generation.
- **人生の狂い咲き (Jinsei no kuruizaki):** "A late-stage blooming in life" — Achieving your greatest life success or finding true love in your twilight years.

## You said: does s change to z

<mark style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);"><span style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);">Yes, the </span><strong>"s"</strong> sound changes to a <strong>"z"</strong> sound</mark> due to a Japanese linguistic rule called **Rendaku (連濁)** or "sequential voicing."

Here is how the sound shift works in this specific word:

### 🔊 The Sound Shift

- The standalone verb for "to bloom" is **saku** (さく / 咲く).
- When attached to the end of **kurui** (狂い), the **"sa"** sound becomes voiced.
- **Kurui** + **saku** = **Kuruizaku** (くるいざく).

### 💡 Why Does This Happen?

Rendaku makes compound words easier and more fluid to pronounce in Japanese. Voicing the first consonant of the second word creates a smoother transition for your tongue and throat.

### 📝 Other Examples of "S" to "Z" Rendaku

This rule applies to many common Japanese compound words:

- **Hana** (flower) + **Saka** (slope) = **Hanazaka** (Flower slope)
- **Kasa** (umbrella) + **Aki** (autumn) → **Amagasa** (Rain umbrella - _Note: Ame changes to Ama here_)
- **Ki** (tree) + **Suki** (liking) = **Kizuki** (Notice / Awareness)

## You said: is r pronounced as l

<mark style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);"><span style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);">No, the Japanese </span><strong>"R"</strong> is not exactly pronounced as an <strong>"L"</strong></mark>. It is a unique sound that sits right between an English **R**, **L**, and **D**.

To an English speaker's ear, it often sounds like an "L" or a light "D," which is why this confusion happens.

### 👅 How to Make the Sound

- **The Technique:** Place the tip of your tongue against the roof of your mouth, just behind your front teeth (the alveolar ridge).
- **The Action:** Flick your tongue forward and down quickly, lightly tapping that ridge.
- **The Comparison:** It is identical to the **"tt"** or **"dd"** sound in the American English pronunciation of the words **"butter"** or **"ladder"** (this is called an alveolar tap).

### ❌ What it is NOT

- It is **not** an English **R**: Your lips do not bunch up, and your tongue does not curl backward.
- It is **not** a pure English **L**: Your tongue does not stay pressed against your teeth to let air escape out the sides.

### 🔊 Hearing it in "Kuruizaku"

When pronouncing the **ru** (る) in _kuruizaku_, try saying "ku-**du**-i-za-ku" or "ku-**lu**-i-za-ku" with a very soft, fast tap of the tongue.

# sakura japanese words

In Japanese, the core word for <mark style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);">cherry blossom</mark> is **Sakura (桜 / さくら)**, which is linguistically derived from the verb _saku_ (咲く), meaning "to bloom" or "to smile". Because cherry blossoms hold immense cultural significance, symbolizing the transient, fleeting nature of life (_mono no aware_), the Japanese language has developed **over 70 specialized words** to describe their different stages, movements, and seasonal beauty. \[[1](https://tandem.net/blog/japanese-words-cherry-blossom), [2](https://sakura.co/blog/vocabulary-workshop-best-words-for-cherry-blossoms?srsltid=AfmBOopx3ZyPI%5FCicx77Wip%5F1ho5wc4xxxVYZ7qNGQjJhfw6SRNGNZv%5F), [3](https://www.gotokyo.org/en/story/guide/the-japanese-cherry-blossom-trees/index.html), [4](https://www.tripoto.com/japan/trips/10-beautiful-japanese-words-for-cherry-blossom-season-you-should-know-60bdc72827696), [5](https://www.spiritjapan.com/blogs/ninja-scrolls-%E6%9C%AC/japan-loves-sakura-so-much-there-are-more-than-70-words-for-cherry-blossoms?srsltid=AfmBOoppdfCe%5FHar1su%5FeuEqpNyE4AMcQ0PePsSq4Ogjf%5FytB3cjGrjY)]

### Essential Cultural Practices

- **Hanami (花見)**: The centuries-old custom of "flower viewing," where people gather for outdoor picnics under the blooming trees.
- **Yozakura (夜桜)**: Literally "night sakura," referring to the magical practice of viewing cherry blossoms illuminated after dark.

### Poetic Descriptions of the Blossoms

- **Sakura-fubuki (桜吹雪)**: A "cherry blossom snowstorm," describing the striking visual of thousands of pink petals swirling through the air like snow.
- **Hanagasumi (花霞)**: A "flower haze," used to describe a landscape where so many distant cherry blossoms are clustered together that it looks like a pink mist.
- **Hana-no-ame (花の雨)**: "Flower rain," a melancholic yet beautiful term for spring rain falling softly onto full-blown cherry blossoms.
- **Hana-akari (花明かり)**: "Flower lights," referring to the phenomenon where the vivid brilliance of full-blooming sakura makes the darkness around them seem brighter.
- **Asazakura (朝桜)**: "Morning sakura," which specifically captures the pristine beauty of cherry blossoms fresh with morning dew. \[[1](https://tandem.net/blog/japanese-words-cherry-blossom), [2](https://livejapan.com/en/article-a0005909/), [3](https://www.facebook.com/JapanConsAK/photos/beautiful-japanese-words-for-sakura-%E6%A1%9Csakurafubuki-%E6%A1%9C%E5%90%B9%E9%9B%AA-cherry-blossom-snowstorm-t/1049960750500591/), [4](https://www.timeout.com/tokyo/things-to-do/japan-has-more-than-70-words-for-cherry-blossoms), [5](https://interacnetwork.com/japanese-words-with-deep-meanings/)]

### Stages of the Bloom

- **Hatsuzakura (初桜)**: The very first cherry blossom sightings of the year.
- **Mankai (満開)**: Full bloom, officially designated when more than 80% of the flowers on the trees have completely opened.
- **Chirihajime (散り始め)**: The transition period when the petals have just begun to drop from the branches.

## You said: sakura saku kanji

The phrase <mark style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);"><strong>"Sakura saku" (桜咲く / さくらさく)</strong></mark> means **"The cherry blossoms bloom."**

### Breakdown of the Kanji

- **桜 (Sakura)**: The kanji for **cherry blossom**.
  - _Left side (木)_: The radical for "tree" or "wood."
  - _Right side (ツ + 女)_: Originally depicted a woman wearing a necklace of shells or gems, hinting at a beautiful arrangement. Together, it represents a spectacularly beautiful tree.
- **咲 (Sa)**: The root kanji for **to bloom** or **to blossom**.
  - _Left side (口)_: The radical for "mouth."
  - _Right side (关)_: An ancient depiction of a person laughing or smiling. In old Japanese, blooming flowers were poetically viewed as the trees "laughing" or "smiling" open.
- **く (ku)**: The hiragana suffix that conjugates the verb to its dictionary (present/future) form: **Saku (咲く)**.

### Cultural & Symbolic Meaning

Beyond its literal definition, **"Sakura saku"** is a famous idiomatic expression in Japan used to say **"Success!"** or **"You passed!"**

- **Exam Results**: It historically became the coded telegram message sent to anxious students to let them know they passed their university entrance exams.
- **The Opposite**: If a student failed, the telegram would read _"Sakura chiru"_ (桜散る), meaning _"The cherry blossoms fall."_
