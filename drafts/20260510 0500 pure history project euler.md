example authors

While the specific identities of most individual problem authors are kept somewhat private to protect the integrity of the challenges, they generally fall into three groups:

1\. The Core Team

The site's founder, **Colin Hughes**, authored many of the initial problems. Over time, he has been supported by a small, hand-picked team of about 8 to 9 administrators who craft and refine the challenges. \[[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project%5FEuler), [2](https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/archive/2011/06/how-i-failed-failed-and-finally-succeeded-at-learning-how-to-code/239855/)]

- **Nathan Goldbaum**: Known for his detailed public documentation and solutions for the early problem sets.

Problem Authorship Trends

Modern problems are usually the result of a rigorous **peer-review process**. A "problem giver" submits a proposal to the team; it is then tested by other high-level solvers to ensure the "one-minute rule" (that an efficient algorithm can solve it in under a minute) is possible before it is officially released.

who is the founder

The founder of Project Euler is **Colin Hughes**, a software developer from England. \[[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project%5FEuler), [2](https://projecteuler.chat/viewtopic.php?t=3422)]

Hughes launched the site in **October 2001** as a passion project, initially hosting it as a sub-section on his other site, `mathschallenge.net`. Due to its growing popularity, Project Euler moved to its own dedicated domain in 2006. \[[1](https://github.com/prashdash112/Project-Euler-solutions), [2](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=45902898)]

Key Facts About the Founder

- **The "Euler" Alias**: On the Project Euler forums and within the community, Hughes often goes by the username **euler**.
- **Solo Origins**: He built the original software and infrastructure for the site entirely on his own and continues to handle much of its maintenance.
- **Philosophy**: Hughes designed the problems to follow an "inductive chain"—a learning method where solving one problem provides the conceptual building blocks needed for the next.
- **Team Expansion**: While he started alone, he eventually hand-picked a core team of **8 to 9 administrators** to help develop and test new problems as the site scaled to over a million users. \[[1](https://projecteuler.chat/viewtopic.php?t=3422), [2](https://github.com/prashdash112/Project-Euler-solutions), [3](https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/archive/2011/06/how-i-failed-failed-and-finally-succeeded-at-learning-how-to-code/239855/), [4](https://shaastra.substack.com/p/project-euler)]

the history of project euler

[Project Euler](https://projecteuler.net/) is a website dedicated to a series of challenging computational problems that combine mathematical insight with computer programming. \[[1](https://projecteuler.net/), [2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project%5FEuler)]

Origins and Creation

- **Founder**: The project was created by **Colin Hughes** in **2001**.
- **Inspiration**: Hughes, a former math teacher, was inspired by his own experiences learning to program on early computers like the **ORIC-1**.
- **Name**: It is named after **Leonhard Euler**, the prolific 18th-century Swiss mathematician, as a tribute to his vast contributions to number theory and analysis. \[[1](https://kids.kiddle.co/Project%5FEuler), [2](https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/archive/2011/06/how-i-failed-failed-and-finally-succeeded-at-learning-how-to-code/239855/), [3](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Fu3SrilW3rA), [4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project%5FEuler)]

Growth and Evolution

- **Problem Collection**: Starting with just a few problems, the site has grown to include **995 challenges** as of April 2026.
- **Community**: It has evolved into a global "distributed classroom" with over **1.3 million registered members** from 220 different countries as of May 2026.
- **The "One-Minute" Rule**: A historical hallmark of the project is that every problem is designed to be solvable in less than **one minute** of CPU time on a modest computer if an efficient algorithm is used. \[, [2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project%5FEuler), [3](https://projecteuler.net/minimal=%7Bn%7D)]

Mission and Impact

- **Educational Goal**: The project aim is to provide a platform for "inquiring minds" to learn new mathematical concepts through a "recreational context".
- **Inductive Chain Learning**: Problems are often arranged in an "inductive chain," where the knowledge gained from solving an earlier, easier problem acts as a building block for more complex challenges.
- **Code of Conduct**: To preserve the educational value, the project maintains a strict rule against sharing solutions for any problems beyond the first hundred. \[[1](https://projecteuler.net/), [2](https://theuniversalset.substack.com/p/a-brief-guide-to-project-euler), [4](https://math.berkeley.edu/~elafandi/euler/)]

founder background

Colin Hughes, an English mathematician and educator, founded Project Euler as a passion project while working at a small British grammar school. \[[1](https://projecteuler.chat/viewtopic.php?t=3422), [2](https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/archive/2011/06/how-i-failed-failed-and-finally-succeeded-at-learning-how-to-code/239855/)]

Professional & Technical Roots

- **Education Career**: Hughes was a math teacher by profession. His background in the classroom heavily influenced the project's educational philosophy, focusing on "recreational learning" and providing a platform for "inquiring minds".
- **Early Computing**: As a student, he taught himself to program on an **ORIC-1**, an early 8-bit home computer released in the 1980s.
- **Solo Developer**: In the project's early years, Hughes personally developed the entire software infrastructure for the site and managed its maintenance single-handedly. \[[1](https://projecteuler.chat/viewtopic.php?t=3422), [2](https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/archive/2011/06/how-i-failed-failed-and-finally-succeeded-at-learning-how-to-code/239855/), [3](https://projecteuler.net/?ref=technews.lk), [4](https://www.rksmusings.com/2011/06/04/project-euler/)]

The "euler" Persona \[[1](https://projecteuler.chat/viewtopic.php?t=3422)]

In the Project Euler community, Hughes is known by the username **"euler"**. He initially launched the problem set in October 2001 as a sub-section of his other educational site, **mathschallenge.net**, before moving it to its own dedicated domain in 2006 due to its massive popularity. \[[1](https://github.com/rhedshi/project-euler)]

His primary goal was to make programming "addictive" by providing instant feedback and creating a "bottom-up" learning environment where users could master complex concepts through trial and error. \[[1](https://www.rksmusings.com/2011/06/04/project-euler/)]

Project Euler contains **995 problems** as of April 2026, with a new challenge added approximately once a week. \[[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project%5FEuler)]

While Colin Hughes personally created the original problem set starting in 2001, the site now utilizes a small, hand-picked team of about eight or nine specialists to help craft and vet new problems. \[[1](https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/archive/2011/06/how-i-failed-failed-and-finally-succeeded-at-learning-how-to-code/239855/), [2](https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/archive/2011/06/how-i-failed-failed-and-finally-succeeded-at-learning-how-to-code/239855/)]

How "Smart" is Colin Hughes?

While IQ scores are not public, his "intelligence" is best evidenced by his ability to bridge high-level mathematics with efficient computation.

- **Pedagogical Insight**: He developed the **"inductive chain"** method, which requires deep intellectual design to ensure each problem teaches a concept that becomes a building block for the next.
- **Mathematical Precision**: Every problem he or his team provides is verified to be solvable in under **one minute** of CPU time on a modest machine, provided the user finds the "elegant" mathematical shortcut—a feat that requires the creator to have a mastery of both number theory and algorithmic efficiency.
- **The "Euler" standard**: Members of the math and programming community often describe his problems as "tricky" and impossible to "brute force," highlighting his skill in creating challenges that bypass simple computer power in favor of pure intellectual insight. \[[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project%5FEuler), [2](https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/archive/2011/06/how-i-failed-failed-and-finally-succeeded-at-learning-how-to-code/239855/), [3](https://projecteuler.net/?ref=technews.lk), [4](https://matheducators.stackexchange.com/questions/12087/how-does-project-euler-come-up-with-such-good-problems-so-rapidly), [5](https://kids.kiddle.co/Project%5FEuler)]

how many problems does he give by himself

While the exact total is not public, Colin Hughes is credited with personally crafting the **first 100 or so problems**. \[[1](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=45902898), [2](http://bryanpendleton.blogspot.com/2011/06/project-euler.html)]

Since the project's inception in 2001, Hughes has gradually transitioned from being the sole author to working with a small, hand-picked team. \[[1](https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/archive/2011/06/how-i-failed-failed-and-finally-succeeded-at-learning-how-to-code/239855/), [2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project%5FEuler)]

Authorship Transition

- **Solo Era (2001–2006)**: During the site's early years, Hughes was the primary source for all challenges. The "Problem 1" he wrote in 2001 is still widely used today as the entry point for millions of new programmers.
- **The Development Team**: As the project grew, a team of **eight or nine assistants** (Admins) began co-authoring and vetting new problems.
- **Community Contributions**: While rare, the team occasionally accepts problem proposals from the general public. These are typically credited as a "work by the team as a whole" once they have been adapted and verified by the admins. \[[1](https://projecteuler.chat/viewtopic.php?t=3422), [2](http://bryanpendleton.blogspot.com/2011/06/project-euler.html)]

Today, while Hughes still oversees the project and maintains the software, the vast majority of the **995 problems** are considered collective efforts by the core [Project Euler Team](https://projecteuler.net/). \[[1](https://projecteuler.chat/viewtopic.php?t=3422), [2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project%5FEuler)]
