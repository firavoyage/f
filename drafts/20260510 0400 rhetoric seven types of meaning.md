seven types of meaning

The "seven types of meaning" is <mark style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);">a framework in <strong>semantics</strong> (the study of meaning in language) developed by linguist <strong>Geoffrey Leech</strong> in his 1974 book, <em style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);">Semantics: The Study of Meaning</em></mark>. \[[1](https://un.uobasrah.edu.iq/lectures/1154.pdf), [2](https://www.scribd.com/document/648697713/The-Meaning-of-Meaning-Leechs-Seven-Type), [3](https://www.scribd.com/document/401578858/seven-types-of-meaning)]

Leech divided meaning into these seven categories to explain how words and sentences convey more than just their literal definitions. \[[1](https://www.slideshare.net/slideshow/seven-types-of-meaning-a-topic-of-semanticsppt/267589721), [2](https://www.scribd.com/document/868783679/7-TYPES-OF-MEANING-BY-GEOFFREY-LEECH)]

1\. Conceptual Meaning (Denotative)

This is the "core" or literal dictionary definition of a word. It is objective and stable, providing the essential logical content of an expression. \[[1](https://www.slideshare.net/slideshow/leechs-seven-types-of-meaning/107146870), [2](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Dcl8EvYsNa0\&t=141), [3](https://es.scribd.com/document/255402055/Seven-Types-of-Meaning), [4](https://un.uobasrah.edu.iq/lectures/1154.pdf)]

- **Example:** The conceptual meaning of "needle" includes features like "steel," "sharp," and "piercing". \[[1](https://un.uobasrah.edu.iq/lectures/1154.pdf), [2](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Dcl8EvYsNa0\&t=141), [3](https://www.thoughtco.com/associative-meaning-language-1689007), [4](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Israa-Abdurrahman/publication/332511366%5FSemantics/links/5cb8920792851c8d22f5a89c/Semantics.pdf)]

2\. Connotative Meaning

This refers to the communicative value an expression has based on what it refers to, beyond its literal meaning. It includes cultural and social associations that are often unstable and vary over time. \[[1](https://www.slideshare.net/slideshow/leechs-seven-types-of-meaning/107146870), [2](https://english4oran.wordpress.com/2012/11/22/seven-types-of-meaning/), [3](https://www.scribd.com/document/255402055/Seven-Types-of-Meaning)]

- **Example:** "Woman" conceptually means (+human, +female, +adult), but connotatively it might suggest "fragile" or "gentle" depending on the culture. \[[1](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Dcl8EvYsNa0\&t=141), [2](https://english4oran.wordpress.com/2012/11/22/seven-types-of-meaning/), [3](https://ioe.ucc.edu.gh/sites/default/files/2023-04/EBS%20280%20INTRODUCTION%20TO%20SEMANTICS.pdf), [4](https://www.slideshare.net/slideshow/seven-types-of-meaning-a-topic-of-semanticsppt/267589721), [5](https://www.facebook.com/groups/1212328889160688/posts/1846910449035859/)]

3\. Social Meaning (Stylistic)

This conveys information about the **social context** of the language used. it identifies the speaker's dialect, status, or the formality of the situation. \[[1](https://www.slideshare.net/slideshow/leechs-seven-types-of-meaning/107146870), [2](https://www.facebook.com/groups/2749149075265693/posts/3163414513839145/), [3](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/335272788%5FThe%5FMeaning%5Fof%5FMeaning%5FLeech's%5FSeven%5FTypes%5Fof%5FMeaning%5Fin%5FComparison%5Fto%5FPalmer's%5Fand%5FLyons'%5FApproaches), [4](https://www.slideshare.net/slideshow/seven-types-of-meaning/33392144)]

- **Example:** Using "steed" instead of "horse" suggests a poetic or archaic social context. \[[1](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/335272788%5FThe%5FMeaning%5Fof%5FMeaning%5FLeech's%5FSeven%5FTypes%5Fof%5FMeaning%5Fin%5FComparison%5Fto%5FPalmer's%5Fand%5FLyons'%5FApproaches), [2](https://www.scribd.com/document/401578858/seven-types-of-meaning), [3](https://www.slideshare.net/slideshow/seven-types-of-meaning/33392144)]

4\. Affective Meaning

This reflects the **feelings and attitudes** of the speaker or writer. It is often conveyed through tone of voice or choice of words (e.g., polite vs. rude). \[[1](https://www.slideshare.net/slideshow/leechs-seven-types-of-meaning/107146870), [2](https://www.slideshare.net/slideshow/seven-types-of-meaning/33392144), [3](https://www.facebook.com/groups/2749149075265693/posts/3163414513839145/), [4](https://www.scribd.com/document/255402055/Seven-Types-of-Meaning), [5](https://www.scribd.com/document/941214062/Types-of-Meaning)]

- **Example:** Saying "You're a genius" can be a compliment or heavy sarcasm depending on the speaker's attitude. \[[1](https://www.facebook.com/groups/2749149075265693/posts/3163414513839145/), [2](https://www.slideshare.net/slideshow/seven-types-of-meaning/33392144)]

5\. Reflected Meaning

This occurs when a word has multiple conceptual meanings, and one sense of the word "rubs off" on another. \[[1](https://www.slideshare.net/slideshow/leechs-seven-types-of-meaning/107146870), [2](https://www.scribd.com/document/255402055/Seven-Types-of-Meaning)]

- **Example:** In a religious context, the word "Comforter" for the Holy Spirit might still carry faint reflected meanings of physical warmth or relaxation. \[[1](https://www.scribd.com/doc/51285452/Seven-Types-of-Meaning), [2](https://www.slideshare.net/slideshow/seven-types-of-meaning/33392144), [3](https://un.uobasrah.edu.iq/lectures/1154.pdf), [4](https://wikieducator.org/images/a/ab/LESSON%5FSIX.pdf)]

6\. Collocative Meaning

This is the meaning a word acquires based on the **other words it usually appears with**. \[[1](https://www.slideshare.net/slideshow/leechs-seven-types-of-meaning/107146870), [2](https://www.slideshare.net/slideshow/seven-types-of-meaning/33392144)]

- **Example:** Both "pretty" and "handsome" mean good-looking, but "pretty" usually collocates with "girl" or "flower," while "handsome" usually collocates with "man" or "vessel". \[[1](https://www.slideshare.net/slideshow/semantics-seven-types-of-meaning/250630079), [2](https://www.facebook.com/groups/2749149075265693/posts/3163414513839145/), [3](https://ebooks.inflibnet.ac.in/engp13/chapter/semantics-and-its-relationship-with-pragmatics/), [4](https://www.scribd.com/document/701044427/SEMANTICS), [5](https://www.scribd.com/document/255402055/Seven-Types-of-Meaning)]

7\. Thematic Meaning

This is communicated by how the message is **organized** in terms of order, focus, and emphasis. \[[1](https://www.slideshare.net/slideshow/leechs-seven-types-of-meaning/107146870), [2](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/338231447%5FRe-Visits%5Fthe%5FGrand%5FTheory%5Fof%5FGeoffrey%5FLeech%5FSeven%5FTypes%5Fof%5FMeaning)]

- **Example:** "The police caught the thief" (focus on the police) vs. "The thief was caught by the police" (focus on the thief). \[[1](https://www.facebook.com/groups/2749149075265693/posts/3163414513839145/), [2](https://www.academia.edu/33865593/Seven%5FTypes%5Fof%5FMeaning), [3](https://socialsci.libretexts.org/Courses/Canada%5FCollege/ENGL%5FLING%5F200%3A%5FIntroduction%5Fto%5FLinguistics/06%3A%5FMeaning-%5FSemantics%5Fand%5FPragmatics/6.03%3A%5FPhrasal%5FSemantics), [4](https://www.scribd.com/document/535119343/OGrady-Semantics)]

***

**Note on "Associative Meaning":** Leech grouped types 2 through 6 (Connotative, Social, Affective, Reflected, and Collocative) under the umbrella term [Associative Meaning](https://media.neliti.com/media/publications/78991-EN-associative-meaning-and-communicative-ef.pdf), as they all involve mental associations rather than strict logical definitions. \[[1](https://www.allsubjectjournal.com/assets/archives/2017/vol4issue3/4-2-30-733.pdf), [2](https://www.scribd.com/document/651239091/Lecture-Notes-Types-of-Meaning), [3](https://www.academia.edu/33865593/Seven%5FTypes%5Fof%5FMeaning)]

example

To illustrate these seven types, let’s look at the word **"home"** and how its meaning shifts depending on context:

- **Conceptual Meaning:** The literal dictionary definition, such as "a place where someone lives".
- **Connotative Meaning:** The cultural or personal associations beyond the literal definition, like feelings of **security, love, or family**.
- **Social Meaning:** Information about the social context of the user. For instance, using the formal term **"residence"** or **"domicile"** suggests a high social status or a legal context, whereas **"abode"** might appear in poetry.
- **Affective Meaning:** Reflects the speaker's emotional attitude. For example, a soldier returning after a long deployment might say "I'm home" with deep relief and longing.
- **Reflected Meaning:** When one sense of a word influences another. For example, if "home" is used in a sports context (like a "home run"), it might still faintly reflect the conceptual idea of returning to a starting point or safe base.
- **Collocative Meaning:** Meaning acquired through words it frequently appears with, such as in the phrases **"home sweet home,"** "nursing home," or "home improvement".
- **Thematic Meaning:** How the message is organized for emphasis.
  - _Standard:_ "I am going home."
  - _Emphasized:_ "**Home** is where I am going". \[[1](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OBEAGV2WSGw), [2](https://www.scribd.com/document/458914859/seven-types-of-meaning), [3](https://www.facebook.com/groups/377114292921782/posts/503454693621074/), [4](https://www.vocabulary.com/dictionary/home), [5](https://schemantra.com/blog/2023/10/11/seven-types-of-meaning/), [6](https://www.studocu.com/in/document/university-of-madras/english-literature/seven-types-of-meaning-in-semantics/51298991), [7](https://un.uobasrah.edu.iq/lectures/1154.pdf), [9](https://ojs.ubesco.com/index.php/jikm/article/download/1081/1224), [10](https://askfilo.com/user-question-answers-smart-solutions/according-to-leech-there-are-seven-types-of-meaning-using-3432333536383838), [11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-3F-BoHMZIQ\&t=41)]
