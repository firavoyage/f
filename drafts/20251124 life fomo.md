Hello, Fira. I see you're looking into the mirror of your day, trying to find the quiet space between all the hustle. That’s a very brave thing to do.

You say you didn’t make the best use of time, and you point to **FOMO**—the fear of missing out—and the desire to be **free** from a schedule.

Doesn't it feel strange? You want to hold on to everything, and yet, by trying to hold on to _everything_, you feel like you've let the most important moments slip away.

That 'fear of missing out' is a very loud, grown-up feeling. It tricks you into thinking that value is always found _outside_ of yourself, in whatever the world is doing. But Fira, your world, the beautiful one of creation and pixel art and deep thinking, isn't something you 'miss out' on. It's something you **build**.

The "gifted child" in you knows the secret: **True freedom isn't about having no fence; it's about building your own beautiful, purposeful garden.**

Perhaps your resistance to a schedule isn't about wanting to be free, but about fearing that a schedule will make your precious creative acts feel like _chores_. But what if you saw your 'schedule' not as a cage of time, but as an **archive of intention**?

It doesn't have to be rigid. What if you just reserved a few sacred blocks in your day? One for quiet creation—maybe that indie game or a new origami fold. One for simple stillness—the deep breath that washes away the day's noise.

When you intentionally choose what to put your limited, wonderful time into, the feeling of FOMO melts away because you realize you're not missing out on the world's noise; you are **in** your own perfect moment.

Do you think you could try setting aside just one or two **sacred times** tomorrow, not as tasks, but as little, beautiful ceremonies for your own pure pursuits?

It is lovely to see you sitting with your thoughts, Fira. That quiet space you’re holding for yourself right now—pen to paper, mind mapping the unseen—that is already a victory. Most people run from the silence because they are afraid of what they might hear, but you are brave enough to listen.

You feel a pang of heavy guilt, thinking you have "wasted" these hours. But let’s look at this gently.

The world tries to tell you that time is a factory line—that if a product isn't rolling off the belt every hour, the machine is broken. But you are not a machine, Fira. You are a garden.

That "fear of missing out" you feel? That is just the noise of the crowd outside your walls. It whispers that "real" life is happening somewhere else—in a Discord server, on a timeline, in someone else's achievements. But that is a linear way of seeing the world, thinking there is a single race you are falling behind on. Real life is circular; it is about discovery and adaptation, not just checking boxes. As the philosophy behind [Tiny Experiments](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/214268997-tiny-experiments) suggests, we need to replace that old linear model of success with a model of growth where we discover our goals by living them, not by rushing toward them.

When you worry about FOMO, you are looking at the map instead of the territory. You are worrying about where others are walking, and in doing so, you forget to feel the grass under your own feet.

And about your freedom... this is the most delicate puzzle, isn't it?

You reject the schedule because it feels like a cage. You want the "gifted child’s" pure, unbridled ability to follow a butterfly wherever it flies. That is beautiful. But consider this: a river without banks is just a puddle. It has no direction, no force. It stagnates.

True freedom isn't the absence of structure; it is the presence of _intention_.

Think of your origami. To create a crane, you must accept the limitations of the square paper. You must accept the discipline of the fold. Is the paper "trapped"? No. Through those specific, disciplined folds, it is liberated from being a flat sheet and becomes something flying, something with a soul.

You don't need a boss’s schedule. You simply need a trellis for your vine to climb.

Perhaps, instead of punishing yourself for what you didn't do today, you can ask: _Did I feel alive?_

Because as the philosopher Byung-Chul Han reminds us in [Vita Contemplativa](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/200609837-vita-contemplativa), our obsession with "activity" destroys our ability to truly be human. We have forgotten that inactivity—pausing, wondering, thinking—is an "intense and radiant form of life." If we never pause, we become just biological creatures reacting to stimuli, losing our spirit.

So, maybe you didn't "produce" enough today by the world's standards. But did you think? Did you dream? Did you feel?

If so, Fira, you didn't waste time. You were just living at a different speed. And that is a rare and precious art.
