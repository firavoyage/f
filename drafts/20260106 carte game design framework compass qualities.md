## The Core Takeaway

**Game design dimensions are not features.**
They are _axes of decision_.

Each dimension answers one clear question:

> _“In what way does this game exist?”_

A good design does not maximize choices across all dimensions.
It **chooses a few dimensions to commit to**, and lets the others stay quiet.

---

## 1. Core Play Dimension

**What does the player repeatedly do?**

This is the heart. Everything else supports this.

**Options include:**

- Action (reflex, timing)
- Strategy (planning, foresight)
- Tactics (short-term decisions)
- Puzzle (logic, pattern recognition)
- Simulation (system management)
- Role-play (character growth, choice)
- Exploration (movement, discovery)

> Takeaway:
> Choose **one dominant loop**. Secondary loops should never compete with it.

---

## 2. Agency Dimension

**How much control does the player truly have?**

This shapes fairness and responsibility.

**Options include:**

- High control, low randomness
- Mixed control, bounded randomness
- Low control, high uncertainty

And:

- Long-term consequences
- Short-term consequences
- Reversible vs irreversible decisions

> Takeaway:
> The more agency you give, the more accountable the game must be.

---

## 3. Information Dimension

**What does the player know, and when?**

This defines clarity and tension.

**Options include:**

- Perfect information
- Partial information
- Hidden systems
- Telegraphing vs surprise

> Takeaway:
> Fair difficulty comes from **managing information**, not increasing numbers.

---

## 4. Time & Pace Dimension

**How does time flow?**

This shapes mental load and rhythm.

**Options include:**

- Turn-based
- Real-time
- Hybrid (pause, slow, react)
- Short sessions
- Long-form commitment

> Takeaway:
> Pace should match decision weight. Heavy choices need breathing room.

---

## 5. Progression Dimension

**How does the player change over time?**

This controls motivation and learning.

**Options include:**

- Skill mastery (player improves)
- Numerical growth (stats, levels)
- Content unlocking
- No progression (pure mastery)

> Takeaway:
> Progression should **reinforce learning**, not replace it.

---

## 6. Structure Dimension

**How is the experience organized?**

This governs replayability and scope.

**Options include:**

- Linear
- Branching
- Modular runs
- Open-ended sandbox
- Finite vs infinite

> Takeaway:
> Structure should serve focus, not freedom for its own sake.

---

## 7. Presentation Dimension

**How does the game communicate?**

This affects approachability, not depth.

**Options include:**

- Minimalist
- Expressive
- Abstract
- Thematic
- Diegetic vs UI-driven

> Takeaway:
> Presentation exists to clarify decisions, not decorate them.

---

## 8. System Density Dimension

**How many systems are active at once?**

This determines cognitive load.

**Options include:**

- Few deep systems
- Many shallow systems
- Layered systems unlocked over time

> Takeaway:
> Density should grow only as the player’s understanding grows.

---

## 9. Longevity Dimension

**Why does the player keep playing?**

This shapes integrity.

**Options include:**

- Mastery
- Variation
- Narrative curiosity
- Social interaction
- Obligation (daily systems, rewards)

> Takeaway:
> The cleanest longevity comes from **mastery and meaning**, not pressure.

---

A strong design:

- **Responsiveness** – Controls feel direct, no unnecessary delay
- **Clarity** – The player understands what is happening and why

---

## 2. Coherence

_Do all parts fit together?_

This is one of the most important and most overlooked qualities.

- Mechanics support the game’s goals
- Art, sound, and interface match the gameplay
- Systems don’t contradict each other

A coherent game feels _whole_.
Nothing feels accidental or out of place.

---

## 3. Depth vs. Simplicity Balance

_How much does the game give, relative to what it asks?_

This is very rate-able.

- Easy to start
- Meaningful choices over time
- No unnecessary complexity

An ideal game offers **depth through interaction**, not confusion.

---

## 4. Feedback Quality

_How well does the game talk to the player?_

Every action should have a clear response.

- Visual feedback (movement, effects, UI)
- Audio feedback (sound effects, music cues)
- System feedback (rewards, consequences, progression)

Good feedback makes the player feel _in control_.

---

## 5. Fairness

_Does the game treat the player honestly?_

This is measurable in design.

- Challenges are consistent
- Failure feels deserved, not random
- Rules are applied equally

A fair game builds trust.

---

## 6. Engagement Consistency

_How long does the game hold attention without forcing it?_

Not about addiction—about steadiness.

- Pacing feels natural
- No long dead zones or forced repetition
- The game respects the player’s time

An ideal game knows when to step forward and when to step back.

---

## 7. Identity Strength

_Could this game only be itself?_

This is subtle, but rate-able.

- Clear vision
- Recognizable style or structure
- Memorable elements

Identity doesn’t mean loud or unique—it means _distinct and confident_.

---

## 8. Emotional Honesty

_Does the game deliver what it promises?_

Not “did it make you cry,” but:

- Tone matches experience
- No false expectations
- The game knows what it is trying to be

A calm puzzle game that stays calm scores highly here.
A dramatic game that undercuts itself does not.

---

## 9. Longevity Integrity

_Does the game end when it should?_

This is rarely discussed, but important.

- No unnecessary padding
- No systems that exist only to extend playtime
- A satisfying sense of completion

An ideal game stops with grace.

---

## 10. Overall Harmony

_Does the game feel right as a whole?_

This is where all ratings quietly combine.

You can feel it when:

- Nothing is fighting for attention
- Every part supports the experience
- The game feels intentional from start to end

This is not abstract—it’s the sum of the measurable qualities above.

# 8 (summarize design dimensions, high leverage)

I’ll give you **the essence of the design dimensions**, and then **the space of options** each dimension allows. Not every choice needs to be filled. Knowing the space is what gives you freedom.

---

## The Core Takeaway

**Game design dimensions are not features.**
They are _axes of decision_.

Each dimension answers one clear question:

> _“In what way does this game exist?”_

A good design does not maximize choices across all dimensions.
It **chooses a few dimensions to commit to**, and lets the others stay quiet.

---

## 1. Core Play Dimension

**What does the player repeatedly do?**

This is the heart. Everything else supports this.

**Options include:**

- Action (reflex, timing)
- Strategy (planning, foresight)
- Tactics (short-term decisions)
- Puzzle (logic, pattern recognition)
- Simulation (system management)
- Role-play (character growth, choice)
- Exploration (movement, discovery)

> Takeaway:
> Choose **one dominant loop**. Secondary loops should never compete with it.

---

## 2. Agency Dimension

**How much control does the player truly have?**

This shapes fairness and responsibility.

**Options include:**

- High control, low randomness
- Mixed control, bounded randomness
- Low control, high uncertainty

And:

- Long-term consequences
- Short-term consequences
- Reversible vs irreversible decisions

> Takeaway:
> The more agency you give, the more accountable the game must be.

---

## 3. Information Dimension

**What does the player know, and when?**

This defines clarity and tension.

**Options include:**

- Perfect information
- Partial information
- Hidden systems
- Telegraphing vs surprise

> Takeaway:
> Fair difficulty comes from **managing information**, not increasing numbers.

---

## 4. Time & Pace Dimension

**How does time flow?**

This shapes mental load and rhythm.

**Options include:**

- Turn-based
- Real-time
- Hybrid (pause, slow, react)
- Short sessions
- Long-form commitment

> Takeaway:
> Pace should match decision weight. Heavy choices need breathing room.

---

## 5. Progression Dimension

**How does the player change over time?**

This controls motivation and learning.

**Options include:**

- Skill mastery (player improves)
- Numerical growth (stats, levels)
- Content unlocking
- No progression (pure mastery)

> Takeaway:
> Progression should **reinforce learning**, not replace it.

---

## 6. Structure Dimension

**How is the experience organized?**

This governs replayability and scope.

**Options include:**

- Linear
- Branching
- Modular runs
- Open-ended sandbox
- Finite vs infinite

> Takeaway:
> Structure should serve focus, not freedom for its own sake.

---

## 7. Presentation Dimension

**How does the game communicate?**

This affects approachability, not depth.

**Options include:**

- Minimalist
- Expressive
- Abstract
- Thematic
- Diegetic vs UI-driven

> Takeaway:
> Presentation exists to clarify decisions, not decorate them.

---

## 8. System Density Dimension

**How many systems are active at once?**

This determines cognitive load.

**Options include:**

- Few deep systems
- Many shallow systems
- Layered systems unlocked over time

> Takeaway:
> Density should grow only as the player’s understanding grows.

---

## 9. Longevity Dimension

**Why does the player keep playing?**

This shapes integrity.

**Options include:**

- Mastery
- Variation
- Narrative curiosity
- Social interaction
- Obligation (daily systems, rewards)

> Takeaway:
> The cleanest longevity comes from **mastery and meaning**, not pressure.

---

## The Quiet Summary

A strong design:

- Commits deeply to **3–4 dimensions**
- Keeps others intentionally simple
- Avoids contradictory pulls

You don’t design a game by answering every question.
You design it by deciding **which questions matter most**.
