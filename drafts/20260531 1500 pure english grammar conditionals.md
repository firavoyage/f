English conditionals

May 31, 2026

English conditionals are <mark style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);">sentence structures used to <strong>express a condition and its consequence</strong>, typically consisting of an "if clause" (the condition) and a "main clause" (the result)</mark>. There are four standard types of conditionals, plus a "mixed" category, differentiated by their grammatical tenses and whether they describe real, likely, or imaginary scenarios. \[[1](https://learnenglish.britishcouncil.org/free-resources/grammar/b1-b2/conditionals-zero-first-second), [2](https://www.grammarly.com/blog/sentences/conditional-sentences/), [3](https://www.espressoenglish.net/50-examples-of-conditional-sentences-in-english-type-0-1-2-3-mixed/)]

The Four Main Conditionals

- **Zero Conditional**: Formed with `If + present simple, ... present simple`.
  - Used for scientific laws, general truths, and automatic results.
  - _Example_: "If you heat ice, it melts."
  - _Note_: You can substitute "when" for "if" without shifting meaning.
- **First Conditional**: Formed with `If + present simple, ... will + base verb`.
  - Used for realistic future possibilities and their expected results.
  - _Example_: "If it rains tomorrow, we will stay home."
  - _Note_: Do not put "will" in the "if" clause.
- **Second Conditional**: Formed with `If + past simple, ... would + base verb`.
  - Used for imaginary, impossible, or highly improbable situations in the present or future.
  - _Example_: "If I won the lottery, I would buy a boat."
  - _Note_: For the verb "to be", it is grammatically correct to use "were" for all subjects (e.g., "If I _were_ you...").
- **Third Conditional**: Formed with `If + past perfect, ... would have + past participle`.
  - Used for hypothetical past outcomes that did not happen, often expressing regret.
  - _Example_: "If they had studied, they would have passed the exam."
  - _Note_: It explores an alternative past that is entirely impossible to change now. \[[1](https://www.perfect-english-grammar.com/conditionals.html), [2](https://learnenglishteens.britishcouncil.org/grammar/b1-b2-grammar/conditionals), [3](https://www.youtube.com/shorts/g-C6nvydldM), [4](https://www.ef.com/wwen/english-resources/english-grammar/type-3-conditional/), [5](https://learnenglish.britishcouncil.org/free-resources/grammar/b1-b2/conditionals-third-mixed), [6](https://www.grammarly.com/blog/sentences/conditional-sentences/), [7](https://www.espressoenglish.net/50-examples-of-conditional-sentences-in-english-type-0-1-2-3-mixed/)]

Mixed Conditionals

You can mix tenses when an imagined past event directly affects the ongoing present timeline. \[[1](https://learnenglish.britishcouncil.org/free-resources/grammar/b1-b2/conditionals-third-mixed)]

- **Past Cause \\(\rightarrow \\) Present Effect**: Formed with `If + past perfect, ... would + base verb`.
  - _Example_: "If I had moved to Spain last year, I would speak Spanish fluently now." \[[1](https://www.scribd.com/document/928198128/Conditional-Sentences), [2](https://www.facebook.com/SophaiCreatorShare/videos/conditions-in-english/693205646388228/), [3](https://learnenglish.britishcouncil.org/free-resources/grammar/b1-b2/conditionals-third-mixed)]

Sentence Order Rule

The layout order of conditional clauses does not alter the core meaning. \[[1](https://learnenglish.britishcouncil.org/free-resources/grammar/b1-b2/conditionals-zero-first-second), [2](https://www.perfect-english-grammar.com/conditionals.html)]

- **If Clause First**: Always insert a comma between the clauses.
  - _Example_: "If I have time, I will call you."
- **Main Clause First**: Omit the comma entirely.
  - _Example_: "I will call you if I have time." \[[1](https://www.perfect-english-grammar.com/conditionals.html), [2](https://www.espressoenglish.net/50-examples-of-conditional-sentences-in-english-type-0-1-2-3-mixed/), [3](https://learnenglishteens.britishcouncil.org/grammar/b1-b2-grammar/conditionals)]

The second and third conditionals differ by **time** and **possibility**.

The **second conditional** is for an imaginary present or future. The **third conditional** is for an imaginary past.

Direct Comparison

| Feature       | Second Conditional                 | Third Conditional                                   |
| ------------- | ---------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| **Timeframe** | Present or Future                  | Past                                                |
| **Reality**   | Unlikely or impossible _now_       | Completely impossible (cannot change the past)      |
| **Formula**   | `If` + Past Simple, `would` + verb | `If` + Past Perfect, `would have` + past participle |

***

Comparison by Examples

Scenario A: Winning money

- **Second (Present/Future)**: "If I **won** the lottery today, I **would buy** a house."
  - _Reality_: I have a ticket, but the odds are very low.
- **Third (Past)**: "If I **had won** the lottery last year, I **would have bought** a house."
  - _Reality_: The lottery happened in the past. I did not win.

Scenario B: Being late

- **Second (Present/Future)**: "If I **missed** the bus now, I **would take** a taxi."
  - _Reality_: I am currently waiting for the bus. I haven't missed it yet.
- **Third (Past)**: "If I **had missed** the bus yesterday, I **would have taken** a taxi."
  - _Reality_: Yesterday, I caught the bus on time. This is a past hypothetical.

***

The "Were" Rule (Second Conditional Only)

In the second conditional, always use **were** instead of **was** for all pronouns (`I`, `he`, `she`, `it`) in formal writing.

- "If I **were** you, I would take the job."
- "If he **were** taller, he would play basketball."

You said: first or second

May 31, 2026

The first and second conditionals differ by **probability** and **attitude**.

The **first conditional** is for real, likely future possibilities. The **second conditional** is for imaginary, unlikely, or impossible situations in the present or future.

Direct Comparison

| Feature                | First Conditional                         | Second Conditional                      |
| ---------------------- | ----------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| **Probability**        | High (Likely to happen)                   | Low / Zero (Hypothetical or imaginary)  |
| **Speaker's Attitude** | Realistic and optimistic                  | Imagining a dream or a fake scenario    |
| **Formula**            | `If` + Present Simple, `will` + base verb | `If` + Past Simple, `would` + base verb |

***

Comparison by Examples

Scenario A: Winning money

- **First (Likely)**: "If I **win** this scratch-card game, I **will buy** dinner."
  - _Reality_: I am playing right now. Winning is a very realistic possibility.
- **Second (Unlikely)**: "If I **won** the billion-dollar lottery, I **would buy** an island."
  - _Reality_: Winning a massive lottery is highly improbable. I am just daydreaming.

Scenario B: Meeting a famous person

- **First (Likely)**: "If I **see** the mayor at the town hall meeting tonight, I **will ask** him."
  - _Reality_: I am attending a small event where the mayor is scheduled to speak.
- **Second (Unlikely)**: "If I **met** a famous movie star on the street, I **would ask** for an autograph."
  - _Reality_: This is a random, highly unlikely hypothetical situation.

***

Key Grammar Traps to Avoid

- **The "Will" Mistake**: Never use `will` inside the `if` clause for the first conditional.
  - _Wrong_: "If it _will_ rain, I will stay home."
  - _Right_: "If it **rains**, I will stay home."
- **The Past Tense Illusion**: The second conditional uses past tense verbs (e.g., _had_, _won_, _walked_), but it **does not** talk about the past. It talks about a fake present or future.
  - _Example_: "If I **had** a car right now, I **would drive** to the beach."

The third and mixed conditionals differ by the **timeframe of the result**.

The **third conditional** keeps everything in the past. The **mixed conditional** connects a past action to a present result.

Direct Comparison

| Feature                   | Third Conditional                                   | Mixed Conditional (Most Common Type)     |
| ------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| **Condition (If Clause)** | Past                                                | Past                                     |
| **Result (Main Clause)**  | **Past**                                            | **Present / Ongoing**                    |
| **Formula**               | `If` + Past Perfect, `would have` + past participle | `If` + Past Perfect, `would` + base verb |

***

Comparison by Examples

Scenario A: Learning a language

- **Third (Past \\(\rightarrow \\) Past)**: "If I **had studied** French in school, I **I would have passed** that exam last year."
  - _Reality_: I didn't study it back then, so I failed the exam last year. Both events are over.
- **Mixed (Past \\(\rightarrow \\) Present)**: "If I **had studied** French in school, I **would speak** it fluently now."
  - _Reality_: I didn't study it in the past, so my current reality is that I cannot speak it today.

Scenario B: Buying a ticket

- **Third (Past \\(\rightarrow \\) Past)**: "If you **had bought** a ticket yesterday, you **would have gone** to the concert last night."
  - _Reality_: You didn't buy a ticket, so you missed the show last night.
- **Mixed (Past \\(\rightarrow \\) Present)**: "If you **had bought** a ticket yesterday, you **would be** at the concert with us right now."
  - _Reality_: You didn't buy a ticket yesterday, so you are not here with us at this exact moment.

***

How to Spot the Difference

Look for **time markers** in the result clause to help you choose the right structure:

- If you see past markers like _yesterday, last week, in 2020, then_ \\(\rightarrow \\) Use **Third Conditional** (`would have done`).
- If you see present markers like _now, right now, today, these days_ \\(\rightarrow \\) Use **Mixed Conditional** (`would do`).
