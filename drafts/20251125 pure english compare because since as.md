### Detailed Breakdown

#### 1. Because

This is the workhorse for expressing cause and effect. It is the most direct and unambiguous choice.

- **Function:** Introduces the primary, direct cause or reason.
- **Usage:** Use it when the reason is the most important part of your sentence.
- **Example:**
  - "The game was canceled **because** of the rain." (The rain is the direct cause.)
  - "She was late **because** her car wouldn't start."
- **Formality:** Appropriate for all contexts, from casual conversation to academic papers.

> **Tip:** When in doubt, use **because**. It is almost never wrong.

#### 2. Since

This word has a dual identity, which can sometimes create ambiguity.

- **Function 1: Time.** Its primary meaning is to refer to a point in time from the past until now.

  - "I have lived here **since** 2010."
  - "**Since** you arrived, the office has been much livelier."

- **Function 2: Reason.** It can be used to mean "inasmuch as" or "seeing that." It often implies a reason that is already known or logical to the listener.

  - "**Since** you have a PhD in physics, you can probably explain this." (The reason is your existing qualification.)
  - "**Since** it's already 10 PM, we should head home." (The reason is the logical conclusion based on the time.)

- **Ambiguity:** The sentence "**Since** he graduated, he got a new job" could mean:

  1.  _Because_ he graduated, he got a new job. (Reason)
  2.  _From the time_ he graduated, he has had a new job. (Time)

- **Formality:** Slightly more formal than _as_ when used for reason.

#### 3. As

Like _since_, **as** can be ambiguous, but its primary meanings are different.

- **Function 1: Simultaneous Action.** It most commonly means "while" or "during the time that."

  - "**As** I was walking home, it began to rain." (The actions happened at the same time.)

- **Function 2: Reason.** It can be used to mean "because," but this usage is more formal and can feel a bit old-fashioned or literary. It often implies a reason that is incidental or less direct.

  - "**As** you are unable to comply, we must terminate the contract." (Formal, legalistic)
  - "**As** it's your birthday, you can choose the restaurant."

- **Ambiguity:** The sentence "**As** we were leaving, he called" is clear (simultaneous action). But "**As** we were tired, we went home" could be interpreted as either "Because we were tired..." or "While we were in a state of being tired..." (The latter is awkward, but the ambiguity exists).

- **Formality:** For reason, it is more formal than _because_.

### Other Similar Expressions

#### 4. Due to

This phrase is often misused. Traditionally, **due to** is an adjectival phrase and should be used to modify a noun, not a verb. A good test is to see if you can replace it with "caused by."

- **Correct (Modifies a Noun):** "The cancellation **was due to** rain." (The cancellation = caused by rain).
- **Correct:** "His success is **due to** hard work." (His success = caused by hard work).
- **Traditionally Incorrect (Modifies a Verb):** "The game was canceled **due to** rain." (Here, it's modifying the verb "was canceled," which is awkward. "Because of" is preferred.)

> **Modern Usage:** In modern English, using "due to" to mean "because of" is becoming increasingly common and is often accepted, especially in informal contexts. However, in formal writing, distinguishing between them is still appreciated.

#### 5. For (as a conjunction)

This is the most formal and literary of the group. It's rarely used in everyday speech but is common in older literature and formal explanations.

- **Function:** Provides a reason, often one that explains or justifies the previous clause.
- **Usage:** It cannot start a sentence. It typically follows a comma and the main clause.
- **Example:**
  - "I know she is telling the truth, **for** I was there and saw it myself."
  - "We must be near the shore, **for** I can hear the seagulls."

---

### Summary and Recommendations

1.  **For the clearest, most direct reason:** Use **because**.

    - "I'm hungry **because** I skipped lunch."

2.  **To talk about time or a logical, known reason:** Use **since**.

    - "I've known her **since** we were children." (Time)
    - "**Since** you're the expert, what should we do?" (Reason)

3.  **To talk about something happening at the same time, or for a formal reason:** Use **as**.

    - "**As** the sun set, the temperature dropped." (Simultaneous)
    - "**As** your request was submitted late, we cannot process it." (Formal Reason)

4.  **To modify a noun (meaning 'caused by'):** Use **due to**.

    - "The delay was **due to** technical issues."

5.  **For a formal, literary explanation:** Use **for**.
    - "He was clearly guilty, **for** he could not meet my gaze."
