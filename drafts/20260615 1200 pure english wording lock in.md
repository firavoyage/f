## You said: lock or lock in

<mark style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);"><span style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);">Both </span><strong>"lock"</strong> and <strong>"lock in"</strong> work perfectly for design choices</mark>, but they carry slightly different nuances depending on your context.

### "Lock in" (Best for the Decision Process)

Use **lock in** when you are talking about finalizing a choice, reaching an agreement, or making a commitment that prevents future changes. It emphasizes the act of choosing. \[[1](https://medium.com/design-bootcamp/key-takeaways-from-tom-greevers-articulating-design-decisions-68ec95d68241), [2](https://quizlet.com/study-guides/understanding-lock-down-in-english-definitions-and-contexts-a184f542-947f-417c-bd3f-9cffc571f428)]

- _Example:_ "We need to **lock in** the color palette before we start the high-fidelity mockups."
- _Example:_ "Once the client **locks in** these typography choices, we can move forward."

### "Lock" (Best for the Technical Execution)

Use **lock** when you are talking about the literal or structural constraints within your design files, software, or layout grids. It emphasizes physical or digital stability.

- _Example:_ "Please **lock** the hero image layer so it doesn't shift during edits."
- _Example:_ "We will **lock** the responsive layout dimensions for mobile screens."

### Quick Rule of Thumb

- If you are securing a **mental agreement or decision**, use **lock in**.
- If you are securing a **digital layer or pixel position**, use **lock**.
