# can i say go do sth

<mark><strong><span>Yes, you can absolutely say "go do something."</span></strong></mark> It is a highly common, informal expression used in daily English. [link](https://www.quora.com/Is-it-grammatically-correct-to-put-the-word-go-before-a-verb-such-as-go-do-your-homework-or-go-keep-my-dad-company)

### Correct Usage Breakdown

In spoken English, especially in American English, people regularly drop the word "and" or "to" between **go** and the second verb. [link](https://ell.stackexchange.com/questions/113680/imperative-with-go-go-do-something)

- **Imperatives (Commands/Suggestions):** "Go do your homework."
- **Bare Infinitives:** "I need to go wash my hands."
- **Future/Present Intents:** "I will go buy some milk." [link](https://www.quora.com/Is-it-grammatically-correct-to-put-the-word-go-before-a-verb-such-as-go-do-your-homework-or-go-keep-my-dad-company)

### The Tense Restriction Rule

You can only drop "and" if **go** is in its base form. You **cannot** use this structure in the past tense. [link](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TcjhNljPSvw\&t=1)

| Correct                          | Incorrect                    |
| -------------------------------- | ---------------------------- |
| I need to **go buy** groceries.  | I **went bought** groceries. |
| I **went and bought** groceries. |                              |

### Regional Differences

- **American English:** Strongly prefers omitting the connector ("Go check the mail").
- **British English:** More frequently retains "and" ("Go and check the mail"). [link](https://ell.stackexchange.com/questions/113680/imperative-with-go-go-do-something)

## grammar

### Grammar Rules for "Go Do \[Something]"

The "go + bare infinitive" structure follows specific grammatical rules that dictate when it is acceptable to use.

#### 1. The Base Form Rule

You can only drop "and" or "to" when the verb **go** is in its **base form**.

- **Allowed:** Imperatives, infinitives, and modal verb constructions.
  - _Example:_ "Please **go find** your shoes." (Imperative)
  - _Example:_ "I might **go run** a few laps." (After modal "might")
- **Forbidden:** You cannot use this structure if **go** changes form (e.g., _goes, went, going_).
  - _Incorrect:_ "He **goes gets** the paper every morning." → _Correct:_ "He **goes and gets**..."
  - _Incorrect:_ "She is **going shop**." → _Correct:_ "She is **going to shop**."

#### 2. The Serialization Phenomenon

In linguistics, this is known as a **pseudo-coordinated serial verb construction**.

- The two verbs function as a single event.
- The first verb ("go") acts almost like an auxiliary modifier indicating movement or intention.
- The second verb carries the primary action.

#### 3. Formal vs. Informal Writing

- **Informal:** "I'll go check the results." (Perfectly fine for speech, texting, and casual emails).
- **Formal:** "I will **go and check** the results" or "I will **go to check** the results." (Required for academic, professional, or legal documents).

# compare go do sth, go and do sth, and go to do sth

These three structures all express going somewhere to perform an action, but they differ in tone, grammar, and regional usage.

### **Go do something**

This structure uses a bare infinitive (the verb without "to").

- **Usage:** Extremely common in casual, spoken American English.
- **Tone:** Informal, direct, and immediate.
- **Grammar Limitation:** Usually only works in the present tense or imperative. You cannot say "He went do shopping" (use "went to do" or "went doing" instead).
- **Example:** "I need to **go wash** my car."

### **Go and do something**

This structure connects the two actions using the conjunction "and."

- **Usage:** Preferred in British English, but also very common in spoken American English.
- **Tone:** Informal and conversational.
- **Nuance:** Can sometimes express surprise, annoyance, or emphasis depending on the context (e.g., "Don't go and ruin the surprise!").
- **Grammar Flex:** Works in the past tense ("He **went and bought** a new phone").
- **Example:** "Let's **go and grab** some lunch."

### **Go to do something**

This is the standard infinitive of purpose, where "to" means "in order to."

- **Usage:** Standard in all varieties of English, both spoken and written.
- **Tone:** Formal, grammatically precise, and neutral.
- **Grammar Flex:** Works perfectly in all tenses (past, present, future).
- **Example:** "She **went to buy** some milk."

## how does each feel

Here is how each structure "feels" to a native speaker, focusing on the mood, attitude, and vibe they project:

### 💨 **Go do something**

- **The Vibe:** Energetic, urgent, and efficient.
- **How it feels:** It feels like a single, seamless action. The distance between "going" and "doing" disappears. It sounds highly proactive and fast-paced.
- **The Sentiment:** Direct and no-nonsense.
- **Example:** _"Go get your shoes."_ (Feels like a quick, direct command).

### 🗣️ **Go and do something**

- **The Vibe:** Conversational, relaxed, or emotionally charged.
- **How it feels:** It feels like a two-step process (_first_ you go, _then_ you do). Because it is wordier, it slows down the sentence and adds emphasis.
- **The Sentiment:** It often carries emotional weight—like warmth in an invitation, or irritation in a complaint.
- **Example 1 (Warm):** _"Come on, let's go and have a drink."_ (Feels friendly and inviting).
- **Example 2 (Annoyed):** _"He went and lost his keys again."_ (Feels critical or exasperated).

### 🏢 **Go to do something**

- **The Vibe:** Deliberate, professional, and serious.
- **How it feels:** It feels highly intentional. The word "to" highlights the strict purpose or objective of the movement. It sounds like an official plan rather than a casual whim.
- **The Sentiment:** Objective, detached, and grammatically proper.
- **Example:** _"I will go to meet the client."_ (Feels like a formal business task).

## grammar

Here are the technical grammatical rules, limitations, and mechanics that dictate how these three structures actually function.

### 🧩 **1. Go do something (Bare Infinitive)**

This structure links two verbs directly without a conjunction or particle.

- **The Mechanic:** The second verb must be in its base form (bare infinitive).
- **The Tense Trap:** This structure is grammatically restricted. You can **only** use it in the base form (imperatives, present tense plural, or after modal verbs).
- **Past Tense Rule:** You **cannot** use this in the past tense.
  - _Correct:_ "I always **go shop** there."
  - _Incorrect:_ "Yesterday, I **went shop** there." ❌
- **Third-Person Singular Rule:** It sounds highly unnatural or incorrect in the third-person present tense.
  - _Incorrect:_ "He **goes change** his clothes." ❌

### 🔗 **2. Go and do something (Coordinated Verbs)**

This structure uses the conjunction "and" to link "go" with a second action, treating them as a sequential pair.

- **The Mechanic:** Both verbs must mirror each other and share the **exact same tense or form**.
- **The Past Tense Solution:** Unlike "go do," this structure works perfectly in the past tense because both verbs inflect.
  - _Correct:_ "We **went and watched** the movie." (Both are past tense).
- **Third-Person Singular:** It works smoothly in the present tense for all subjects.
  - _Correct:_ "She **goes and checks** the mail every morning." (Both verbs take the "-s").
- **The "And" Erasure:** In fast spoken English (especially American), the "and" often gets reduced to a simple "n'" sound (_"go-n-get"_).

### 🎯 **3. Go to do something (Infinitive of Purpose)**

This is the textbook-standard grammatical structure for expressing intention.

- **The Mechanic:** "Go" acts as the main verb of movement, and "to + \[verb]" acts as an adverbial phrase explaining _why_ the movement happens (in order to do something).
- **The Ultimate Flex:** This structure has zero grammatical limitations. It works flawlessly across every single aspect, tense, and subject.
  - _Past:_ "They **went to fix** the car."
  - _Future:_ "I **will go to buy** groceries."
  - _Continuous:_ "She **is going to meet** her doctor."
- **The "Gonna" Reduction:** In informal spoken English, "going to" frequently contracts to **"gonna"** (_"I'm gonna see a movie"_).
