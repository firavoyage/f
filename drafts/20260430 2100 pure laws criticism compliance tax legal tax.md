can a site legally fail wcag if they intentionally dont serve the disabled due to its product type

No, a website cannot legally fail accessibility standards (WCAG) simply because it claims its products are not intended for disabled users. Under laws like the **Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)** in the U.S., websites are considered "places of public accommodation," and businesses must provide equal access to their goods and services regardless of the nature of the product.

Why "Product Type" Is Not a Valid Defense

- **Broad Legal Scope**: The ADA and similar laws (like California's [Unruh Civil Rights Act](https://silktide.com/glossary/web-accessibility-laws-in-california/)) prohibit discrimination in the "full and equal enjoyment" of goods and services. Even if a product seems physically impossible for a certain group to use, the _information_ about that product and the _process_ of purchasing it must still be accessible.
- **Intentional Exclusion**: Deliberately failing to meet [WCAG](https://www.levelaccess.com/blog/ada-vs-section-508-whats-the-difference/) standards because you do not intend to serve disabled users can be viewed by courts as **intentional discrimination**, which carries significantly higher risks for damages and penalties.
- **Diverse Needs**: Users with disabilities access sites for many reasons beyond directly using a product, such as researching for someone else, checking prices, or viewing safety information. Inaccessible features like poor color contrast or missing alt-text for images create illegal barriers to this information.

Exceptions and Nuances

While the baseline is that your website must be accessible, there are rare, specific legal exceptions:

- **Undue Burden**: A business may be exempt if making a site accessible would cause "significant difficulty or expense". However, this is a very high legal bar and is evaluated on a case-by-case basis; it is almost never granted just because a business "doesn't want" to serve a specific demographic.
- **Fundamental Alteration**: You aren't required to make changes that would fundamentally change the nature of your service. However, making a website navigable for a screen reader or keyboard-only user rarely qualifies as a "fundamental alteration" of a product's nature.
- **Alternative Accommodations**: In some jurisdictions, if a website is inaccessible but the business provides an **equally effective alternative** (like a 24/7 staffed customer service line that can perform every function of the site), they may have a defense, though this is increasingly being challenged in court.

Consequences of Non-Compliance

Failing to meet accessibility standards like WCAG 2.1 Level AA can lead to:

- **Federal Fines**: Initial violations can reach up to **$75,000–$115,231**, with repeat offenses costing up to **$150,000–$230,464**.
- **Private Lawsuits**: Over 4,600 digital accessibility lawsuits were filed in 2023 alone. Plaintiffs often seek mandatory site remediation and the payment of their attorney's fees.
- **Reputational Damage**: Publicly failing to provide inclusive access can lead to significant brand harm and loss of potential customers.

what does it mean for like a video game

For video games, accessibility laws typically distinguish between the **website/storefront** where you buy the game and the **actual gameplay** itself. While the core "gameplay" has historically had more legal flexibility, specific parts of the software are strictly regulated.

1\. Storefronts and Web Interfaces

The [Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)](https://www.audioeye.com/post/does-my-website-have-to-be-ada-compliant/) and similar laws treat game websites and e-commerce platforms as "places of public accommodation".

- **Legal Standing**: If a game is sold through a website or an in-game store, that interface must generally follow WCAG 2.1 Level AA.
- **Common Failures**: Issues like low color contrast, missing [alt-text for item images](https://reciteme.com/us/news/6-most-common-wcag-failures/), or buttons that can't be used with a keyboard are illegal barriers to making a purchase.
- **Purchasing Rights**: The ADA applies to web or in-app purchases of full games, as well as unlocking new features or items.

2\. Communication Features (CVAA)

Unlike general gameplay, any communication system within a game is strictly regulated by the [21st Century Communications and Video Accessibility Act (CVAA)](https://vispero.com/resources/cvaa-what-you-need-to-know-about-video-accessibility/).

- **Mandatory Accessibility**: Any game released after January 1, 2019, that includes **voice chat, text chat, or video conferencing** must make these features accessible.
- **Requirements**: This includes providing [text-to-speech for chat](https://www.fcc.gov/consumers/guides/accessibility-communications-video-games), adjustable font sizes for messages, and ensuring users can navigate to these settings using assistive technology.
- **Fines**: Non-compliance with CVAA can lead to fines of up to **$100,000 per violation**.

3\. Core Gameplay and "Fundamental Alteration"

The actual mechanics of a game (e.g., jumping, aiming, solving puzzles) are often protected from strict WCAG mandates if accessibility would "fundamentally alter" the experience.

- **The "Challenge" Defense**: Courts generally recognize that a game’s core value is its challenge. Requiring a developer to remove that challenge (like making a reflex-based shooter playable without reflexes) might be seen as a "fundamental alteration".
- **Achievability**: Developers must still implement accessibility features that are "achievable" without undue burden, such as [subtitles for dialogue](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/uwp/gaming/accessibility-for-games), colorblind modes, or [remappable controls](https://www.levelaccess.com/blog/three-ways-to-level-up-video-game-accessibility/).

4\. Educational Games

If a video game is used in a **classroom or for public education**, the legal bar is much higher. Under laws like [Section 508](https://www.filamentgames.com/blog/accessibility-in-educational-games-what-the-law-requires-and-what-comes-next/) and IDEA, these games _must_ provide accommodations so all students can participate meaningfully in the learning activity.

The reality is that **nearly 95% of homepages fail** automatically detectable [WCAG 2 standards](https://webaim.org/projects/million/). While the law is strict, enforcement is largely reactive, driven by a surge in private lawsuits rather than proactive government inspections.

1\. The Statistical Reality

Inaccessibility is the default state of the internet, not the exception.

- **Massive Failure Rates**: Studies of the top one million websites consistently find that [approximately 95–96% of homepages](https://beaccessible.com/post/web-accessibility-statistics/) have at least one detectable accessibility failure.
- **Density of Errors**: The average homepage contains [about 51 distinct accessibility errors](https://webaim.org/projects/million/2025).
- **The "Six Errors" Problem**: Just six recurring issues—led by [low contrast text](https://testparty.ai/blog/wcag-22-compliance-statistics-how-many-sites-pass) and missing alt text—account for 96% of all detected errors.

2\. Why Violations Persist

Many businesses remain non-compliant due to a mix of high costs, technical complexity, and misinformation.

- **Cost of Remediation**: A full manual audit and professional remediation for a single site can [easily exceed $20,000](https://www.quora.com/Why-do-the-majority-of-websites-fail-to-comply-with-WCAG-accessibility-guidelines).
- **Complexity Growth**: Modern websites are becoming more complex (averaging over 1,200 elements per page), making manual accessibility upkeep a moving target.
- **The "Overlay" Trap**: Many companies use "accessibility overlays" (automated plugins) that claim instant compliance. In reality, [over 30% of web accessibility lawsuits](https://www.accessibility.works/blog/only-5-percent-websites-pass-wcag-ada-eaa-compliance/) target sites that already use these widgets.

3\. Lawsuit Trends (Reactive Enforcement)

Because there is no "accessibility police" proactively checking every site, enforcement happens through the courts.

- **Volume**: Over [5,100 digital accessibility lawsuits](https://accessibe.com/blog/knowledgebase/ada-website-lawsuits) were filed in 2025 alone.
- **Targeted Industries**: E-commerce is the primary target, accounting for [nearly 70% of accessibility lawsuits](https://www.audioeye.com/post/accessibility-statistics/).
- **State Hotspots**: California and New York lead in litigation because their state laws allow plaintiffs to claim [statutory damages (e.g., $4,000 per violation in California)](https://www.adacompliancepros.com/blog/what-happens-if-your-business-ignores-wcag-guidelines-legal-risks-explained) in addition to attorney's fees.

4\. New Federal Deadlines (2027-2028)

While private businesses under **ADA Title III** have lived in a "gray area" of when they must comply, **ADA Title II** (government and public entities) now has firm deadlines. The Department of Justice recently established that these entities must meet WCAG 2.1 Level AA by **April 2027** (for populations over 50,000) or **April 2028** (for smaller groups). This is expected to set a new baseline for how courts judge private businesses as well.

While WCAG is the global benchmark for accessibility, it faces significant criticism from developers, legal experts, and the disability community regarding its complexity, the way it is legally enforced, and its failure to guarantee a "good" user experience.

1\. Conformance

Usability

One of the most frequent criticisms is that a website can technically meet all WCAG success criteria while remaining difficult or frustrating to use.

- **The "Checklist" Trap**: Critics argue that developers often treat WCAG as a rigid technical checklist rather than focusing on real-world inclusive design.
- **Technical Compliance vs. User Experience**: A site might have "proper" [alt-text for images](https://www.boia.org/blog/why-wcag-conformance-doesnt-mean-perfectly-accessible), but if that text is excessively long or keyword-stuffed for SEO, it creates a miserable experience for screen reader users.
- **Low Contrast Inaccuracies**: Research shows the current [WCAG 2.2 color contrast algorithm](https://blog.logrocket.com/ux-design/wcag-3-vs-2-ux/) is surprisingly inaccurate; studies suggest 47% of colors that "pass" are actually inaccessible to many users, while 23% of those that "fail" are perfectly readable.

2\. Legal "Shakedown" Culture

Critics and defense attorneys often target the way WCAG is used in the U.S. legal system, particularly under the **Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)**.

- **Predatory Litigation**: Because the ADA doesn't have official technical rules, plaintiffs' firms often use "cookie-cutter" lawsuits to target small businesses that lack the resources to defend themselves, seeking quick settlements.
- **Lack of Uniform Standards**: The Department of Justice (DOJ) has never formally codified WCAG into law for private businesses, creating a [legal "gray area"](https://www.webability.io/blog/web-accessibility-lawsuits) where expectations vary wildly between different court circuits.
- **The "Overlay" Myth**: In response to legal pressure, many businesses use automated accessibility widgets or "overlays." However, [25% of accessibility lawsuits in 2024](https://www.accessibility.works/blog/ada-lawsuit-trends-statistics-2024-summary/) specifically cited these widgets as being barriers themselves rather than solutions.

3\. Complexity and Cost

For developers and organizations, the sheer density of the guidelines is a major barrier.

- **Steep Learning Curve**: WCAG documentation is often criticized for being overly technical, cryptic, and lacking the practical code examples developers need.
- **Prohibitive Costs**: A full professional manual audit and remediation can [exceed $20,000](https://www.quora.com/Why-do-the-majority-of-websites-fail-to-comply-with-WCAG-accessibility-guidelines) per site, leading many organizations to settle for "bare minimum" fixes rather than true inclusion.
- **Inconsistent Tooling**: Different browsers and assistive technologies interpret accessibility code differently, forcing developers to solve the same problem multiple times.

4\. Gaps in Disability Coverage

Some disability advocates argue that the current standards leave certain groups behind.

- **Cognitive and Learning Disabilities**: Critics suggest earlier versions of WCAG (2.0 and 2.1) did not sufficiently address the needs of users with ADHD, dyslexia, or memory impairments.
- **Deaf Community Needs**: Current standards often make sign language support optional (Level AAA), which many advocates feel is a failure of basic access.
- **Mobile and Touch**: Older standards were built for desktop browsers; the move to mobile has highlighted gaps in how target sizes and touch interactions are governed.

criticism on Americans with Disabilities Act

Criticism of the **Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)** often focuses on its economic impact, enforcement limitations, and perceived unintended consequences regarding employment. While hailed as a landmark civil rights law, critics from both business and disability advocacy circles have raised concerns about its implementation and effectiveness since its passage in 1990.

Economic and Business Concerns

- **Costs of Compliance**: Business groups, particularly those representing small businesses like the [National Federation of Independent Business](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-policy-history/article/examining-the-opposition-to-the-americans-with-disabilities-act-of-1990-nothing-more-than-bad-quality-hogwash/DA3446971540A36870AFAAD781350DD2), initially argued that the costs of structural modifications—such as installing elevators or ramps—could lead to bankruptcy.
- **Vague Terminology**: Critics have frequently targeted terms like "reasonable accommodation" and "undue hardship," arguing they are [vague and legally ambiguous](https://scholarship.law.vanderbilt.edu/vlr/vol52/iss3/4/), leaving businesses in a state of uncertainty until forced into court to define them.
- **Litigation Burden**: A common criticism is that the ADA has spawned a "bottomless pit" of litigation. Some refer to "drive-by" or "tester" lawsuits, where individuals file numerous claims over minor violations (like incorrect signage or mirror height) to extract settlements from small businesses.

Unintended Employment Effects

- **Negative Impact on Hiring**: Some economic studies, such as those from the [Cato Institute](https://www.cato.org/sites/cato.org/files/serials/files/regulation/2000/4/deleire.pdf), suggest that the ADA may have perversely reduced employment for people with disabilities. The argument is that the threat of lawsuits and the potential cost of accommodations make employers more hesitant to hire disabled candidates in the first place.
- **Persistent Employment Gap**: Despite the law, the employment rate for people with disabilities has often remained stagnant or declined compared to non-disabled peers, leading to claims that the ADA has [failed its primary goal](https://cepr.net/publications/solidarity-beyond-the-ada-an-international-look-at-disabled-employment/) of labor market integration.

Advocacy and Systemic Critiques

- **Enforcement Burden**: Disability rights advocates criticize the fact that the ADA relies almost entirely on [private litigation for enforcement](https://www.damemagazine.com/2025/07/25/the-promise-and-failure-of-the-american-disabilities-act/). This places the financial and emotional burden on the individual with a disability to sue for their basic rights, rather than having a government agency proactively ensure compliance.
- **Gaps in Coverage**: Critics point out significant gaps, such as the lack of clear federal regulations for [digital and web accessibility](https://lawliberty.org/americans-with-disabilities-act-an-epic-tragedy-of-good-intentions/), which has led to a surge in confusing and inconsistent court rulings for online businesses.
- **Narrow Judicial Interpretations**: For years, the Supreme Court was criticized for narrowly interpreting the definition of "disability," which disqualified many people with chronic conditions from receiving protections until the [ADA Amendments Act of 2008](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Americans%5Fwith%5FDisabilities%5FAct%5Fof%5F1990) was passed to broaden the law.

Criticisms of tax compliance often center on the extreme complexity and high costs that burden both individuals and businesses. These requirements are frequently cited as being outdated and poorly matched to modern life.

- **High Compliance Costs**: In 2024, the total cost of tax compliance in the U.S. was estimated at **$546 billion**, representing approximately **2% of the national GDP**.

Impact on Small Businesses
