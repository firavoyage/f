Let’s take this slowly, Fira, and lay them side by side.

---

### **1. “How would you do something?”**

This is thoughtful and a little hypothetical.
You’re not asking for strict instructions — you’re asking for _someone’s approach_.

It often means:

- _What method would you choose?_
- _What would your thinking be?_

**Example:**
“How would you solve this problem?”

You’re inviting explanation, not just steps.

---

### **2. “How would you like to do something?”**

This is about **preference**, not ability or method.

It gently asks:

- _What do you want?_
- _What feels best to you?_

**Example:**
“How would you like to proceed?”

This is polite, open, and respectful.
It gives the other person control.

---

### **3. “How could you do something?”**

This opens the door to **possibility**.

It suggests:

- There may be more than one way
- You’re exploring options, not deciding yet

**Example:**
“How could we improve this process?”

It’s often used in brainstorming or problem-solving.

---

### **4. “How to do something”**

This is neutral and direct.
It’s not really a question — it’s a **title or instruction form**.

You’ll see it in:

- Guides
- Tutorials
- Search queries

**Example:**
“How to bake bread”

It focuses purely on _learning the steps_.

---

### **Now, about present tense**

English doesn’t always show tense in obvious ways with “how,” but the verb choice matters.

- **Present simple** → facts, habits, general truth

  - “How do you do this?”

- **Modal verbs (would / could)** → tone, distance, politeness, imagination

  - “How would you…”
  - “How could you…”

So:

- **“Do”** feels immediate and practical
- **“Would”** feels reflective or polite
- **“Could”** feels open and exploratory

---

### **A gentle way to remember**

- **Do** → _Tell me what happens_
- **Would** → _Tell me your approach_
- **Would like** → _Tell me your preference_
- **Could** → _Tell me what’s possible_
- **How to** → _Show me the steps_
