No, it is not always "stupid" to buy both sides of a market at different proportions; in fact, it can be a mathematically sound strategy for **guaranteed profit**, **hedging**, or **market making**. While buying only one side offers higher potential returns, balancing both sides serves specific strategic purposes.

1\. Pure Arbitrage (Risk-Free Profit)

There are moments when the sum of the "Yes" and "No" prices on [Polymarket](https://polymarket.com/) equals less than $1.00.

- **The Logic**: Since every binary contract resolves to exactly $1.00 for the winner and $0.00 for the loser, buying both sides for a combined cost of, say, $0.98 guarantees a $0.02 profit (approx. 2%) regardless of the outcome.
- **Competition**: These opportunities are often short-lived as [high-frequency trading bots](https://medium.com/illumination-curated/how-to-win-polymarket-bets-safely-with-and-without-bots-68096055ddfc) monitor order books to snap up these mispricings instantly.

2\. Strategic Hedging

Buying both sides at different proportions allows you to manage risk as new information emerges.

- **Locking in Gains**: If you bought "Yes" at $0.30 and the price rises to $0.80, buying some "No" shares acts as a hedge. This reduces your maximum potential profit but ensures you don't walk away empty-handed if the event unexpectedly flips.
- **Bankroll Protection**: Significant winners often sacrifice some [expected value (EV)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HAlYMtluzCk) by hedging to protect their total bankroll from catastrophic loss.

3\. Market Making and Liquidity Rewards

Users often place orders on both sides to act as market makers.

- **Spread Capture**: By placing a "Buy" order slightly below the current price and a "Sell" order slightly above, you can profit from the "spread" as other traders move the price back and forth.
- **Platform Incentives**: Some platforms, including Polymarket, offer rewards for providing liquidity (keeping the order book "thick"), which can make dual-sided trading profitable even if the trades themselves just break even.

4\. Cross-Market Dependencies

Sometimes buying "Yes" on one market and "No" on a logically related market can reveal "hidden" arbitrage.

- **Example**: If Market A is "Will Trump win Pennsylvania?" and Market B is "Will Republicans win Pennsylvania by 5+ points?", these outcomes are logically linked. If their prices don't reflect this dependency, buying specific combinations across both can create a mathematically superior position than a single-sided bet.

You are essentially paying for the privilege of holding a position that cannot win, which is why professionals only do this when there is a specific structural or tax-related reason.

1\. The "Automatic Loss" from Transaction Fees

While holding 1 "Yes" and 1 "No" share technically equals $1.00, getting into those positions is not free.

- **Taker Fees**: Most markets on [Polymarket](https://polymarket.com/) now charge "taker" fees (up to **1.80%** for crypto and **1.00%** for politics/finance).
- **The Double Hit**: If you buy both sides, you pay fees on both transactions. If the fee is 1% on each side, you’ve spent $1.02 to hold a contract that will only ever be worth $1.00.
- **Fee-Free Exceptions**: This "guaranteed loss" only disappears in [Geopolitics or World Events](https://help.polymarket.com/en/articles/13364478-trading-fees) categories, which remain fee-free.

2\. The 2026 "Phantom Income" Tax Trap

A massive risk in buying both sides is how they are taxed. If your activity is characterized as **gambling** (which often happens with sports or outcome-based bets), you face a major problem starting in 2026:

- **The 90% Loss Cap**: Under new rules (like the [One Big Beautiful Bill Act](https://camusocpa.com/prediction-market-loss-deductions/)), you may only be allowed to deduct up to **90% of your losses** against your winnings.
- **Result**: If you "break even" by winning $1,000 on "Yes" but losing $1,000 on "No," you could still be taxed on **$100 of "phantom income"** because you can't fully deduct that $1,000 loss. You end up owing taxes on money you never actually made.

3\. When It Is _Not_ Stupid

Despite these costs, traders do this in two specific scenarios:

- **Arbitrage**: If "Yes" is $0.48 and "No" is $0.50, the total is $0.98. Even with a 0.5% fee on each side, you might still clear a tiny profit.

If you are a maker on both sides, you are getting paid to provide liquidity, regardless of which way the market moves.

1\. The Mirror Order Book Barrier

On [Polymarket](https://polymarket.com/), "Yes" and "No" are not separate assets; they are two halves of the same $1.00 contract.

- **The Math**: The system enforces the rule that **Yes + No = 1** through a "shared order book."
- **The Trap**: If you see "Yes" at $0.40 and "No" at $0.40 and try to buy both, you are actually bidding $0.40 for "Yes" and $0.60 for the _other_ side of "No".
- **Result**: The system will automatically match your order against itself or other users at a price that ensures you don't find a "cheap" bundle. You simply cannot buy both for less than $1.00 on a single liquid market.

2\. Double Transaction Friction

Even if you manage to buy both sides for exactly $1.00, you are still "stupid" from a profit perspective because you pay to play:

- **Fees**: You pay trading fees on **both** transactions. If fees are 1% per side, you spend $1.02 to hold a bundle that will only ever pay out $1.00.
- **Opportunity Cost**: You are locking up your capital in a position that has a **negative expected return** (losing exactly the amount of the fees) while missing out on other actual opportunities.

3\. Risk of "Wash Trading" Bans

Doing this repeatedly can get you banned.

- **Rule Violation**: Buying and selling the same contract to create artificial volume without taking a real market position is considered **wash trading**.
- **Sanctions**: Under [Polymarket's 2026 Integrity Rules](https://polymarketexchange.com/files/legal/latest/rulebook), accounts flagged for this behavior can face **wallet blocking**, **monetary penalties**, or **referral to regulators** like the NFA.
