That’s a good, quiet question, Fira.

**Transcript** and **transcription** are closely related, but they are not the same thing.

Think of it this way:

**Transcription** is the _process_.
It is the act of listening to spoken words and writing them down.

**Transcript** is the _result_.
It is the written text that comes out of that process.

So:

- You _do_ a **transcription**
- You _get_ a **transcript**

A simple example:
If someone records a meeting, and you listen to the audio and type everything that was said, you are doing **transcription**.
The finished document you hand in is the **transcript**.

One is the journey.
The other is what you carry home.

Ah, I see what you’re pointing to, Fira.
When we talk about **subtitle** or **caption**, the meaning shifts a little.

Here’s a gentle way to sort them out.

**Transcription** (in this context)
This is still the _process_. It means converting spoken audio into written text.
When you transcribe a video, the words you type can later be used for subtitles or captions.

**Transcript**
This is the _full written text_ of what is said, usually as a document.
It’s often plain text, not timed to the video.
You might read it, print it, or search through it.

Now, **subtitles** and **captions** are _formats_, not processes.

**Subtitles**

- Text shown on screen, synced with the video
- Usually meant for viewers who can hear the audio
- Often focus on spoken dialogue
- May translate the speech into another language

**Captions**

- Also shown on screen and synced with the video
- Designed for viewers who cannot hear the audio
- Include dialogue _and_ important sounds
  (like _[music playing]_ or _[door slams]_)

So in simple terms:

- **Transcription** → the act of writing speech as text
- **Transcript** → the written text itself
- **Subtitles/Captions** → timed, on-screen versions made from that text

A transcript is like a calm, complete record.
Subtitles and captions are like guides that walk beside the video, moment by moment.
