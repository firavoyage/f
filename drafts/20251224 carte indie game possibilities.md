---

That’s a beautiful question, Fira.
It already tells me you’re looking beyond _how_ to make games, and into _why_ they exist.

Games are not one thing.
They are a _space_ where many things can happen.

At the simplest level, a game can be **play**.
Movement. Curiosity. Joy. A small world that responds when someone touches it.
That alone is powerful — a living thing made of rules.

But games can also be **stories you don’t just watch, but inhabit**.
Not a tale told _to_ someone, but one that unfolds _with_ them.
The player’s choices become part of the meaning.
Sometimes the silence between actions says more than dialogue ever could.

Games can be **systems**.
Tiny universes where ideas collide:

- What happens when resources are scarce?
- What happens when trust is rewarded?
- What happens when kindness is optional?

In games, you don’t argue ideas — you _let people feel them_.

They can be **emotional spaces**.
A game can hold grief gently.
It can express loneliness without a single word.
It can make someone feel seen, or less alone, at 2 a.m., when nothing else helps.

Games can be **poetry made of interaction**.
Short. Strange. Abstract.
No score. No winning. Just a moment that lingers.
Some of the most creative games don’t explain themselves — they _invite_.

They can also be **tools for imagination**:

- Teaching without lectures
- Healing without claiming to
- Letting people try on different lives safely

And of course, games can be **fun chaos** — silly, loud, fast, playful.
That matters too. Joy is not shallow. It’s necessary.

The real possibility of games is this:
They are one of the few mediums where _rules create meaning_.
You don’t just tell players what matters — you show them through what the world allows, encourages, or forbids.

If you want to be amazing and creative, don’t rush to impress.
Ask quiet questions instead:

- What do I want someone to _feel_?
- What kind of world do I want to build rules for?

# 3

i have an idea of a card game. how could i make it unique.

---

That’s a good place to be, Fira — right at the edge where an idea wants to become _itself_.

Most card games are not remembered because of how many cards they have,
but because of **what they ask the player to care about**.

To make your card game unique, don’t start by adding more mechanics.
Start by listening to the _heart_ of it.

Here are some gentle ways to approach uniqueness.

First, ask a quiet question:
**What is this game _really_ about?**
Not the theme on the surface, but the feeling underneath.

- Is it about risk?
- Trust?
- Patience?
- Control?
- Letting go?

When you know that, every rule can serve it.

Second, let the **cards mean more than numbers**.
A card can be:

- a memory
- a promise
- a lie
- a burden
- a secret

If playing a card feels like _doing_ something meaningful, not just optimizing, players will remember it.

Third, consider **unusual costs**.
Most games ask players to spend mana, health, or turns.
But what if the cost is:

- revealing information
- helping an opponent
- permanently losing a card
- changing the rules of the game itself

Costs shape emotion. Emotion creates identity.

Fourth, think about **what players cannot do**.
Limitations are powerful.

- Maybe players can’t hold many cards.
- Maybe they must speak when playing certain cards.
- Maybe they don’t fully understand their own deck.

Confusion, when intentional, can be poetic.

Fifth, let the game **change as it’s played**.
A game that remembers is rare.

- Cards that evolve
- Rules that unlock or disappear
- Decks that scar over time

Even small changes can make a game feel alive.

Sixth, ask yourself:
**What choice do I want players to struggle with?**
The most interesting games don’t ask “what’s optimal?”
They ask “what kind of person are you in this moment?”

And finally — and this matters —
you don’t need to be original in _everything_.
You only need **one honest twist** that you care about deeply.

When you imagine someone playing your card game for the first time…
