- **Solve your own problems:** Look for issues you face in your daily life or work. If you have a problem, others likely do too.
- **Start small:** Focus on a niche or a small feature rather than a massive platform. A small, focused product is easier to build and market.
- **Don't worry about originality:** Execution matters more than the idea itself. You don't need to be the first; you just need to be better or different.
- **Look for trends:** Identify growing markets or technologies and build tools that support them (e.g., tools for remote workers, crypto, or creators).

### **Phase 2: Building (MVP)**

- **Build an MVP (Minimum Viable Product):** Create the simplest version of your product that solves the core problem. Avoid "feature creep."
- **Use tools you know:** Don't waste time learning a new tech stack if you don't have to. Use whatever allows you to build fastest (e.g., WordPress, no-code tools, simple HTML/CSS).
- **Timebox your development:** Set a strict deadline (e.g., 2 weeks or 1 month) to launch the first version. This forces you to focus on essentials.
- **Do it yourself:** Learn the basics of design, development, and marketing. Being a "jack of all trades" is a superpower for bootstrappers.

### **Phase 3: Launching**

- **Launch early:** Don't wait for perfection. Launch as soon as the product is usable to get feedback.
- **Launch on relevant platforms:** Submit your project to Product Hunt, Hacker News, Reddit, and relevant niche communities.
- **Tell a story:** When launching, don't just list features. Explain _why_ you built it and _who_ it is for. Be authentic and personal.
- **Don't rely on a "big bang":** A launch is just the beginning. Focus on getting your first 10, then 100, then 1,000 users.

### **Phase 4: Growing**

- **Do things that don't scale:** Manually recruit users, send personal emails, and gather direct feedback.
- **Build in public:** Share your progress, revenue, successes, and failures on social media (like Twitter/X) and blogs. This builds trust and an audience.
- **Content marketing:** Write articles or guides related to your product's niche. Share valuable information that attracts your target audience.

### **Phase 5: Monetization**

- **Charge from day one:** Validate your idea by asking for money. If people aren't willing to pay, you might not have a business.
- **Experiment with pricing models:** Try subscriptions (SaaS), one-time payments, or freemium models. Find what works for your specific product.
- **Focus on revenue, not just users:** Vanity metrics (like signups) don't pay the bills. Focus on profitability and sustainable growth.

### **Phase 6: Automating & Scaling**

- **Automate repetitive tasks:** Use scripts or tools (like Zapier) to handle emails, user onboarding, and other manual processes.
- **Hire contractors for specific tasks:** Once you have revenue, hire freelancers for tasks you're bad at or that take up too much time (e.g., accounting, legal, advanced design).
- **Keep the team small:** Avoid hiring full-time employees until absolutely necessary to keep overhead low and maintain agility.
- **Exit or keep running:** Decide if you want to sell the project (exit) or keep running it as a cash cow. Both are valid paths.
