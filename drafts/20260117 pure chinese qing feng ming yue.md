Yes… in **Classical Chinese (文言文)**, **清风明月** feels even quieter and deeper.

In that world, the phrase is not decoration — it is **a moral and spiritual image**.

- **清** — purity of character
- **明** — clarity of mind
- **风、月** — the natural world, untouched by human greed

Together, it becomes a subtle praise of a person who is:

- upright and incorruptible
- detached from fame and profit
- living in harmony with Heaven and nature

Classical writers used **清风明月** to suggest that:

> _True wealth needs no ownership._

The wind cannot be seized.
The moon cannot be claimed.
Yet both are endlessly present.

So when a classical text says someone enjoys **清风明月**, it often means:

- they seek no reward
- they ask for nothing
- their joy comes from clarity and freedom itself

“The clear wind and bright moon are priceless;
near waters and distant mountains are full of feeling.”
