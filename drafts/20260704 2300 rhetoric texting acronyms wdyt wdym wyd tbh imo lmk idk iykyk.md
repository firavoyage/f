**WDYT** is a common internet acronym that stands for <mark><strong>"What Do You Think?"</strong></mark>. [link](https://www.collinsdictionary.com/us/submission/404/wdyt)

It is primarily used in text messages, social media, and casual workplace chats to ask for someone's opinion, feedback, or advice on a specific topic.

### Common Ways to Use It

- **To get an opinion on a choice:** "I'm thinking of buying this jacket, wdyt?"
- **To check in on a suggestion:** "Let's grab pizza for dinner tonight, wdyt?"
- **To get feedback on an idea:** "I might change my profile picture to this one. Wdyt?"

### Similar Texting Acronyms

- **WDYM**: What Do You Mean? (Used to ask for clarification).
- **WYD**: What You Doing? (Used to check what someone is up to).

## the history of these

the history of these

7:57 AM

The history of internet acronyms like **WDYT**, **IMO**, and **BRB** is a fascinating story of technology forcing humans to change how they write. While it feels like a modern trend, the practice of shortening words actually spans three distinct historical eras. [link](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SMS%5Flanguage)

***

### 1. Pre-Internet: The Spiritual Ancestors (1800s–1970s)

Long before computers, humans were already abbreviating phrases to save time and money. [link](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SMS%5Flanguage)

- **The Telegraph (1800s):** Telegraph operators were charged by the word. To save cash, they invented a shorthand called "telegraphese". They even used numbers as abbreviations, like "73" for "best regards." [link](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SMS%5Flanguage)
- **Military & Corporate Acronyms (1900s):** Many phrases we use online today actually predated computers. For example, according to historical language records, **"OMG"** was first used in a letter written to Winston Churchill in 1917, and **"FYI"** originated in military and business memos around 1941. [link](https://www.salon.com/2014/08/03/the%5Ftruth%5Fabout%5Finternet%5Fslang%5Fit%5Fgoes%5Fway%5Fback/)

### 2. Early Web: The 10-Key and Dial-Up Era (1980s–1990s)

The modern explosion of internet slang began with early tech hobbyists. [link](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet%5Fslang)

- **Usenet & BBS (1980s):** On early electronic bulletin boards (BBS) and [Usenet networks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Usenet), users had to type on clumsy terminals with incredibly slow dial-up speeds. Acronyms like **"IMHO"** (In My Humble Opinion) were coined during this decade to drastically reduce keystrokes. In fact, "IMHO" was officially printed in a PC Magazine dictionary of email acronyms as early as 1986. [link](https://www.yourdictionary.com/articles/imho-explained)
- **Chat Rooms & AIM (1990s):** With the rise of AOL Instant Messenger (AIM) and IRC chat rooms, conversations happened in lightning-fast, real-time text. If you didn't type quickly, the conversation would move past you. This gave birth to foundational survival acronyms like **"BRB"** (Be Right Back), **"LOL"** (Laughing Out Loud), and **"G2G"** (Got To Go). [link](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0O7FwDskwbY\&t=10)

### 3. Mobile Era: The Character Limit (2000s–Present)

When communication shifted to cell phones, the restrictions became literal hardware limitations. [link](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SMS%5Flanguage)

- **The 160-Character Limit:** Early SMS text messages capped every single text at exactly **160 characters**. If you went over by one letter, mobile carriers would charge you for two separate text messages. [link](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SMS%5Flanguage)
- **T9 Predictive Text:** Before touchscreens, people typed by repeatedly clicking the numbers 1 through 9 on physical phone keypads. Typing out "What do you think?" required clicking buttons dozens of times. Shorthand like **WDYT**, **WDYM**, and **WYD** allowed people to ask complex questions in a fraction of a second. [link](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SMS%5Flanguage)

***

### Where It Stands Today

Interestingly, linguistic researchers note that internet slang has shifted from a **functional necessity** into a **cultural dial**. Now that we have unlimited smartphone data and keyboards that predict full words automatically, we do not _need_ to use acronyms to save money or time. [link](https://www.quora.com/When-did-texting-become-a-common-form-of-communication-and-what-is-the-reason-for-the-use-of-abbreviations-in-text-messaging)

Instead, we use them to control the **tone** of our writing. Typing "What do you think?" can sound formal or demanding in a casual text thread, whereas typing "wdyt" signals that the conversation is relaxed, low-stakes, and friendly.
