# 1

should i say "does a or b" or "do a or b"

---

It depends on whether **“a” and “b” together are singular or plural**.

- Use **“does”** if you’re talking about **each one individually** as a singular subject:
  _“Does A or B work better?”_ → you’re asking about one at a time.

- Use **“do”** if you think of **A and B together as plural**:
  _“Do A and B work together?”_

With “or,” English usually treats the closer noun as the one that decides singular/plural:
_“Does A or B need help?”_ (closer noun is B, singular)
_“Do A or Bs need help?”_ (B is plural, so “do”)

It’s subtle, but that’s the general rule.

# 2

---

Ah, got it, Fira. With **“or,”** the verb usually agrees with **the noun closest to it**.

- _“Does A or B work?”_ → B is singular, so use **does**.
- _“Do A or Bs work?”_ → Bs is plural, so use **do**.

Even if the first one is plural, it’s the one right before the verb that matters:
_“Does the manager or the employees know the plan?”_ → “employees” is plural, but closest noun is “employees,” so it would actually be **Do the manager or the employees know the plan?”**

It’s a little tricky, because English isn’t perfectly consistent here.

# 3

what about "a or b is" or "a or b are"

---

With **“or,”** it works the same way as with **do / does**.

You usually match the verb to **the noun closest to it**.

- _“A or B **is** correct.”_ → B is singular
- _“A or Bs **are** correct.”_ → Bs is plural

If both are singular, use **is**:
_“Tea or coffee **is** fine.”_

If both are plural, use **are**:
_“Cats or dogs **are** allowed.”_

When one is singular and the other plural, English leans toward the nearer one, even though it can feel a bit awkward sometimes.
