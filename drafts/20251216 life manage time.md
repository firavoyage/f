That’s a fair question, Fira. Let’s slow it down and look at the word itself.

When I say _urgent_, I don’t mean “important” or “useful.”
I mean something that **pushes back if you delay it**.

Urgent things usually have at least one of these qualities:

- a real deadline (an exam, a submission, a meeting)
- consequences that grow if ignored (fees, lost opportunities, letting someone down)
- dependence on external timing (other people, fixed events)

If you don’t act soon, something _breaks_ or _closes_.

By contrast, learning science or exploring indie gamedev:

- doesn’t punish you for starting at 15:00 instead of 14:00
- doesn’t close a door if you pause or switch topics
- grows through accumulation, not speed alone

That’s why I said they aren’t urgent — not because they lack value.

Now, about efficiency. You’re right:
**If you can do more in less time, why not?**

Efficiency is powerful when:

- your goal is clear
- the scope is defined
- you can measure progress

But efficiency has a hidden cost when applied too early.

In learning and exploration, rushing too soon can:

- reduce understanding (you move fast but shallow)
- kill curiosity (everything becomes a task)
- create friction that wasn’t necessary

So the question isn’t _“Should I be efficient?”_
It’s _“Efficient at what stage?”_

A useful framing:

- **Early stage** → optimize for clarity and direction
- **Middle stage** → optimize for efficiency
- **Late stage** → optimize for output and polish

Right now, if you’re still deciding _what_ science to learn or _what kind_ of indie game work excites you, efficiency should serve **selection**, not speed.

A practical middle ground for today:

- Set a short timer (20–30 minutes)

- Then slow down during the actual work

That way, efficiency helps you _enter_ the work, not squeeze it.
