compare put and put away

The core difference between **"put"** and **"put away"** lies in the specificity of the final location: **"put"** requires a specific, explicitly stated location, while **"put away"** implies moving an object to its designated storage space out of sight. \[[1](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ukj3fflfyug), [2](https://www.collinsdictionary.com/us/dictionary/english/put), [3](https://www.facebook.com/groups/englishquestionsanswered/posts/2587982584821750/)]

Detailed Usage and Examples

"Put" (Base Verb)

Use **"put"** when you want to name the exact surface, container, or location where an object is going. The sentence will feel incomplete without a prepositional phrase like _on the table_, _in the car_, or _under the bed_. \[[1](https://www.collinsdictionary.com/us/dictionary/english/put)]

- _Incorrect_: "I took my keys and put." (Incomplete)
- _Correct_: "I **put** my keys **on the counter**."
- _Correct_: "Please **put** the milk **in the refrigerator**." \[[1](https://www.facebook.com/learn.english.with.maria.fernandez/videos/how-to-use-the-phrasal-verb-to-put-away-english-lesson/1097146470901527/)]

"Put Away" (Phrasal Verb)

Use **"put away"** when you are cleaning up or storing items in their usual, hidden storage places (like closets, drawers, or cabinets). Because the "proper place" is implied, you do not need to name the location. \[[1](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=q1NqcXatBw4\&t=1), [2](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ukj3fflfyug), [3](https://www.collinsdictionary.com/dictionary/english/put-away), [4](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=q3xQW8Tcdr8)]

- _Correct_: "The kids need to **put away** their toys." (Implies putting them in the toy box)
- _Correct_: "After shopping, I **put** the groceries **away**."
- _Note on Word Order_: Because it is a separable phrasal verb, you can place the noun in the middle (_put the clothes away_) or at the end (_put away the clothes_). However, if you use a pronoun like _it_ or _them_, it **must** go in the middle (_put them away_). \[[1](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=q1NqcXatBw4\&t=1), [2](https://forum.wordreference.com/threads/whats-difference-between-put-away-and-put-by.3005379/), [3](https://hinative.com/questions/26403747), [4](https://www.collinsdictionary.com/us/dictionary/english/put-away), [5](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CrlJx5o8BhA)]

word origin

The phrase **"put away"** comes from combining two Old English words that evolved over centuries to mean "storing something out of sight."

Here is the breakdown of its origins:

The Roots

- **Put:** Comes from the Old English word _putian_ (or _potian_), which meant "to thrust, push, or shove."
- **Away:** Comes from the Old English _onweg_, meaning "on the way" or "moving onward from a specific place."

The Evolution

- **14th Century:** The words merged into a phrase meaning to physically push something to the side or cast it out.
- **16th Century:** The meaning shifted to putting objects into their proper storage spaces.
- **19th Century:** Slang uses emerged, including "putting away" food (eating a lot) and "putting away" criminals (imprisonment).

1\. The Literal Foundation (1300s)

- _Historical Use:_ It was heavily used in early English Bibles to mean **divorce** (e.g., "to put away one's wife" meant to legally cast her out or dismiss her).

2\. The Storage Meaning (1500s)

By the late 16th century, the meaning shifted from "throwing something out" to **"storing something neatly."** People began using it to describe putting items into chests, closets, or cupboards so they were out of sight but safe.

3\. The Prison Slang (1800s)

In the 19th century, British and American underworld slang adapted the storage meaning. Criminals and police began using "put away" as a euphemism for **imprisonment**. The logic was that a criminal was being "stored away" in a cell, out of public sight. \[[1](https://www.ldoceonline.com/dictionary/put-away)]

4\. The Food Slang (Late 1800s)

Around the same time, the phrase became popular slang for **eating a massive meal**. It treated the human stomach like a storage container or a cupboard that you were packing full of food. \[[1](https://www.phrasalverbsexplained.com/post/the-phrasal-verb-put-away-explained)]

is it counterintuitive

No, the evolution of "put away" is actually highly logical rather than counterintuitive.

Every modern meaning stems from the same core concept: **moving something out of sight and into a contained space.**

Here is why the shift makes sense:

- **From "discard" to "store"**: Both mean removing an item from your current workspace. The only change was whether you threw it out or put it in a cupboard.
- **From "store" to "imprison"**: A jail cell acts exactly like a cupboard for a society. You are placing a person inside a contained box to keep them out of public sight.
- **From "store" to "eat"**: Your stomach is treated as the container. You are packing food away into your body.

The logic remains identical across centuries. The only thing that changed was the "container" being used (a closet, a prison, or a stomach).

why it means that now

It means that now because of a natural linguistic process called **semantic shift**, where words gradually change meaning over centuries. \[[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semantic%5Fchange)]

The shift from "throwing away" to "storing neatly" happened because of two main reasons:

1\. A Change in Focus (The 1500s)

Originally, when you "put something away," the focus was on the word **away**—you were removing a nuisance from your sight forever.

Over time, people started using the phrase when cleaning up their homes. When you tidy a room, you are still removing objects from your sight, but instead of throwing them out, you are placing them in a cupboard or chest. The linguistic focus shifted from **destroying/abandoning** the item to **organizing** it. \[[1](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=q3xQW8Tcdr8)]

2\. The Influence of Technology and Furniture

In the Middle Ages, everyday people had very few possessions and almost no built-in storage or closets. If something was in your way, you usually discarded it.

By the 1500s and 1600s, manufacturing improved. People acquired more personal belongings, and furniture like chests, dressers, and wardrobes became common. Society needed a standard daily phrase to describe **placing an object inside a piece of furniture**. Because "put away" already meant "remove this from the table/floor," it naturally adapted to mean "place this in its designated storage drawer." \[[1](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=q3xQW8Tcdr8)]

Summary of the Logic

- **1300s:** "Get this object **out of my sight** (by throwing it outside)."
- **1600s:** "Get this object **out of my sight** (by putting it in the closet)." \[[1](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=q3xQW8Tcdr8)]

The physical action changed, but the mental intent (removing clutter from sight) stayed exactly the same. \[[1](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=q3xQW8Tcdr8)]
