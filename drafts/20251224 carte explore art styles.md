# 3

how could i divide art into something comprehensive yet not overlapping

---

That’s a thoughtful question, Fira.
You’re noticing something important: most art categories blur into each other. That can feel confusing when you want clarity.

If we want a system that is **comprehensive** _and_ **non-overlapping**, we have to step back.
Instead of dividing art by history or style, we divide it by **primary intent** — _what the artwork is mainly trying to do_.

This won’t describe everything about a piece, but it gives each work **one clear home**.

Here is a calm, clean framework that avoids overlap as much as possible.

---

### A Clear, Non-Overlapping Way to Divide Art

#### 1. **Narrative Art**

**Purpose:** to tell a story

This includes:

- anime
- manga
- comics
- illustration
- concept art
- story-driven paintings

The focus is characters, events, or worlds.
Even a single image feels like part of a larger story.

---

#### 2. **Representational Art**

**Purpose:** to depict the visible world

This includes:

- realism
- portraiture
- landscapes
- still life
- observational drawing

The goal is seeing clearly, not inventing.
The subject exists outside the artist’s imagination.

---

#### 3. **Expressive Art**

**Purpose:** to express inner emotion or mood

This includes:

- expressionism
- emotional abstraction
- painterly, gestural work

Accuracy doesn’t matter here.
What matters is feeling.

---

#### 4. **Symbolic Art**

**Purpose:** to communicate ideas through symbols

This includes:

- surrealism
- allegorical art
- religious or mythic imagery

Objects stand for something else.
The artwork asks to be interpreted.

---

#### 5. **Formal Art**

**Purpose:** to explore visual structure itself

This includes:

- abstraction
- geometric art
- minimalism
- color studies

The subject is shape, color, balance, rhythm.
Meaning comes from form, not story or emotion.

---

#### 6. **Functional Art**

**Purpose:** to serve a practical role

This includes:

- design
- architecture
- typography
- decorative art
- applied arts

Beauty supports function.

---

#### 7. **Conceptual Art**

**Purpose:** to foreground an idea over appearance

This includes:

- conceptual installations
- performance art
- text-based art

The artwork is the thought itself.
The visual form is secondary.

---

### Why this works

Each category is defined by **primary intention**, not style.

An artwork _may_ contain emotion, symbols, or beauty —
but only one of those is the **main reason it exists**.

This keeps the categories from overlapping.

---

### A quiet insight

Most people are drawn strongly to **one or two** of these groups.
The rest feel interesting, but distant.

If you think about the art that stays with you, Fira —
which intention feels most alive to you?

We can explore that space together, slowly.

### A calm way to decide your game’s art

#### 1. Start with the **player’s inner state**

Ask yourself:

> _How should the player feel most of the time?_

Not excitement or fun — something deeper.

- safe
- lonely
- curious
- tense
- nostalgic
- playful
- reflective

This feeling is the root.
Everything else grows from it.

---

#### 2. Choose one **primary art intention**

From the framework we talked about, pick **one**.

For indie games, these are the most stable choices:

- **Narrative art** → story, characters, atmosphere
- **Expressive art** → mood, emotion, texture
- **Formal art** → clarity, shapes, readability

Trying to mix all three usually weakens the result.

---

#### 3. Decide your **level of abstraction**

This is where many creators get stuck.

Choose one:

- **Highly abstract** (shapes, symbols, suggestion)
- **Stylized** (anime, cartoon, simplified realism)
- **Grounded** (realistic proportions, believable light)

Lower abstraction = more work, more assets.
Higher abstraction = stronger identity, less burden.

Indie games often thrive in the middle.

---

#### 4. Limit your visual language

This is where art direction becomes real.

Gently constrain:

- color palette (3–6 main colors)
- line style (soft, hard, none)
- texture (flat, painterly, pixel, noisy)
- camera distance (close, mid, far)

Limits create cohesion.
Cohesion creates calm.

---

#### 5. Pick a **reference anchor**

Not many — just one or two.

These can be:

- a single anime
- a painter
- another game
- even a mood photograph

The rule:

> _Borrow structure, not surface._

If you copy surface, it feels hollow.
If you copy structure, it becomes yours.

---

### A gentle example

Let’s say your game is quiet and introspective.

You might choose:

- player feeling: **solitude**
- intention: **expressive art**
- abstraction: **stylized**
- palette: muted blues, warm lights
- reference: soft anime backgrounds, impressionist light

> _Does this protect solitude?_
