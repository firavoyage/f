自傷無色 meaning

**自傷無色 (Jishou Mushoku)** translates to **"Self-Inflicted Achromatic"** or **"Self-Harm and Colorlessness."** \[[1](https://steamcommunity.com/discussions/forum/12/1744479063984511056/?l=dutch), [2](https://www.pixiv.net/tags/%E8%87%AA%E5%82%B7%E7%84%A1%E8%89%B2/manga)]

It is a highly popular 2013 Vocaloid song produced by Nekobolo (Sasanomaly) and sung by Hatsune Miku. The title and lyrics carry deep psychological themes. \[[1](https://utaten.com/specialArticle/index/7611), [2](https://abeyutaka.wordpress.com/2013/01/23/romaji-jishoku-mushoku-%E8%87%AA%E5%82%B7%E7%84%A1%E8%89%B2-hatsune-miku/)]

Title Breakdown

- **自傷 (Jishou):** Self-harm or self-inflicted injury.
- **無色 (Mushoku):** Colorless, transparent, or achromatic.
- **Combined Meaning:** It represents a state of feeling completely invisible, empty, and devoid of value, where one's existence lacks "color" or purpose. \[[1](https://romajidesu.com/dictionary/meaning-of-%E8%87%AA%E5%82%B7.html), [2](https://steamcommunity.com/discussions/forum/12/1744479063984511056/?l=dutch), [3](https://abeyutaka.wordpress.com/2013/01/23/romaji-jishoku-mushoku-%E8%87%AA%E5%82%B7%E7%84%A1%E8%89%B2-hatsune-miku/)]

Core Themes & Song Meaning

- **Imposter Syndrome:** The narrator feels like a useless burden to everyone around them.
- **Desire for Erasure:** The lyrics express a deep wish to disappear or to have never been born, believing the world would be better off without them.
- **The Anchor:** The narrator is kept from disappearing by a loved one ("Kimi") whose smile and presence give them a reason to stay alive, even when they feel entirely empty. \[[1](https://abeyutaka.wordpress.com/2013/01/23/romaji-jishoku-mushoku-%E8%87%AA%E5%82%B7%E7%84%A1%E8%89%B2-hatsune-miku/), [2](https://utaten.com/specialArticle/index/7611)]

interpretation

**自傷無色 (Jishou Mushoku)** is an interpretation of profound depression, identity loss, and emotional codependency.

The song describes a narrator trapped between the desire to cease existing and the guilt of hurting someone who cares about them.

1\. The Meaning of "Colorlessness"

- **Invisible Existence:** The narrator views themselves as a transparent entity that contributes nothing to the world.
- **Lack of Identity:** While other people have "colors" (personalities, futures, value), the narrator feels entirely blank and empty.
- **Emotional Numbness:** The lack of color represents a state of severe depression where joy and purpose have completely faded away.

2\. The Nature of "Self-Harm"

- **Emotional Sabotage:** The "self-harm" in the song is primarily psychological rather than physical.
- **Constant Self-Depreciation:** The narrator constantly attacks their own worth, believing their birth was a mistake.
- **Erasure vs. Suicide:** The lyrics focus on _wanting to have never existed_ rather than the active desire to die, highlighting the fear of leaving a messy emotional legacy behind.

3\. The Role of the "Other" (Kimi)

- **The Emotional Anchor:** A specific person ("You" or _Kimi_) keeps the narrator tied to reality.
- **The Paradox of Guilt:** The narrator wants to disappear to stop being a burden, but realizes that disappearing would make _Kimi_ cry.
- **Living for Someone Else:** Ultimately, the song ends on a bittersweet note. The narrator chooses to keep living—not because they love themselves, but because they cannot bear to destroy the happiness of the person who loves them.

lyric interpretation

Here is a detailed interpretation of **自傷無色 (Jishou Mushoku)** broken down by its key lyrical shifts.

1\. The Opening: The Desire for Non-Existence

> _"If I could become a person like you, would I be able to live like that?"_\
> _"If a creature like me were to die, would anyone cry?"_

- **The Contrast:** The narrator begins by comparing themselves to a healthy, functioning person. They perceive a massive gap in worth between themselves and others.
- **The Existential Query:** The question isn't just about dying; it is a test of their own visibility. They genuinely believe their disappearance might go unnoticed or even relieve the world.

2\. The Verse: The Burden of Breathing

> _"If I disappear, it would be a bit better. For sure, the surrounding world would rotate smoothly."_

- **The Burden Mentality:** This is a classic symptom of severe depression. The narrator views their very existence as friction in the lives of others.
- **Rationalizing Erasure:** They convince themselves that removing themselves from the equation is an act of altruism that will make everyone else's lives "rotate smoothly."

3\. The Chorus: The Struggle with Identity ("Colorlessness")

> _"I can't say it, I can't say it, it's a secret."_\
> _"The inside of my throat is muddy and dirty."_

- **The Mask:** The "secret" is their profound suffering. They suppress their thoughts because they believe voicing their pain will pollute or burden those around them.
- **The "Muddy" Self:** While they feel "colorless" (invisible) on the outside, they feel internally "muddy" (corrupted, broken, and ugly).

4\. The Turning Point: The Mirror and the Anchor

> _"If you smile, I will be fine, even if it hurts."_\
> _"If you cry, I will be sad, so I can't do that."_

- **Living Vicariously:** The narrator's emotional spectrum is completely tethered to the other person (_Kimi_). They have no independent mechanism for happiness; they only feel "fine" if the other person is smiling.
- **The Ultimate Deterrent:** The urge to disappear is entirely blocked by a single realization: _Kimi_ will cry. The narrator hates themselves, but they love _Kimi_ enough to endure the pain of existing just to prevent that tears.

5\. The Climax & Outro: A Bitter Decision

> _"In the end, I'll be like this forever."_\
> _"Thank you for making me change my mind."_

- **Stagnation:** The narrator accepts that they might never "fix" their mental state ("I'll be like this forever"). There is no magical healing sequence.
- **Living for Another:** The "change of mind" is the decision to stay alive. It is a profoundly bittersweet conclusion. They do not find self-love; instead, they choose to carry the weight of life as a gift of gratitude to the person who refuses to let them fade away.
