IELTS Academic Writing Task 1

Question

_You should spend about 20 minutes on this task._

The bar chart shows the average number of hours per week that people in four countries spent on leisure activities in 2022, categorised by three age groups.

Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant.

_Write at least 150 words._

***

Band 7–8 Model Answer _(approximately 185 words)_

The bar chart compares weekly leisure time among people in the UK, Germany, Japan, and Brazil across three age groups: 18–34, 35–54, and 55 and over, in 2022.

Overall, Brazil consistently recorded the highest leisure hours in every age group, while Japan reported the lowest. A clear and notable trend is that leisure time follows a U-shaped pattern across age groups in all four countries — it declines in middle age before rising significantly among older adults.

Among younger adults aged 18–34, Brazilians spent the most time on leisure at 30 hours per week, compared to just 17 hours for their Japanese counterparts. The UK and Germany fell in between, at 26 and 23 hours respectively. In the 35–54 age bracket, figures dropped across all nations, with Japan reaching its lowest point of only 13 hours.

By contrast, the 55+ group showed a dramatic rise in leisure time. The UK saw the sharpest increase, jumping from 18 to 33 hours, while Brazil reached the highest figure overall at 37 hours. Japan, despite its consistently lower levels, also climbed to 25 hours in this oldest group.

Sample Band 7–8 Response

The bar chart illustrates the percentage of their income that adults and children in the United Kingdom allocated to four categories of expenditure — food, clothing, entertainment, and transport — in 1998.

Overall, the spending patterns of adults and children differed considerably. Adults devoted the largest share of their income to food, while children prioritised entertainment and clothing. Transport was the only category where adults significantly outspent children.

In terms of food, adults spent the highest proportion of all categories at 35%, whereas children allocated only 20% to this item, making it their second-lowest expenditure. The reverse pattern was evident for clothing: children spent 35% of their income on it, more than double the adult figure of 15%, which was the lowest proportion recorded for adults.

Entertainment was the biggest expenditure for children, who spent 42% of their income on it — the single highest figure in the chart. Adults, by contrast, spent 25% on entertainment, the same proportion they allocated to transport. Children, however, barely spent anything on transport at just 3%, the lowest proportion across all groups and categories.

In summary, while adults spread their spending more evenly — with food as the clear priority — children concentrated their expenditure heavily on entertainment and clothing, and showed almost no interest in transport costs, which likely reflects their limited need or ability to travel independently.
