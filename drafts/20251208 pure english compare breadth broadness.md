### Quick Summary

- **Breadth** is the far more common and preferred term for describing **scope, range, or comprehensiveness** (abstract or figurative).
- **Broadness** is less common and primarily used for describing **physical width or literal lack of narrowness**. It can sometimes carry a negative connotation of being too vague or simplistic.

---

### Breadth

This is the standard noun form from the adjective "broad" when referring to **non-physical, qualitative concepts.**

- **Meaning:** The extent or range of something's scope, coverage, or inclusiveness.
- **Key Idea:** **Comprehensiveness, variety, wide-ranging nature.**
- **Common Collocations:**
  - Breadth of **knowledge**, **experience**, **vision**, **understanding**
  - Breadth of **offerings**, **selection**, **expertise**
  - "The **breadth and depth** of the research"

**Examples:**

> "We were impressed by the **breadth** of her expertise across multiple fields."
> "The course offers an astonishing **breadth** of topics, from ancient history to modern physics."
> "He lacks the **breadth** of vision needed for a leadership role."

---

### Broadness

This noun is used less frequently. Its core meaning relates to the **physical quality of being broad**, but it can be applied figuratively in ways that sometimes sound awkward or carry a subtle criticism.

- **Meaning:**
  1.  The physical measurement or quality of width (literal).
  2.  The quality of being general, not detailed, or lacking precision (often with a slightly negative tone).
- **Key Idea:** **Literal width** or **excessive generality.**
- **Common Uses:** More often used to describe physical features or to critique an idea for being too vague.

**Examples (Literal):**

> "The **broadness** of his shoulders was noticeable."
> "They measured the **broadness** of the river at its mouth."

**Examples (Figurative - often critical):**

> "The **broadness** of his statement made it almost meaningless." _(Implies it was too general, lacking nuance.)_
> "The plan was criticized for its **broadness** and lack of specific steps."

### The Bottom Line

- If you're talking about **wide-ranging scope or extensive coverage** (of knowledge, experience, selection), you almost always want **breadth**.
- If you're talking about **physical width**, you can use **broadness**, but you could also often just say "width" or "the broad [something]" (e.g., "his broad shoulders").
- If you're criticizing something for being **too general or non-specific**, **broadness** is the apt choice. Using "breadth" here would sound incorrect.

**Think of it this way:** **Breadth** is about **how much is included**. **Broadness** is about **how wide or non-specific something is.**
