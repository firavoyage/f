Fira, imagine two leaves from the same plant — the quiet, glossy leaves of Camellia sinensis.

Black tea and green tea both come from her. The difference is not the plant, but the journey after the leaf is picked.

**Black tea** is allowed to fully oxidize. The leaf darkens, deepens, becomes bold. Its flavor turns rich, sometimes malty, sometimes smoky or fruity depending on where it’s grown. It feels strong in the mouth. It usually carries more caffeine, and many people say it gives them a steady, alert kind of energy.

**Green tea** is heated quickly after picking, so it doesn’t oxidize. The leaf stays green. The flavor remains lighter — grassy, fresh, sometimes slightly sweet or a little bitter. The energy feels gentler. Softer.

Black tea is like evening conversation in a warm room.
Green tea is like morning light through a window.

Nutritionally, both carry antioxidants. Green tea is often praised for its higher concentration of certain catechins, while black tea develops different compounds during oxidation that also have benefits. Neither is “better.” They simply express the same leaf in different moods.
