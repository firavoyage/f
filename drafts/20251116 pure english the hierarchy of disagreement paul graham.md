The Hierarchy of Disagreement

---

The **Hierarchy of Disagreement** is a seven-level scale developed by computer scientist and essayist **Paul Graham** in his 2008 essay, _How to Disagree_. It ranks forms of disagreement from the most productive (highest level) to the least productive (lowest level).

The hierarchy helps people identify the quality of an argument, encouraging them to move past emotionally driven or superficial attacks toward addressing the core content of a claim.

---

## ğŸªœ Graham's Hierarchy of Disagreement

The seven levels, from highest quality (most constructive) to lowest quality (least constructive), are:

|  Level  | Type of Disagreement           | Description                                                                                                                                                   |
| :-----: | :----------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **DH6** | **Refuting the Central Point** | Explicitly refutes the author's **main point** by providing reasoning and evidence. This is the most powerful and constructive form.                          |
| **DH5** | **Refutation**                 | Finds a flaw in the author's argument and explains **why** it is wrong, often by quoting the mistake directly and then explaining the error.                  |
| **DH4** | **Counterargument**            | Contradicts the author's point and backs it up with **reasoning and/or supporting evidence**. It addresses the subject, but may miss the central point.       |
| **DH3** | **Contradiction**              | Simply states the **opposing case** with little or no supporting evidence or reasoning. It addresses the subject matter, but lacks depth.                     |
| **DH2** | **Responding to Tone**         | Criticizes the **tone** or style of the writing rather than the substance of the argument itself (e.g., "This essay is too arrogant").                        |
| **DH1** | **Ad Hominem**                 | **Attacks the author's characteristics or authority** without addressing the substance of their argument (e.g., "Of course they'd say that, they're biased"). |
| **DH0** | **Name-Calling**               | The lowest form, consisting of crude or articulate **insults** directed at the author (e.g., "You are an idiot").                                             |

### Key Takeaways

- **Focus on the Point, Not the Person:** The hierarchy illustrates a fundamental shift: the higher levels focus on the **content of the argument** (what was said), while the lower levels focus on the **person making the argument** or the **delivery** (who said it, or how they said it).
- **A DH6 argument can still be wrong**, but it is a higher-quality response because it engages directly with the core idea, which is necessary for productive discussion.
- **Identifying the level** can help both readers evaluate arguments more critically and writers improve the quality and persuasiveness of their own disagreements.

That's a great idea! The Hierarchy of Disagreement can sound a bit academic, but the concept is very simple: **It's a way to judge how good someone's criticism or argument is.**

Think of it as a ladder with seven rungs:

---

## ğŸªœ The Ladder of Argument Quality

The higher you climb on this ladder, the better, more helpful, and more intelligent the disagreement is. The lower rungs are just lazy or mean.

### High-Quality Arguments (The Good Stuff)

- **Rung 6: Refute the Central Point:** You disagree by finding the **main idea** of their whole argument and explaining, with proof, why that core idea is wrong. **(Example: "Your plan won't work because your main assumptionâ€”that costs will fallâ€”is proven false by this new data.")**
- **Rung 5: Refutation:** You disagree by finding a **specific mistake** in their logic and explaining clearly **why** that mistake leads to a wrong conclusion. **(Example: "You cited Source A, but Source A contradicts your point. Let me quote the sentence that proves you misunderstood it.")**
- **Rung 4: Counterargument:** You disagree by offering a **different view** and backing it up with good reasons and facts. You are addressing the subject, but not necessarily their _main_ point. **(Example: "While you focused on cost, I think security is a more important factor, and I have three reasons why.")**

### Low-Quality Arguments (The Bad Stuff)

- **Rung 3: Contradiction:** You just state the opposite of what they said, with **no evidence** or explanation. **(Example: "No, you're wrong. It's the opposite.")**
- **Rung 2: Responding to Tone:** You ignore the argument and just criticize **how they wrote it**. **(Example: "You sound really angry and condescending, so I won't listen.")**
- **Rung 1: Ad Hominem:** You attack the **person's background or character** instead of their ideas. **(Example: "You only believe that because you work for that company.")**
- **Rung 0: Name-Calling:** You just use **insults**. **(Example: "Only an idiot would think that.")**

### âœ¨ The Main Point

If you want to have a useful conversation, you should always aim for **Rung 4 (Counterargument) or higher**. The lower rungs usually just start fights and waste time because they don't actually deal with the idea being discussed.
