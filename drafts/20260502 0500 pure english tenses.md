english tenses

English verb tenses <mark style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);">indicate when an action happens (past, present, or future) and its status (completed or ongoing)</mark>. While there are technically two morphological tenses in English—past and present—English learners typically study **12 main tenses** formed by combining three time periods with four grammatical aspects. \[[1](https://www.grammarly.com/blog/parts-of-speech/verb-tenses/), [2](https://www.busuu.com/en/english/tenses), [3](https://www.bbc.co.uk/learningenglish/features/tenses%5Fwith%5Fgeorgie/ep-240119), [4](https://cisedu.com/en-gb/world-of-cis/articles-education/how-many-english-tenses/), [5](https://www.planetspark.in/english-grammar/grammar-tenses-rules)]

Key Grammar Rules

To use these tenses correctly, follow these core principles:

- **Subject-Verb Agreement**: The verb must match the subject in number and person (e.g., "He **plays**" vs. "They **play**").
- **Helping Verbs**: Auxiliary verbs like _be_, _have_, and _will_ are essential for forming continuous, perfect, and future tenses.
- **Time Markers**: Words like _yesterday_, _already_, _yet_, and _tomorrow_ often signal which tense to use.
- **Common Usage**: Research indicates that **95% of spoken English** uses only five tenses: Simple Present, Simple Past, Simple Future, Present Perfect, and Present Continuous. \[[1](https://www.grammarly.com/blog/parts-of-speech/verb-tenses/), [2](https://www.planetspark.in/english-grammar/grammar-tenses-rules), [3](https://www.ego4u.com/en/cram-up/grammar/tenses), [4](https://ginsengenglish.com/blog/english-verb-tense-frequency), [5](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Se5zT8Sjtg4)]
