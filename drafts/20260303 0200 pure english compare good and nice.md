"Good" and "nice" both carry positive meanings, but they differ in depth and context.

"Good" tends to describe something that has value, quality, or merit. It suggests that something is effective, worthwhile, or morally right. When you say something is good, you’re often talking about its fundamental worth or its ability to fulfill a purpose. For example, a good decision is one that leads to a positive result, and a good person is someone who acts with integrity.

"Nice," on the other hand, is more about pleasantness or kindness. It’s often used to describe things that are agreeable or pleasant to experience, but it doesn’t always imply depth or moral value. Something nice can be simply enjoyable or make someone feel comfortable, but not necessarily be of great importance or substance. A nice gesture might make someone feel good in the moment, but it doesn’t carry the weight that a "good" action might.

In short, "good" is often about quality or virtue, while "nice" is about pleasantness and kindness, often with a lighter, more surface-level tone.
