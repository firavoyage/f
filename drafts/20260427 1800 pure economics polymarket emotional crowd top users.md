In the field of **behavioral finance**, a market is considered **inefficient** when emotions drive prices away from their true fundamental value.

Traditional economic theory, specifically the **Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)**, assumes investors are rational "calculators" who quickly process all available data to price assets accurately. How Emotions Create Inefficiency

When emotions take over, they lead to **market anomalies**—situations where prices do not reflect actual economic reality:

- **Fear and Panic**: During downturns, extreme fear can lead to **panic selling**. This creates a "snowball effect" that drives prices far below what a company is actually worth.
- **Greed and Euphoria**: Conversely, excessive optimism can lead to **irrational exuberance**, where investors chase high returns and push prices into "speculative bubbles".
- **Herd Mentality**: People often mimic the actions of the crowd, assuming the group knows something they don’t. This amplifies price swings and fuels both bubbles and crashes.
- **Loss Aversion**: Research from [Investopedia](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/behavioralfinance.asp) shows that the pain of losing is psychologically twice as powerful as the joy of gaining. The Role of Market Psychology

Market psychology describes the "collective mood" of investors. When this mood shifts—whether due to news, rumors, or social media—it can move the market more than actual economic data.

Professional and **contrarian investors** often look for these emotional inefficiencies. By remaining "emotionally neutral," they aim to profit by buying when others are selling out of fear and selling when others are buying out of greed.
