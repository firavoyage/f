A _methodology_ (often used in research projects, dissertations, reports, or professional studies) explains **how** you carried out your work. It can include many components depending on the field, but here are the most common elements:

### **1. Research Design**

- Qualitative, quantitative, or mixed methods

### **2. Data Collection Methods**

- Surveys or questionnaires
- Interviews
- Focus groups
- Observations
- Experiments
- Document or archival analysis

### **3. Sampling Strategy**

- Population and sample
- Sampling method (random, purposive, convenience, etc.)
- Sample size and justification

### **4. Materials, Tools, or Instruments**

- Survey forms, interview guides
- Lab equipment
- Software or analytical tools
- Measurement instruments

### **5. Procedures / Step-by-Step Process**

- How the research was conducted
- Timeline
- Protocols followed

### **6. Data Analysis Techniques**

- Statistical tests (e.g., regression, t-test, ANOVA)
- Qualitative coding or thematic analysis
- Content analysis
- Computational models

### **7. Ethical Considerations**

- Informed consent
- Anonymity and confidentiality
- Risk management

### **8. Limitations of the Methodology**

- Potential weaknesses
- Biases
- Constraints (time, access, resources)

### **9. Reliability and Validity / Trustworthiness**

- How you ensured accurate, consistent, credible results
- Pilot testing, triangulation, inter-rater reliability, etc.

### **10. Justification of Choices**

---

## **Methodology vs. How-to Guide (Simple Comparison)**

### **1. What they are**

**Methodology:**
A _way of thinking_ about how to approach something.
It explains **principles**, **steps**, and **why** things are done a certain way.

**How-to Guide:**
A _set of instructions_ that tells you **exactly what to do**.
It is mostly about **action**, not explanation.

---

### **2. Purpose**

**Methodology:**
Helps you **understand the overall process** so you can adapt it to many situations.

**How-to Guide:**
Helps you **complete one specific task** as quickly and easily as possible.

---

### **3. Flexibility**

**Methodology:**
Flexible. You can change steps based on the situation.

**How-to Guide:**
Fixed. You follow the steps as written to get the intended result.

---

### **4. Scope**

**Methodology:**
Broad. Covers the **big picture** of how to handle a type of problem or work.

**How-to Guide:**
Narrow. Covers **one exact task** (e.g., ‚Äúhow to boil an egg‚Äù).

---

### **5. Level of detail**

**Methodology:**
More general. Describes patterns, stages, and rules you can reuse.

**How-to Guide:**
More specific. Describes precise actions:
‚ÄúDo this. Then do that.‚Äù

---

### **6. Who it‚Äôs for**

**Methodology:**
People who want to understand _how a process works_ so they can apply it anywhere.

**How-to Guide:**
People who just want to _get something done_ without learning the theory.

---

## **Simple Example**

### **Methodology:**

‚ÄúWhen trying to solve a problem, first understand it, then break it into small pieces, try a solution, observe the results, and adjust.‚Äù

### **How-to Guide:**

1. Turn the screw left.
2. Apply oil to the hinge.
3. Turn the screw back.
4. Test the door.

---

## **In one sentence**

A **methodology** teaches you _how to think and act_ in general situations.
A **how-to guide** teaches you _exactly what to do_ in one specific situation.
