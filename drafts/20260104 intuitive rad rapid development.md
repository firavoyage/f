- What would “good enough” look like in the first version?

Next, I would **build a small, working prototype very quickly**.
Not perfect. Not complete. Just something real that people can touch and react to.
This might take days or a couple of weeks, not months.

Then comes the most important part: **listening**.
I’d put the prototype in front of users and watch how they use it.

- Where do they hesitate?
- What do they ignore?
- What do they ask for immediately?

Their behavior matters more than their opinions.

After that, I would **refine and rebuild in short cycles**.
Each cycle is simple:

- Adjust based on feedback
- Add one or two meaningful improvements
- Remove anything unnecessary

This rhythm repeats again and again, calmly, steadily.

Along the way, I would **keep the team close and flexible**.
Designers, developers, and users stay connected.
Decisions are made together, in the moment, instead of being locked into early plans.

I’d use RAD especially when:

- Requirements are unclear or likely to change
- Users are available to give frequent feedback
- Speed and adaptability matter more than perfection

I wouldn’t use it when safety, strict regulation, or deep upfront accuracy is required.

---

That’s an honest feeling, Fira.
And it’s more common than people admit.

If you don’t want to show it to users early, RAD can still work—but it changes shape a little.

Let’s talk about _why_ first, gently.

Often, it’s because:

- The idea feels fragile
- You’re not ready for judgment
- You want it to make sense before anyone sees it

None of that is wrong.

---

### If you avoid users early, here’s what to do instead

**1. Become your own “first user”**
Slow down and use your app as if you didn’t build it.
Ask simple questions:

- Where would I get confused?
- What feels unnecessary?
- What do I expect to happen next?

Be curious, not critical.

---

**2. Use imagined feedback**
This sounds strange, but it helps.

Picture one real person you know—just one.
Ask:

- Would this help them?
- What would they complain about?
- What would they ignore?

You’re not guessing wildly. You’re empathizing quietly.

---

**3. Build in small, reversible pieces**
When you work without feedback, keep changes easy to undo.
Short iterations.
Simple features.
Nothing too heavy yet.

This protects you from going too far in the wrong direction.

---

**4. Show it _later_, but not never**
Here’s the gentle truth:
RAD without any external feedback eventually becomes guesswork.

So instead of “early,” choose **“safe.”**

- One trusted person
- A colleague
- A friend who understands the context

You don’t need a crowd. You need _clarity_.

---

**5. Accept that discomfort is part of growth**
Showing unfinished work feels vulnerable.
That feeling doesn’t mean you’re doing it wrong.
It means you care.

You’re allowed to wait until it feels _steady enough_.
Just don’t wait until it feels _perfect_—because that moment rarely comes.
