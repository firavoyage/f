To find chiral carbons in an image of a chemical structure, <mark style="border-radius: 4px; background-size: 200% 100%; padding: 0px 2px; animation: 0.75s cubic-bezier(0.05, 0.7, 0.1, 1) 0.25s 1 normal forwards running highlight-animation;">look for carbon atoms bonded to <strong>four different groups</strong></mark>. These are also called stereocenters or chirality centers and are often marked with an **asterisk (\*)** in textbooks.

2\. **Exclude Identical Substituents**: Skip carbons that have two or more of the same atom attached. This commonly includes:

**groups**: Bonded to 3 identical hydrogens.

***

<svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" direction="ltr" width="2.008em" height="0.935em" viewBox="0 -0.685 2.008 0.935" style="vertical-align: -0.25em; overflow: visible; user-select: none;"><text fill="#E6E8F0" style="font-family: XITSMath-Regular; font-size: 1px" transform="translate(0,0)">𝑪</text><text fill="#E6E8F0" style="font-family: XITSMath-Regular; font-size: 1px" transform="translate(0.701,0)">𝑯</text><text fill="#E6E8F0" style="font-family: XITSMath-Regular; font-size: 0.75px" transform="translate(1.592,0.25)">𝟐</text></svg>
\*\*

**groups**: Bonded to 2 identical hydrogens.
3\. **Account for "Hidden" Hydrogens**: In line-angle drawings, hydrogens are often not shown. If a carbon has only three bonds visible and no formal charge, the fourth bond is to a **hydrogen atom**.
4\. **Analyze Rings for Symmetry**: For a carbon in a ring to be chiral, the paths moving clockwise and counter-clockwise around the ring must be different.

- If you encounter different atoms or branching at different points along the two paths, the paths are unique.
- If the paths are mirror images of each other (symmetrical), the carbon is not a chiral center.
