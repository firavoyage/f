what is license ISC

The **ISC License** is <mark style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);">a <strong>highly permissive, open-source software license</strong> created by the <span style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);"><a rel="noopener" href="https://www.isc.org/about/" style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-thickness: 1px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(153, 195, 255); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(153, 195, 255);">Internet Systems Consortium (ISC)</a></span></mark>. It is functionally identical to the [MIT License](https://gist.github.com/indexzero/10602128) but features much more concise language because it removes text made redundant by the [Berne Convention](https://fossa.com/blog/open-source-software-licenses-101-isc-license/). \[[1](https://fossa.com/blog/open-source-software-licenses-101-isc-license/)]

Key Characteristics

- **Permissive Nature:** It grants users complete freedom to use, copy, modify, and distribute the software for any purpose, with or without a fee.
- **Commercial Use Allowed:** You can use ISC-licensed code in proprietary, commercial applications without disclosing your source code.
- **Minimal Requirements:** The single obligation is that you must include the original copyright notice and the license text in all copies or substantial portions of the software.
- **No Warranty:** It provides the software "as is," completely disclaiming liability and warranties of fitness or merchantability. \[[1](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2e3ShayuAW4), [2](https://interoperable-europe.ec.europa.eu/licence/isc-license), [3](https://fossa.com/blog/open-source-software-licenses-101-isc-license/)]

Popular Use Cases

- **npm Packages:** The [npm CLI tool](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2e3ShayuAW4) defaults to setting the ISC License when initializing new JavaScript/TypeScript projects.
- **OpenBSD:** It is the preferred license for new code contributions to the [OpenBSD operating system](https://wiki.tcl-lang.org/page/ISC). \[[1](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2e3ShayuAW4), [2](https://www.reddit.com/r/opensource/comments/1atpsz4/why%5Fdevelopers%5Fchoose%5Fmit%5Flicense%5Fover%5Fisc/), [3](https://wiki.tcl-lang.org/page/ISC)]

ISC vs. MIT License

While they offer the same legal permissions, the primary difference lies in the phrasing. The ISC license template is much shorter. However, because the MIT license is more globally recognized, the European Union's Publications Office and other groups often recommend choosing the [MIT License](https://interoperable-europe.ec.europa.eu/licence/isc-license) over ISC strictly to minimize license proliferation. \[[1](https://interoperable-europe.ec.europa.eu/licence/isc-license), [2](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2e3ShayuAW4), [3](https://fossa.com/blog/open-source-software-licenses-101-isc-license/), [4](https://fossa.com/blog/open-source-software-licenses-101-isc-license/)]

what is berne convention

The **Berne Convention** is <mark style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);">a foundational <strong>international treaty</strong> signed in 1886 that governs <strong>copyright law</strong> across the globe</mark>. It ensures that creative works are protected automatically in all member nations without needing formal registration. \[[1](https://www.copyrighthistory.org/cam/tools/request/showRecord.php?id=commentary%5Fuk%5F1886), [2](https://copyrightservice.co.uk/copyright/international%5Fcopyright), [3](https://blog.ipleaders.in/leading-international-instruments-related-to-intellectual-property-rights/), [4](https://blog.ipleaders.in/intellectual-property-rights-an-overview-of-leading-organizations-and-conventions/), [5](https://testbook.com/question-answer/the-berne-convention-for-the-protection-of-intelle--691674e90bf3a1318f808f96)]

Core Principles

- **Automatic Protection:** Copyright is granted immediately upon the creation of a work. You do not need to register it or use a copyright symbol (`©`).
- **National Treatment:** Member countries must grant foreign creators from other member states the exact same copyright protections they give to their own citizens.
- **Independence of Protection:** A creator's legal rights in a country do not depend on whether the work is protected in its country of origin. \[[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berne%5FConvention), [2](https://www.studysmarter.co.uk/explanations/law/international-and-humanitarian-law/berne-convention-for-the-protection-of-literary-and-artistic-works/), [3](https://www.ingberiplawyer.com/blog/how-does-the-berne-convention-apply-to-my-work/), [4](https://www.hobartlegal.org.au/handbook/rights-disability-and-access/intellectual-property-and-copyright/copyright/international-copyright-the-berne-convention/)]

Minimum Standards of Protection

- **Duration:** Copyright must last for at least the **author's life plus 50 years** (though many countries, like the US, have extended this to 70 years).
- **Scope:** It covers "literary, scientific, and artistic works," including books, music, paintings, software code, and films.
- **Moral Rights:** Authors retain the right to claim authorship and object to any modifications that harm their reputation, even if they sell the economic copyright. \[[1](https://opencanada.org/as-creators-rights-face-new-challenges-canada-needs-to-keep-pace-with-international-developments/), [2](https://guides.dml.georgetown.edu/c.php?g=904530\&p=6544639), [3](https://www.studysmarter.co.uk/explanations/law/international-and-humanitarian-law/berne-convention-for-the-protection-of-literary-and-artistic-works/), [4](https://testbook.com/question-answer/the-berne-convention-for-the-protection-of-intelle--691674e90bf3a1318f808f96), [5](https://academic.oup.com/jiplp/article/20/6/398/8003600)]

Impact on Software and Licenses

The Berne Convention is why modern open-source licenses like **ISC and MIT** can be so brief. Because the treaty automatically grants authors full copyright protection worldwide the moment they write code, licenses do not need long, complex legalese to establish ownership. Instead, they just need to explicitly state what permissions they are granting to the public.

is it good to have copyright even after the author's life

Whether extending copyright past an author's life is "good" is a major debate in law and economics, with no simple answer.

The original goal was to let creators support their children and grandchildren \[1]. Today, critics argue it blocks public access to culture, while supporters argue it protects a family's financial legacy.

The Arguments For (Why it is good)

- **Family Provision:** It allows creators to pass down financial security to their heirs, just like real estate or stocks \[1].
- **Continued Investment:** Publishers and estates are more likely to restore, distribute, and market old works if they still hold exclusive rights.
- **Dignity and Control:** The author's family can prevent the work from being used in offensive ways or in advertisements that the late author would have hated.

The Arguments Against (Why it is bad)

- **The "Mickey Mouse" Problem:** Large corporations often lobby to extend laws strictly to protect corporate profits, not the families of individual artists.
- **Orphan Works:** Millions of old books, photos, and films are lost to history because no one knows who owns the copyright, making it illegal to digitize or preserve them.
- **Stifling Creativity:** Future artists cannot easily build upon, remix, or adapt older stories (like Shakespeare or fairy tales) without facing expensive lawsuits.

The Economic Reality

Most copyright experts and economists argue that **"Life plus 70 years" is too long**. Studies show that the vast majority of books and music stop making money within 10 to 20 years of publication. Therefore, ultra-long copyrights mostly benefit a tiny fraction of mega-hits, while locking away the rest of human culture from the public domain.

the history of copyright

The history of copyright <mark style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);">began as a tool for <strong>state censorship and printing monopolies</strong> before evolving into a modern legal framework for protecting individual creators</mark>. \[[1](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mhBpI13dxkI\&t=25), [2](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H%5FaOHpn%5FvqQ\&t=12)]

Pre-Printing Press Era

- **Ancient World:** No formal concept of copyright existed.
- **Early Disputes:** In 6th-century Ireland, a dispute over an unauthorized copy of a psalter led to a king ruling, "to every cow belongs her calf, to every book its copy". \[[1](https://www.dw.com/en/explainer-the-history-of-copyright/video-49432984), [2](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4b%5F5MO04qGg\&t=30)]

The Printing Press and State Control (15th–17th Century) \[[1](https://www.elgaronline.com/monochap/9781839101267.00013.pdf)]

- **Technology Shift:** Johannes Gutenberg’s movable-type press revolutionized book reproduction, eliminating practical barriers to copying.
- **Monopolies Granted:** European rulers began granting temporary "printing privileges" to specific printers.
- **Private Censorship:** In 1557, Queen Mary I granted a royal charter to the London Company of Stationers. This guild held a total monopoly over printing and acted as an information police force to suppress seditious materials. \[[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History%5Fof%5Fcopyright), [2](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LAkd2f2y9ls\&t=9), [3](https://alacc.org.au/the-history-of-copyright/), [4](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H%5FaOHpn%5FvqQ\&t=12), [5](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mhBpI13dxkI\&t=25)]

The Birth of Modern Copyright (18th Century)

- **First Copyright Law:** When the Stationers' monopoly expired, Parliament passed the [Statute of Anne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statute%5Fof%5FAnne) in 1710.

- **Shift to Authors:** For the first time, legal rights were vested in the author rather than the printer.

- **Term Limits:** It granted a 14-year protection term, renewable for another 14 years, after which the work entered the public domain.

- **U.S. Foundations:** In 1787, the framers embedded copyright into the U.S. Constitution (Art. I, Sec. 8) to "promote the progress of science and useful arts". This led to the Copyright Act of 1790, signed by George Washington. \[[1](https://www.copyright.gov/timeline/), [2](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mhBpI13dxkI\&t=25), [3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copyright), [4](https://socialsci.libretexts.org/Courses/Arkansas%5FTech%5FUniversity/EDMD%5F6163%5FInternet%5FResources/07%3A%5FModule%5F07-%5FCopyright%5Fand%5FOrganizing%5FDigital%5FInformation/7.01%3A%5FUnderstanding%5FCopyright%5F-%5FKnowing%5FYour%5FRights%5Fand%5FKnowing%5FWhen%5FYoure%5FRight/7.1.02%3A%5FA%5FBrief%5FHistory%5Fof%5FCopyright), [5](https://revolutionsincommunication.com/law/copyright/copyright-history/), [6](https://opentext.uoregon.edu/payforplay/chapter/chapter-23-copyright-theory-and-history/), [7](https://www.arl.org/copyright-timeline/), [8](https://www.mountvernon.org/education/primary-source-collections/primary-source-collections/article/copyright-act-of-1790)]

International Standardization (19th–20th Century)

is it good to have copyright after the author's life

Whether copyright should extend past an author's life is a highly debated topic among legal scholars, economists, and creators. <mark style="margin: 0px; text-decoration-style: solid; text-decoration-color: rgb(230, 232, 240); border-bottom: 0px none rgb(230, 232, 240);">There is no simple answer, as the policy must balance the financial interests of a creator's family against the public's right to access and build upon cultural works.</mark>

Here is a breakdown of the primary arguments for and against post-mortem copyright.

Arguments For Post-Mortem Copyright (Pro-Extension)

- **Family Security:** It ensures an author's children and heirs can financially benefit from the creator's hard work and success.
- **Investment Incentive:** Publishers and record labels are more likely to invest heavily in marketing a work if they know they can control the exclusive rights long-term.
- **Legacy Control:** It allows a creator's estate to protect the integrity of the work and prevent it from being used in offensive, highly commercialized, or low-quality ways immediately after their death.

Arguments Against Post-Mortem Copyright (Pro-Public Domain)

- **Stifles Creativity:** New creators cannot easily adapt, remix, or build upon older cultural touchstones without navigating costly licensing fees and aggressive estate lawyers.
- **Orphan Works:** Many older books, films, and songs become legally "orphaned"—they are still under copyright, but no one can find the current rights holder, leaving the art to rot in obscurity.
- **Diminishing Returns:** Critics argue that an author cannot be incentivized to create new work by the promise of money paid _after_ they die.
- **Corporate Monopolies:** Critics point out that modern extensions (like the "Mickey Mouse Protection Act") primarily benefit massive entertainment conglomerates rather than individual human families.

The modern copyright system faces heavy criticism from economists, legal scholars, technologists, and consumer advocates. - **The "Public Domain" Delay:** Cultural remixing takes place in the present, not the deep past. By the time a famous work enters the public domain, it is often generations old and less relevant to active societal dialogue. \[[1](https://wlr.law.wisc.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/1263/2011/07/07-DDesai-Final.pdf), [2](https://repository.law.umich.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=2582\&context=mjlr)]

They are forced to sign them away early in their careers to major record labels, movie studios, or academic publishers who pocket the majority of long-term royalties. \[[1](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract%5Fid=2137795), [2](https://digitalcommons.law.uga.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?referer=\&httpsredir=1\&article=1102\&context=jipl), [3](https://wlr.law.wisc.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/1263/2011/07/07-DDesai-Final.pdf), [4](https://www.quora.com/Why-should-copyright-last-longer-than-the-life-of-the-author-creator)]

- **Censorship Tool:** Public figures and corporations frequently misuse copyright laws (specifically DMCA takedown notices) to force down unfavorable reviews, parodies, political commentary, or journalistic leaks.
