# are all ppl outside male or female non binary

<mark><strong><span>No, not everyone who exists outside the categories of male or female identifies specifically as non-binary.</span></strong></mark>

While **non-binary** is widely used as a broad umbrella term, the ways people experience, describe, and name their identities outside of the traditional male/female binary vary significantly. [link](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-binary)

### The Non-Binary Umbrella

For many, [non-binary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-binary) is an active identity meaning they do not exclusively identify as a man or a woman. Within this umbrella, there are numerous specific identities, including: [link](https://www.minus18.org.au/articles/i-just-came-out-as-non-binary-here's-what-that-means/)

- **Agender**: Experiencing a complete lack of gender or having a neutral gender identity.
- **Genderfluid**: Having a gender identity that shifts and changes over time rather than remaining fixed.
- **Bigender / Pangender**: Identifying with two or more genders at once, or experiencing all genders. [link](https://www.minus18.org.au/articles/i-just-came-out-as-non-binary-here's-what-that-means/)

### Identities Distinct From Non-Binary

Many individuals whose identities fall outside of "male" or "female" do not use the term non-binary to describe themselves: [link](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-binary)

- **Indigenous and Cultural Identities**: Many cultures across the globe have historically recognized genders beyond male and female. For example, many Indigenous North American individuals use the term [Two-Spirit](https://glaad.org/reference/nonbinary/) to describe a specific spiritual, cultural, and gender role that is distinct from Western concepts like "non-binary". Similarly, communities like the _Hijra_ in India or the _Muxe_ in Mexico have unique cultural definitions.
- **Gender Non-Conforming / Genderqueer**: Some people prefer historical or politically distinct terms like "genderqueer" or simply describe themselves as "gender non-conforming," focusing on their expression rather than adopting a non-binary label.
- **No Label**: Some individuals simply reject gender labels altogether, preferring to just exist as individuals without adopting a specific category or community word. [link](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-binary)

### Gender Identity vs. Biological Sex

It is also helpful to distinguish between [gender identity and biological sex](https://www.hrc.org/resources/transgender-and-non-binary-faq), as people often confuse the two: [link](https://www.minus18.org.au/articles/i-just-came-out-as-non-binary-here's-what-that-means/)

- **Intersex**: This refers to biological sex characteristics (such as chromosomes, hormones, or anatomy) that do not fit the typical definitions of male or female bodies. Being intersex is biological. Most intersex people actually identify as men or women, though some may identify as non-binary.

- **Non-binary**: This refers to an internal, psychological sense of gender identity, independent of anatomy or birth sex. [link](https://transequality.org/issues/resources/understanding-nonbinary-people-how-to-be-respectful-and-supportive)

## how can one be neither male or female or non binary in logics

In classical logic, a statement is either true or false—there is no middle ground. However, modern logic and mathematics have created systems specifically designed to handle situations that fall outside of simple binary categories.

Here is how a system can be neither option A (male), option B (female), nor option C (non-binary) using formal logic:

### 1. Rejecting the Presupposition (Free Logic)

In classical logic, you must pick a value from a defined set. **Free Logic** drops this requirement. It allows for terms that do not refer to anything in the existing system.

- **The Logic:** If a system presumes that _everyone_ must fit into Category A, B, or C, Free Logic allows an individual to exist completely outside of that predefined universe of discourse. It proves the categories themselves are incomplete.

### 2. Many-Valued Logics (Łukasiewicz Logic)

Classical logic relies on the Principle of the Excluded Middle (everything is either X or not-X). Many-valued logics introduce more truth values.

- **Three-Valued Logic:** Adds a third value like "unknown," "irrelevant," or "neither" (I).
- **Four-Valued Logic:** Belnap’s four-valued logic allows for four states: True (T), False (F), Both (B), and **None (N)**. An identity completely outside the three options would map perfectly to the value N (None/Neither).

### 3. Boundary Paradoxes (Supervaluationism)

Supervaluationism is used in logic to handle vague predicates or terms that lack sharp boundaries.

- If the definitions of "male," "female," and "non-binary" are treated as rigid boxes, an individual's identity might fall into a "penumbra"—a logical gray space where none of those three specific predicates can be truly affirmed or denied.

### 4. Null and Empty Sets (Set Theory)

## i mean how could one be neither a or b, nor "not a or b"

To be neither A or B, nor "not A or B," you are asking how a system can violate the fundamental law of classical logic: that <mark>something must either be a statement or its exact opposite</mark>.

In classical logic, this is impossible because "not (A or B)" covers _everything_ else in the universe. However, advanced logical systems solve this puzzle by breaking the rules of classical logic in three specific ways:

### 1. The Intuitionistic Logic Approach (Rejecting Double Negation)

In Intuitionistic (or Constructive) logic, a statement is only true if you can actively prove or construct it.

- **The Logic:** In this system, saying "It is not the case that something is not true" (\
  ¬\
  ¬\
  𝑃\
  ) is **not** the same as proving it _is_ true (P).
- **The Solution:** If you cannot prove someone is A or B, but you also cannot prove a structural contradiction if they aren't, you land in a logical space that refuses to assert either side. You reject the requirement to choose.

### 2. Multi-Dimensional Logic (Orthogonality)

This happens when you treat the categories as separate dimensions rather than opposite sides of a single coin.

- **The Logic:** Think of a 2D grid. The X-axis is "Scale of A and B." The Y-axis is "Relevance of the System."
- **The Solution:** If a person rates a 0 on the X-axis, they are "not A or B." But if they exist on a completely different Z-axis (a third dimension), they transcend the entire X-axis system. They are neither the categories (A/B) nor the simple absence of those categories within that specific framework.

### 3. Presupposition Failure (The "King of France" Paradox)

Logician Bertrand Russell famously analyzed the sentence: _"The present King of France is bald."_

- Is it true? No, France has no king.
- Is it false (meaning the King of France is not bald)? No, because that still implies a king exists.
- **The Solution:** The statement is completely meaningless because it relies on a false premise (that France has a king). Similarly, if the premise "this system applies to this person" is false, then asserting they are A/B or "not A/B" are both logically invalid statements.

### Summary

To escape this trap, you must move from a flat, two-sided logical system to a system that measures **proof** (Intuitionism), adds **dimensions** (Orthogonality), or checks if the question makes sense to begin with (**Presupposition**).

Imagine you have a box of Lego bricks.

In classical logic, there are strict rules: a brick can be **Red (A)**, **Blue (B)**, or **Not Red or Blue (Everything else)**. In that simple system, every single brick _must_ fit one of those options.

To break that rule and be neither of them, you have to change the game entirely. Here is how you do it:

### 1. The Game is Wrong (Presupposition Failure)

Imagine someone asks you, _"Is the smartphone in your hand Red, Blue, or Not Red or Blue?"_\
But wait—you aren't holding a smartphone. You are holding an **apple**.

- The question itself doesn't work.
- Because you are an apple, you aren't a "Red smartphone," a "Blue smartphone," or a "Not-Red-or-Blue smartphone." The whole category of "smartphone" does not apply to you.

### 2. A Whole New Dimension (Orthogonality)

Imagine a flat piece of paper. On the left side is **Red**, and on the right side is **Blue**. Anything else on that flat paper is **Not Red or Blue**.

- Now, imagine you pick up a toy airplane and fly it **high up into the air**, off the paper entirely.
- The airplane is not on the left, it's not on the right, and it's not even on the paper's "everything else" space. It is flying in a completely new direction (up and down) that the flat paper didn't even know existed.

### 3. It's Secretly a Different Color (Vagueness)

Imagine a brick that is a microscopic mix of neon green, clear glass, and mirrors.

- If you look at it, you can't truly say it is Red or Blue.
- But if you try to put it in the "Not Red or Blue" bucket, it feels wrong because that bucket is meant for normal things like yellow or green bricks. This brick is so strange and unique that trying to label it with _any_ of those words ruins what it actually is.

### In Short

To be neither of those things, you aren't just picking a different option on the menu—**you are leaving the restaurant.**
