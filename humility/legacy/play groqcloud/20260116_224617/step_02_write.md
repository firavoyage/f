### Introduction

In the previous sections, we have explored the fundamental building blocks of matter: atoms. We have discussed the properties of atoms, such as atomic number and mass, and how they are related to the elements. In this section, we will delve deeper into the structure of atoms, specifically the arrangement of electrons within an atom. This will lead us to the periodic table, a powerful tool for understanding the relationships between elements.

### Electron Configuration

Recall that electrons are negatively charged particles that orbit the nucleus of an atom. The number of electrons in an atom is equal to its atomic number (Z). The electron configuration of an atom is a way of describing the arrangement of electrons in the atom. It is typically written in a shorthand notation, using the following rules:

* Each energy level (or shell) is represented by a number (1, 2, 3, etc.).
* Each orbital within an energy level is represented by a letter (s, p, d, f).
* The number of electrons in each orbital is represented by a superscript number.

For example, the electron configuration of carbon (Z = 6) is:

1s² 2s² 2p²

This notation indicates that the first energy level (n = 1) has two electrons in the s orbital (1s²), the second energy level (n = 2) has two electrons in the s orbital (2s²) and two electrons in the p orbitals (2p²).

### Periodic Trends

The periodic table is a tabular arrangement of elements, organized by their atomic number (Z). The elements are listed in order of increasing atomic number, and the elements in each row (period) have similar chemical properties. The periodic table is a powerful tool for understanding the relationships between elements, and it can be used to predict the properties of elements based on their position in the table.

The periodic table is arranged in a way that elements with similar electron configurations are grouped together. The elements in each group (column) have similar chemical properties, such as the number of valence electrons. The elements in each period have similar chemical properties, such as the number of energy levels.

### Periodic Table Trends

The periodic table exhibits several trends, including:

* **Atomic radius**: The atomic radius decreases as you move from left to right across a period, and increases as you move down a group.
* **Electronegativity**: The electronegativity of an element increases as you move from left to right across a period, and decreases as you move down a group.
* **Valence electrons**: The number of valence electrons in an element increases as you move down a group, and decreases as you move across a period.

### Problem Set

1. Write the electron configuration for the following elements:
	* Oxygen (Z = 8)
	* Nitrogen (Z = 7)
	* Sodium (Z = 11)
2. Identify the group and period of the following elements:
	* Carbon (C)
	* Oxygen (O)
	* Nitrogen (N)
3. Predict the properties of the following elements based on their position in the periodic table:
	* Fluorine (F)
	* Chlorine (Cl)
	* Argon (Ar)

### Solutions

1. Oxygen (Z = 8): 1s² 2s² 2p⁴
Nitrogen (Z = 7): 1s² 2s² 2p³
Sodium (Z = 11): 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s¹
2. Carbon (C) is in group 14 and period 2.
Oxygen (O) is in group 16 and period 2.
Nitrogen (N) is in group 15 and period 2.
3. Fluorine (F) is a highly reactive gas, with a high electronegativity and a small atomic radius.
Chlorine (Cl) is a highly reactive gas, with a high electronegativity and a small atomic radius.
Argon (Ar) is a noble gas, with a low reactivity and a large atomic radius.

### Conceptual Questions

1. What is the relationship between the electron configuration of an element and its position in the periodic table?
2. How do the properties of an element change as you move across a period or down a group in the periodic table?
3. What is the significance of the noble gases in the periodic table?

### Solutions

1. The electron configuration of an element is related to its position in the periodic table by the fact that elements with similar electron configurations are grouped together in the periodic table.
2. The properties of an element change as you move across a period or down a group in the periodic table due to changes in the electron configuration and the number of energy levels.
3. The noble gases are a group of elements that are highly unreactive and have a full outer energy level, which makes them useful for a variety of applications.
