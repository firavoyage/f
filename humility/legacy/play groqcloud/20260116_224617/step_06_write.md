# Step 6 — Written Section

**Chemical Reactions: Stoichiometry and Balancing Equations**
===========================================================

**Introduction**
---------------

Chemical reactions are the foundation of chemistry, and understanding them is crucial for predicting the outcomes of experiments and designing new processes. In this chapter, we will delve into the world of stoichiometry and balancing equations, two essential tools for analyzing and describing chemical reactions.

**What is Stoichiometry?**
-------------------------

Stoichiometry is the study of the quantitative relationships between reactants and products in chemical reactions. It involves calculating the amounts of substances required or produced in a reaction, and is essential for understanding the efficiency and feasibility of chemical processes.

**Moles and Molar Ratios**
-------------------------

To perform stoichiometric calculations, we need to understand the concept of moles and molar ratios. A mole is a unit of measurement that represents 6.022 x 10^23 particles (atoms or molecules). Molar ratios are the ratios of the number of moles of different substances in a reaction.

**Balancing Equations**
----------------------

A balanced chemical equation is one in which the number of atoms of each element is the same on both the reactant and product sides. Balancing equations involves adding coefficients to the reactants and products to ensure that the number of atoms of each element is equal on both sides.

**The Law of Conservation of Mass**
---------------------------------

The law of conservation of mass states that matter cannot be created or destroyed in a chemical reaction. This means that the total mass of the reactants must equal the total mass of the products.

**Balancing Equations: A Step-by-Step Guide**
--------------------------------------------

1. **Write the unbalanced equation**: Start by writing the unbalanced equation, with the reactants on the left and the products on the right.
2. **Count the atoms**: Count the number of atoms of each element on both the reactant and product sides.
3. **Identify the imbalances**: Identify the elements that have an unequal number of atoms on both sides.
4. **Add coefficients**: Add coefficients to the reactants and products to balance the equation.
5. **Check the equation**: Check the equation to ensure that the number of atoms of each element is equal on both sides.

**Example: Balancing the Equation for Photosynthesis**
---------------------------------------------------

2H2O + CO2 → C6H12O6 + O2

1. Write the unbalanced equation: 2H2O + CO2 → C6H12O6 + O2
2. Count the atoms: H: 4 (reactants), 12 (products); C: 1 (reactants), 1 (products); O: 3 (reactants), 6 (products)
3. Identify the imbalances: H, O
4. Add coefficients: 6H2O + 6CO2 → C6H12O6 + 6O2
5. Check the equation: H: 12 (reactants), 12 (products); C: 6 (reactants), 6 (products); O: 18 (reactants), 18 (products)

**Practice Problems**
-------------------

1. Balance the equation for the combustion of methane: CH4 + O2 → CO2 + H2O
2. Calculate the number of moles of oxygen required to react with 2 moles of nitrogen to form nitrogen dioxide: N2 + O2 → NO2
3. Balance the equation for the reaction between sodium and chlorine to form sodium chloride: Na + Cl2 → NaCl

**Challenge Problem**
--------------------

Balance the equation for the reaction between hydrogen and oxygen to form water and oxygen: H2 + O2 → H2O + O

**Answer Key**
---------------
