**Section 14: Analytical Chemistry**

### 14.1 Introduction to Analytical Chemistry

Analytical chemistry is the branch of chemistry that deals with the analysis and identification of chemical substances. It is a fundamental concept in chemistry that helps us understand the composition and properties of matter. Analytical chemistry plays a crucial role in various fields, including medicine, environmental science, and forensic science, where accurate identification and quantification of substances are essential.

#### Key Concepts

* **Analysis**: the process of breaking down a substance into its component parts
* **Identification**: the process of determining the identity of a substance
* **Quantification**: the process of determining the amount of a substance in a sample

### 14.2 Types of Analytical Techniques

There are several types of analytical techniques, each with its own strengths and weaknesses. Understanding these techniques is essential for selecting the appropriate method for a particular analysis.

#### 14.2.1 Spectroscopy

Spectroscopy is the study of the interaction between matter and electromagnetic radiation. There are several types of spectroscopy, including:

* **UV-Vis spectroscopy**: the study of the interaction between molecules and ultraviolet and visible light
* **IR spectroscopy**: the study of the interaction between molecules and infrared radiation
* **NMR spectroscopy**: the study of the interaction between nuclei and magnetic fields

#### 14.2.2 Chromatography

Chromatography is the separation of mixtures based on the interaction between the components and a stationary phase. There are several types of chromatography, including:

* **Gas chromatography**: the separation of mixtures based on the interaction between the components and a gas stationary phase
* **Liquid chromatography**: the separation of mixtures based on the interaction between the components and a liquid stationary phase

#### 14.2.3 Electrochemistry

Electrochemistry is the study of the relationship between chemical reactions and electricity. It involves the use of electrodes to measure the concentration of ions in a solution.

#### 14.2.4 Mass Spectrometry

Mass spectrometry is the study of the mass-to-charge ratio of ions. It involves the use of a mass spectrometer to measure the mass of ions in a sample.

### 14.3 Quantitative Analysis

Quantitative analysis is the process of determining the amount of a substance in a sample. It involves the use of analytical techniques, such as spectroscopy and chromatography, to measure the concentration of a substance.

#### Key Concepts

* **Concentration**: the amount of a substance in a sample, expressed as a ratio of the amount of substance to the amount of solvent
* **Calibration curve**: a graph that shows the relationship between the concentration of a substance and the response of the analytical instrument

### 14.4 Qualitative Analysis

Qualitative analysis is the process of identifying the presence of a substance in a sample. It involves the use of analytical techniques, such as spectroscopy and chromatography, to determine the identity of a substance.

#### Key Concepts

* **Identification**: the process of determining the identity of a substance
* **Confirmation**: the process of verifying the identity of a substance using multiple analytical techniques

### 14.5 Calibration and Standardization

Calibration and standardization are essential steps in analytical chemistry. Calibration involves the process of adjusting the analytical instrument to ensure that it is functioning correctly, while standardization involves the process of establishing a reference point for the analysis.

#### Key Concepts

* **Calibration**: the process of adjusting the analytical instrument to ensure that it is functioning correctly
* **Standardization**: the process of establishing a reference point for the analysis

### 14.6 Error Analysis

Error analysis is the process of evaluating the accuracy and precision of analytical results. It involves the use of statistical methods to determine the uncertainty of the results.

#### Key Concepts

* **Accuracy**: the closeness of the analytical result to the true value
* **Precision**: the reproducibility of the analytical result
* **Uncertainty**: the range of values within which the true value is likely to lie

This section has introduced the fundamental concepts of analytical chemistry, including the types of analytical techniques, quantitative analysis, qualitative analysis, calibration and standardization, and error analysis.
